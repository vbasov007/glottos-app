01

How Glottos actually works

Most language apps have their own content, their own pace, their own idea of what you should be learning right now. Glottos doesn't do that. You pick the material — or generate something on the spot — and work through it in four stages.

Phase 1Get your text

Paste anything in your target language, or use the built-in AI to generate a text from scratch. You set the level, the topic, and any specific angle — say, a B1 conversation about renting a flat that works in some conditional tense practice.

If you're not sure where to start, a few things that tend to work well:

News, simplified. Take something from today's headlines and ask the AI to rewrite it at your level. You get real content without drowning in vocabulary.
YouTube transcripts. Find a video in your target language, grab the transcript, paste it in. Unscripted speech especially — it's full of the phrases textbooks skip.
Generated dialogues. Job interviews, awkward dinner conversations, arguments about whose turn it is to do the dishes — whatever situations you're likely to actually find yourself in.
Phase 2Read carefully — and do a first pass through the flashcards

Not a skim. You're going for complete understanding — not just what the text says, but how it says it. Why this tense and not another. What makes that phrase sound natural instead of textbook-ish.

Click any word or phrase and you get a full breakdown: translation, grammar, all the forms the word can take, and voiced example sentences. Everything you look up goes automatically into a flashcard deck — in dictionary form, translation included.

Once you've finished reading, flip through the deck a few times before moving on. The goal here isn't deep memorisation — it's stopping yourself from looking up the same word three times. Two or three passes is usually enough.

Where you want to be by the end: every sentence understood, new words familiar enough that they won't trip you up when you hear them.
Phase 3Audio training

Now you listen. Glottos plays the text sentence by sentence — you repeat each one out loud during the pause, then it plays again so you can check. You can loop individual sentences, replay single words or phrases, and adjust the pause length and number of repetitions right from the player.

A good starting point is 5-second pauses with 2–3 repetitions per sentence. If that's too fast, slow it down. The aim: you can hear the text without effort and reproduce it without stumbling. Because you understood every sentence in Phase 2, your brain at this point isn't translating — it's getting the patterns into muscle memory.

There's also a microphone button next to each sentence. Tap it, record yourself, play it back. Simple — but hearing your own pronunciation tends to clarify very quickly what still needs work.

Finish by listening to the full text straight through, no pauses. If it flows easily, you're done. That feeling — when something effortful an hour ago now feels natural — is what progress actually feels like.

Offline: The download button saves the audio with your pause and repetition settings as a file. Useful for the commute, or anywhere you don't want to be staring at a screen.
Phase 4Vocabulary review

Go back through your deck one more time with the full explanations showing. Good moment to trim it — remove anything that now feels obvious, add something you realise you want to keep.

You can browse the word list and play each word before reviewing, or go straight into flashcard mode. The card shows the word — click to flip and see the translation. Controls at the bottom let you set the wait time between cards, shuffle the deck, restart, or navigate manually.

Go in both directions — foreign word first, then swap it around. Recognising a word when you see it is one thing; producing it when you need it is another.

Come back to the deck over the next few days. Words you're shaky on will keep appearing until they stick; the ones you know will quietly fade into the background.

The whole thing — picking a text, reading it properly, drilling the audio, reviewing the vocab — is usually somewhere between 20 and 45 minutes. Once a week is enough to make real progress. Three times a week and you'll start surprising yourself.

02

Pasting your first text

Open Glottos, paste your text. The language defaults to whatever you've set in your preferences, but you can change it per workspace — useful if you're juggling more than one language at once, which, good for you.

First time? Keep it short. Three to five paragraphs is enough to see how everything fits together without spending the next two hours on one session.

Hit go and Glottos opens the reading view. The wall of foreign text is now something you can actually interact with. Honestly, just getting to this point counts.

If the text looks hard, that's fine. You're not supposed to understand it yet — that's the whole next step.
03

Reading with annotations

The reading view is where most of the actual work happens. Every word is clickable. You don't need to leave the app to look things up, copy-paste into another tab, or any of that.

Click a word and you get the translation plus a full grammatical explanation — not just what it means but what it's doing in this sentence, what forms it can take, and a couple of voiced examples you can play right there. Select a phrase instead of a single word and you get the whole thing explained as a unit, which is particularly useful for expressions that don't translate word by word.

04

Clicking words & building vocab

Your workspace collects everything you've looked up — words, phrases, the sentences they came from. It's a personal dictionary made entirely of things you encountered in real texts, which sounds obvious but makes a noticeable difference when you're reviewing later.

Save freelyIf you're not sure, save it. You can always remove things later. The cost of saving something you didn't need is zero.
Context mattersThe saved sentence is the most important part. You'll remember where you learned a word long before you remember its definition.
Don't memorise yetThat's what the flashcards are for. Just read, click what you don't know, keep moving.
Breadth firstMeeting 100 words once in real sentences beats drilling 10 words in isolation until you can recite them backwards.
05

Flashcard review

The flashcard tab is where "I've seen this somewhere" turns into actually knowing a word. Each card has the word, its grammatical form, and the sentence it came from. Rate how well you knew it — honest ratings work better than optimistic ones. Words you're shaky on come back sooner; the ones you know give you a break.

Ten minutes a day is genuinely enough. More than that is fine, but the gap between "doing some reviewing" and "doing no reviewing" matters a lot more than how long each session is.
06

Choosing good texts

Pick something you'd actually read in your own language. That said — before you think about topic or difficulty, think about length.

Working through a text properly takes about 2 to 3 minutes per sentence. Not because the app is slow, but because that's how long it takes to actually understand something, drill the audio, and not just skim past it. If you have 30 minutes, 10 sentences is plenty. An hour gives you maybe 20. Trying to do more means rushing the parts that matter.

Difficulty-wise:

LevelGood sourcesUnknown words
BeginnerChildren's news, simple blog posts, Wikipedia intros~30%
IntermediateNews articles, short stories, subtitles from shows~15–20%
AdvancedEssays, novels, opinion pieces, native forums5–10%
If you understand everything, the text is too easy. If you understand nothing, it's too hard — and also pretty demoralising. You want the middle: you can follow what's happening, but there's enough new material to actually work with.
When in doubt, use the AI generator and keep the sentence count low. Ten sentences done properly will do more than thirty done in a rush.
07

Things worth knowing before you start

A few things that tend to trip people up early on:

Don't overthinkIf a word appears once and feels irrelevant to your life, skip it. Save the ones that feel like they'll come back.
Re-read textsWork through something, review for a few days, then read it again. The jump in comprehension is one of those things you have to experience to believe.
Use real contentA recipe, a Reddit thread in your target language, subtitles from a show — anything that doesn't feel like homework tends to stick better.
Do the audioReading and listening are genuinely different skills. A lot of people are surprised by how much harder the audio is, even on the same text.
Keep it smallOne text a week is a real, sustainable habit. Don't design a routine that only works if everything goes perfectly.

That's really all there is to it.

Find a text. Read it properly. Do the audio. Review the words. Come back next week. Languages get learned in small sessions over a long time, not in marathons.
