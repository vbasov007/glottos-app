# Lesson 10: -RE verbs. Faire, prendre, venir. Venir de + infinitive
**Vocabulary: Everyday scenes — faire les courses, prendre le métro, venir de**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Run the scale** — every verb through je → tu → il → nous → vous → ils
3. **Matrix** — drill question-and-answer until it flies out on its own

> This lesson closes the present tense. After it, you can conjugate any verb in all three groups, plus the heavyweight irregulars. This is the spine — without it, nothing else stands up.

---

## Part 1: The third group — verbs in -RE

After -ER (group 1, Lesson 7) and -IR (group 2, Lesson 9), the third group. The smallest and most chaotic, but it does have a "regular core": verbs like **<say>vendre</say>**, **<say>attendre</say>**, **<say>répondre</say>**.

**The main rule:** drop `-re`, add the endings. In the third person singular — **add nothing at all** (no -t, no -e, just the bare stem!).

### Vendre (to sell) — the model

| Person | Form | Ending | How it sounds |
|--------|------|--------|---------------|
| je | **<say>vends</say>** | -s | "vahn" (s, d silent) |
| tu | **<say>vends</say>** | -s | "vahn" |
| il / elle | **<say>vend</say>** | — (nothing!) | "vahn" |
| nous | **<say>vendons</say>** | -ons | "vahn-DOHN" |
| vous | **<say>vendez</say>** | -ez | "vahn-DAY" |
| ils / elles | **<say>vendent</say>** | -ent | "VAHND" (the -ent is silent, but the **d is pronounced**) |

> **Trap!** In *<say>il vend</say>* there is **no -t** on the end. This is unique to the -RE group. -ER verbs end in `-e` (*<say>il parle</say>*), -IR verbs end in `-it` (*<say>il finit</say>*), -RE verbs end in **nothing**: *<say>il vend</say>*, *<say>il attend</say>*, *<say>il répond</say>*. The stem ends in a consonant, and that consonant stays silent at word end.

> **Ear trap:** *<say>je vends</say>* and *<say>ils vendent</say>* sound different! Singular: "vahn" (d silent). Plural: "VAHND" (d pronounced because a vowel — the silent e of -ent — used to follow it). That difference is the only audible singular/plural marker in this group.

### Verbs that conjugate the same way

| Verb | Meaning | Note |
|------|---------|------|
| **<say>vendre</say>** | to sell | |
| **<say>attendre</say>** | to wait (for) | direct object, no preposition: *<say>j'attends le bus</say>* — "I'm waiting **for** the bus" (no *for* in French!) |
| **<say>répondre (à)</say>** | to answer | needs **<say>à</say>**: *<say>je réponds à la question</say>* |
| **<say>entendre</say>** | to hear | not to be confused with *<say>écouter</say>* (to listen) |
| **<say>descendre</say>** | to go down; to get off (a vehicle) | takes *<say>être</say>* in the past! |
| **<say>rendre</say>** | to give back, to return (something) | *<say>rendre un livre</say>* — to return a book |
| **<say>perdre</say>** | to lose | |

All of them conjugate exactly like *<say>vendre</say>*. Run *<say>attendre</say>* through: *<say>j'attends, tu attends, il attend, nous attendons, vous attendez, ils attendent</say>*.

## Part 2: Faire — "to do/make", but much more

**<say>Faire</say>** is the third-most-frequent French verb after *<say>être</say>* and *<say>avoir</say>*. Completely irregular — learn it whole, no shortcuts.

| Person | Form | How it sounds |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | **<say>fais</say>** | "feh" |
| tu | **<say>fais</say>** | "feh" |
| il / elle | **<say>fait</say>** | "feh" |
| nous | **<say>faisons</say>** | "fuh-ZOHN" (warning: ai = "uh"!) |
| vous | **<say>faites</say>** | "fet" |
| ils / elles | **<say>font</say>** | "fohn" |

> **Three traps in faire:**
> 1. *<say>nous faisons</say>* reads "**fuh**-zohn", not "feh-zohn". This is the **only** place in French where `ai` is pronounced "uh" (schwa). Strange, but universal.
> 2. *<say>vous faites</say>* ends in **-tes**, not -ez. Only three verbs in the entire language do this: *<say>vous êtes</say> (être), <say>vous faites</say> (faire), <say>vous dites</say> (dire)*. Three. Total.
> 3. *<say>ils font</say>* is short — same pattern as *<say>ils sont</say>* (être), *<say>ils ont</say>* (avoir), *<say>ils vont</say>* (aller). Memorize the foursome: **<say>font, sont, ont, vont</say>**.

### What faire actually means

Base meaning is "to do" or "to make", but in idioms it covers "to play (a sport)", "to engage in", "to perform". English uses three or four different verbs where French just uses *faire*.

| Expression | Meaning |
|------------|---------|
| <say>faire les courses</say> | to do the (grocery) shopping |
| <say>faire le ménage</say> | to do the housework |
| <say>faire la cuisine</say> | to cook |
| <say>faire la vaisselle</say> | to do the dishes |
| <say>faire ses devoirs</say> | to do one's homework |
| <say>faire du sport</say> | to do sports / to exercise |
| <say>faire du vélo</say> | to go cycling |
| <say>faire la queue</say> | to stand in line / queue |
| <say>faire un voyage</say> | to take a trip |
| <say>faire attention</say> | to pay attention / be careful |
| <say>Il fait beau / froid / chaud</say> | The weather is nice / it's cold / it's hot |

> **English-speaker trap:** the weather idioms use **<say>faire</say>** + adjective, not **<say>être</say>**. Not *<say>il est froid</say>* (that would mean "he is cold-natured"), but *<say>il fait froid</say>* — literally "it makes cold". *<say>Il fait beau</say>* = "the weather's nice". Lock this in early.

## Part 3: Prendre — "to take", "to have (food)", "to take (transport)"

**<say>Prendre</say>** is the other big champion. Learn it once and you get **<say>comprendre</say>** (to understand) and **<say>apprendre</say>** (to learn) for free — they conjugate identically.

| Person | Form | How it sounds |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | **<say>prends</say>** | "prahn" |
| tu | **<say>prends</say>** | "prahn" |
| il / elle | **<say>prend</say>** | "prahn" |
| nous | **<say>prenons</say>** | "pruh-NOHN" (one n!) |
| vous | **<say>prenez</say>** | "pruh-NAY" (one n!) |
| ils / elles | **<say>prennent</say>** | "PREN" (double n, nasal disappears!) |

> **The big trap:** in the plural, **the stem changes**. In *<say>nous</say>/<say>vous</say>*, you get a single `n` and the sound "pruh-". In *<say>ils prennent</say>*, you get a double `nn`, and that double n **kills the nasal vowel**: the *en* opens up to a flat "eh" sound, like English *pen*. So *<say>ils prennent</say>* sounds like "PREN", nothing nasal about it.

### What prendre means

| Expression | Meaning |
|------------|---------|
| <say>prendre le métro / le bus / le train</say> | to take the metro / bus / train |
| <say>prendre un taxi</say> | to take a taxi |
| <say>prendre le petit-déjeuner</say> | to have breakfast |
| <say>prendre un café</say> | to have a coffee |
| <say>prendre une douche</say> | to take a shower |
| <say>prendre une photo</say> | to take a photo |
| <say>prendre rendez-vous</say> | to make an appointment |

> French speakers don't "go by metro" — they **take** the metro. They don't "have breakfast" — they **take** breakfast. The logic: "I take = I select and use." Once you spot the pattern, dozens of idioms become predictable.

### Bonus: comprendre, apprendre — same pattern

| | comprendre (to understand) | apprendre (to learn) |
|---|------------|-----------|
| je | <say>comprends</say> | <say>apprends</say> |
| tu | <say>comprends</say> | <say>apprends</say> |
| il | <say>comprend</say> | <say>apprend</say> |
| nous | <say>comprenons</say> | <say>apprenons</say> |
| vous | <say>comprenez</say> | <say>apprenez</say> |
| ils | <say>comprennent</say> | <say>apprennent</say> |

## Part 4: Venir — "to come"

| Person | Form | How it sounds |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | **<say>viens</say>** | "vyahn" (nasal) |
| tu | **<say>viens</say>** | "vyahn" |
| il / elle | **<say>vient</say>** | "vyahn" |
| nous | **<say>venons</say>** | "vuh-NOHN" |
| vous | **<say>venez</say>** | "vuh-NAY" |
| ils / elles | **<say>viennent</say>** | "VYEN" (double n, nasal gone) |

Same logic as *<say>prendre</say>*: in *<say>nous</say>/<say>vous</say>*, the stem is "ven-" (single n, plain vowel). In every other person, the stem is "vien-". And in *<say>ils viennent</say>*, the double `nn` kills the nasal — you get the open "VYEN" sound, like English *yen*.

Same conjugation: **<say>tenir</say>** (to hold), **<say>devenir</say>** (to become), **<say>revenir</say>** (to come back), **<say>se souvenir (de)</say>** (to remember).

### Venir de + country / city — "to come from"

Where are you from? Use *<say>venir de</say>*. The preposition is the mirror of what you'd use with *<say>aller</say>* (Lesson 9):

| Country | venir de … | aller … |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| <say>la France</say> (fem.) | <say>de France</say> | <say>en France</say> |
| <say>le Canada</say> (masc.) | <say>du Canada</say> | <say>au Canada</say> |
| <say>les États-Unis</say> (plural) | <say>des États-Unis</say> | <say>aux États-Unis</say> |
| <say>Paris</say> (a city) | <say>de Paris</say> | <say>à Paris</say> |

- *<say>Je viens de France.</say>* — I'm from France.
- *<say>Tu viens d'où ?</say>* — Where are you from?
- *<say>Elle vient du Japon.</say>* — She's from Japan.
- *<say>Nous venons des États-Unis.</say>* — We're from the United States.

## Part 5: Venir de + infinitive — "to have just done"

This isn't movement. This is **time**. The construction "*venir de* + infinitive" means "to have just done something" — an action that finished a moment ago. It's the perfect mirror of *aller + infinitive* (the futur proche from Lesson 9).

| Construction | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| **<say>aller</say> + infinitive** | about to do (futur proche) | *<say>Je vais manger.</say>* — I'm going to eat. |
| **<say>venir de</say> + infinitive** | just did (passé récent) | *<say>Je viens de manger.</say>* — I just ate. |

This is called the **<say>passé récent</say>** — the recent past. It's a clever bit of grammar: the verb *<say>venir</say>* sits in the present tense, but the meaning is past. Think of it as "I come from doing X" → "I just did X".

| Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| <say>Je viens d'arriver.</say> | I just arrived. |
| <say>Tu viens de finir ?</say> | Did you just finish? |
| <say>Il vient de partir.</say> | He just left. |
| <say>Nous venons de manger.</say> | We just ate. |
| <say>Vous venez d'appeler ?</say> | Did you just call? |
| <say>Ils viennent de rentrer.</say> | They just got home. |

> **Don't mix up the two uses of *de*!**
> *<say>Je viens de Paris.</say>* = "I'm **from** Paris" (where you come from).
> *<say>Je viens de manger.</say>* = "I **just** ate" (what you just finished).
> The difference: if a **verb in the infinitive** follows *<say>de</say>*, it's the passé récent. If a **place** follows *<say>de</say>*, it's geographical origin.

> **English speakers — note:** English does this with "just" + the past tense ("I just ate"). French does it with present-tense *<say>venir</say>* + *<say>de</say>* + infinitive. The infinitive never changes form, regardless of who did it: *<say>Je viens de manger</say>*, *<say>Nous venons de manger</say>* — only *<say>venir</say>* changes.

## Language scales

Run each verb through aloud, all six persons. Slow first, then up to speed.

### Scale 1: attendre le bus

> <say>J'attends le bus. — Tu attends le bus. — Il attend le bus. — Nous attendons le bus. — Vous attendez le bus. — Ils attendent le bus.</say>

### Scale 2: faire les courses

> <say>Je fais les courses. — Tu fais les courses. — Elle fait les courses. — Nous faisons les courses. — Vous faites les courses. — Ils font les courses.</say>

### Scale 3: prendre le métro

> <say>Je prends le métro. — Tu prends le métro. — Il prend le métro. — Nous prenons le métro. — Vous prenez le métro. — Elles prennent le métro.</say>

### Scale 4: venir de France

> <say>Je viens de France. — Tu viens de France. — Il vient de France. — Nous venons de France. — Vous venez de France. — Elles viennent de France.</say>

> <say>Je viens d'arriver. — Tu viens d'arriver. — Il vient d'arriver. — Nous venons d'arriver. — Vous venez d'arriver. — Ils viennent d'arriver.</say>

> <say>Je ne fais pas les courses. — Tu ne fais pas les courses. — Il ne fait pas les courses. — Nous ne faisons pas les courses. — Vous ne faites pas les courses. — Ils ne font pas les courses.</say>

## Language matrix

Mirror principle: the answer is already in the question. Drill yourself, then your group, then a third party.

### Matrix 1: faire / prendre / venir

| Question | You (sg.) | You (pl.) — we | They |
|----------|-----------|----------------|------|
| <say>Qu'est-ce que tu fais ?</say> (answer: the shopping) | <say>Je fais les courses.</say> | <say>Nous faisons les courses.</say> | <say>Ils font les courses.</say> |

| <say>Tu attends le bus ?</say> | <say>Oui, j'attends le bus.</say> | <say>Oui, nous attendons le bus.</say> | <say>Oui, ils attendent le bus.</say> |
| <say>Tu comprends ?</say> | <say>Oui, je comprends.</say> | <say>Oui, nous comprenons.</say> | <say>Oui, ils comprennent.</say> |

### Matrix 2: passé récent (venir de + inf.)

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Tu as faim ?</say> (answer: no, just ate) | <say>Non, je viens de manger.</say> |
| <say>Elle est là ?</say> (answer: no, just left) | <say>Non, elle vient de partir.</say> |
| <say>Vous voulez un café ?</say> (answer: no, just had one) | <say>Non merci, nous venons de prendre un café.</say> |

### Matrix 3: contrast futur proche ↔ passé récent

| Verb | aller + inf. (about to) | venir de + inf. (just did) |
|------|-------------------------|----------------------------|
| manger | <say>Je vais manger.</say> | <say>Je viens de manger.</say> |

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
THIRD GROUP -RE (vendre, attendre, répondre):
  je    vend-s        nous  vend-ons
  tu    vend-s        vous  vend-ez
  il    vend  (!)     ils   vend-ent
  → il vend — NO ending. Bare stem.
  → je vends "vahn" (d silent), ils vendent "VAHND" (d pronounced!).

FAIRE (to do/make):
  je fais       nous faisons  (reads "fuh-ZOHN"!)
  tu fais       vous faites   (-tes, like êtes, dites)
  il fait       ils font
  IDIOMS: faire les courses / le ménage / la cuisine / la vaisselle
          faire ses devoirs / du sport / du vélo / la queue
          faire un voyage / attention
          il fait beau / froid / chaud  (weather!)

PRENDRE (to take, have, take transport):
  je prends     nous prenons   (one n, "pruh-NOHN")
  tu prends     vous prenez
  il prend      ils prennent   (double nn, nasal disappears → "PREN"!)
  → same pattern: comprendre, apprendre
  IDIOMS: prendre le métro / le bus / un taxi
          prendre le petit-déjeuner / un café
          prendre une douche / une photo / rendez-vous

VENIR (to come):
  je viens      nous venons    (one n, "vuh-NOHN")
  tu viens      vous venez
  il vient      ils viennent   (double nn, nasal disappears → "VYEN"!)
  → same pattern: revenir, devenir, tenir, se souvenir

VENIR DE + COUNTRY (where from):
  de France / d'Italie / du Canada / des États-Unis / de Paris
  (mirror of aller en/au/aux — Lesson 9)

VENIR DE + INFINITIVE (just did):
  Je viens de manger. — I just ate.
  ↔ Je vais manger. — I'm about to eat. (futur proche, Lesson 9)

THE SHORT FOURSOME — 3rd person plural:
  ils SONT (être) / ils ONT (avoir) / ils FONT (faire) / ils VONT (aller)

PRESENT TENSE — SUMMARY OF THE THREE GROUPS:
  -ER (parler):  -e,  -es, -e,  -ons,    -ez,    -ent
  -IR (finir):   -is, -is, -it, -issons, -issez, -issent
  -RE (vendre):  -s,  -s,  —,   -ons,    -ez,    -ent
```
