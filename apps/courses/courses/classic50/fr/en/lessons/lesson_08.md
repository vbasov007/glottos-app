# Lesson 8: Negation and Questions
**Vocabulary: Question words — qui, que, où, quand, comment, combien, pourquoi**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — get the mechanics of two-part negation and the three question registers (7 minutes)
2. **Run the scales** — every rule through je → tu → il → nous → vous → ils
3. **Question-answer matrix** — the answer is already hidden in the question; just flip it

> French negation = **brackets** around the verb: `ne [verb] pas`.
> French question = **three registers**: intonation (street), est-ce que (neutral), inversion (bookish).

---

## Part 1: Negation — the core rule

> **English uses "do/don't" for negation. French does not.** No auxiliary, no helper verb. Instead, French wraps the verb in a two-part frame: `ne … pas`.

| English | French | Literal |
|---------|--------|---------|
| I speak. | <say>Je parle.</say> | I speak |
| I **don't** speak. | <say>Je ne parle pas.</say> | I not speak not |
| He **doesn't** like coffee. | <say>Il n'aime pas le café.</say> | He not likes not the coffee |

There's no French equivalent of "don't / doesn't". Forget about the auxiliary; just **wrap the verb**. *<say>ne</say>* goes in front, *<say>pas</say>* goes right after.

| Affirmative | Negative |
|-------------|----------|
| <say>Je parle.</say> | <say>Je ne parle pas.</say> |
| <say>Tu manges.</say> | <say>Tu ne manges pas.</say> |
| <say>Il aime le café.</say> | <say>Il n'aime pas le café.</say> |
| <say>Nous travaillons.</say> | <say>Nous ne travaillons pas.</say> |
| <say>Vous écoutez.</say> | <say>Vous n'écoutez pas.</say> |
| <say>Elles habitent ici.</say> | <say>Elles n'habitent pas ici.</say> |

> **Élision (remember Lesson 1):** before a vowel or silent `h`, *<say>ne</say>* → *<say>n'</say>*. *<say>Je n'aime pas</say>, <say>il n'habite pas</say>, <say>on n'écoute pas</say>.*

> **Trap #1 — `ne` drops in speech.** In everyday spoken French, the *<say>ne</say>* is routinely swallowed. You'll hear *<say>Je sais pas</say>, <say>j'aime pas ça</say>, <say>t'as pas faim?</say>* — only *<say>pas</say>* survives. In writing, exams, and formal speech, **always keep the full *<say>ne … pas</say>***. On the street, your ears will hear only the second half. (This is the opposite of English, where we slur the verb-helper itself: *I do not* → *I don't*. French slurs the negator instead.)

---

## Part 2: The `pas` family — variants of the second slot

The second part of the bracket isn't only `pas`. It's a **slot** that can hold different meanings. The `ne` stays put; only the second word changes.

| Construction | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| <say>ne … pas</say> | not | <say>Je ne parle pas.</say> — I don't speak. |
| <say>ne … jamais</say> | never | <say>Je ne mange jamais de viande.</say> — I never eat meat. |
| <say>ne … rien</say> | nothing | <say>Je ne vois rien.</say> — I see nothing. |
| <say>ne … personne</say> | nobody | <say>Je n'aime personne.</say> — I love nobody. |
| <say>ne … plus</say> | no longer / not anymore | <say>Je ne travaille plus.</say> — I don't work anymore. |
| <say>ne … pas encore</say> | not yet | <say>Je n'ai pas encore fini.</say> — I haven't finished yet. |
| <say>ne … que</say> | only (not actually a negation!) | <say>Je ne bois que de l'eau.</say> — I drink only water. |

> **Don't combine `pas` with another negator.** You can't say *<say>ne pas jamais</say>*. Pick **one** second word.

> **Trap #2:** *<say>rien</say>* and *<say>personne</say>* can act as the **subject** — then they jump to the front, and *<say>ne</say>* stays before the verb:
> - *<say>Personne ne parle.</say>* — Nobody is speaking.
> - *<say>Rien n'est facile.</say>* — Nothing is easy.

## Part 3: The great `de` rule after negation

> After a negation, **un / une / des / du / de la / de l'** all collapse into **`de`** (or `d'` before a vowel).

This is the single biggest grammar reflex you'll need to build. In English, the article doesn't change: "I have **a** dog" → "I don't have **a** dog". In French, the indefinite/partitive article **dies** and is replaced by `de`.

| Affirmative | Negative |
|-------------|----------|
| <say>J'ai un chien.</say> | <say>Je n'ai pas de chien.</say> |
| <say>Elle a une voiture.</say> | <say>Elle n'a pas de voiture.</say> |
| <say>Nous avons des amis.</say> | <say>Nous n'avons pas d'amis.</say> |
| <say>Il mange du pain.</say> | <say>Il ne mange pas de pain.</say> |
| <say>Tu bois de la bière.</say> | <say>Tu ne bois pas de bière.</say> |
| <say>Vous prenez de l'eau.</say> | <say>Vous ne prenez pas d'eau.</say> |

> **Memorize this as a chant:** *<say>pas de pain</say>, <say>pas de voiture</say>, <say>pas d'amis</say>*. Saying *<say>pas du pain</say>* is the most common English-speaker mistake — the partitive collapses.

> **Exception 1 — `être`.** After the verb *<say>être</say>*, the article **does NOT change**:
> - *<say>C'est un chat.</say>* → *<say>Ce n'est pas un chat.</say>* (NOT *<say>pas de chat</say>*)
> - *<say>C'est du café.</say>* → *<say>Ce n'est pas du café.</say>*

> **Exception 2 — definite articles `le / la / les` never change**, ever:
> - *<say>J'aime le café.</say>* → *<say>Je n'aime pas le café.</say>*
> - *<say>Tu connais les Dupont ?</say>* → *<say>Tu ne connais pas les Dupont ?</say>*

The logic: definite articles point at a specific thing ("the coffee, coffee-as-a-concept"). Negating that doesn't make the concept vanish. But *<say>un chien</say>* (some dog, any dog) — if you don't have one, the dog itself is gone, so its article disappears too.

## Part 4: Questions — three registers

> **English uses "do/does" to ask: "Do you speak French?" French does not.** Again, no auxiliary. Instead, French has **three ways** to turn a statement into a question. They are not synonyms — they're **three levels of formality**.

### Register 1: Intonation (casual, spoken)

Take the statement and **raise your voice** at the end. Word order doesn't change at all. This is the easiest and by far the most common in real conversation.

- *<say>Tu parles français.</say>* → *<say>Tu parles français ?</say>* ↗
- *<say>Vous habitez à Paris.</say>* → *<say>Vous habitez à Paris ?</say>* ↗
- *<say>Il aime le café.</say>* → *<say>Il aime le café ?</say>* ↗

In writing it's just a question mark. In speech it's a rising tone. That's it.

### Register 2: Est-ce que (neutral, all-purpose)

Stick the magic formula **est-ce que** in front (literally "is it that…"). The rest of the sentence keeps normal word order. This is the safe, universal question form — works in a café, on an exam, in a job interview.

- *<say>Est-ce que tu parles français ?</say>*
- *<say>Est-ce que vous habitez à Paris ?</say>*
- *<say>Est-ce qu'il aime le café ?</say>* (élision before a vowel!)

> Think of *<say>est-ce que</say>* as a verbal question mark you put at the front. Native speakers don't analyze it; they just feel a question is coming.

### Register 3: Inversion (formal, written, bookish)

Swap the **verb and the subject pronoun** and join them with a **hyphen**. This is the most formal form — typical of writing, news, formal speech.

- *<say>Tu parles français.</say>* → *<say>Parles-tu français ?</say>*
- *<say>Vous habitez à Paris.</say>* → *<say>Habitez-vous à Paris ?</say>*
- *<say>Il aime le café.</say>* → *<say>Aime-t-il le café ?</say>* ← see the **-t-**!

> **Trap #3 — the inserted `-t-`:** if the verb in the 3rd person singular (il / elle / on) ends in a **vowel** (typically `-e` or `-a`), French inserts a **-t-** between the verb and the pronoun, purely for sound (two vowels in a row are ugly to French ears):
> - *<say>parle-t-il</say>* (NOT *<say>parle-il</say>*)
> - *<say>aime-t-elle</say>*
> - *<say>va-t-on</say>*
> - *<say>a-t-elle un chien ?</say>* (the verb *<say>a</say>* ends in a vowel)
>
> If the verb already ends in `-t` or `-d`, no insertion is needed: *<say>est-il</say>, <say>sont-ils</say>, <say>prend-elle</say>*.

> **Inversion only works with pronouns.** With a noun subject, you keep the noun at the front and add a "doubled" pronoun: *<say>Marie parle-t-elle français ?</say>* — but in practice, with a noun subject, just use *<say>est-ce que</say>* and save yourself the trouble.

## Part 5: The three registers side by side

Memorize as a **trio**. One meaning — three forms.

| Intonation (street) | Est-ce que (neutral) | Inversion (bookish) |
|---------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| <say>Tu manges ?</say> | <say>Est-ce que tu manges ?</say> | <say>Manges-tu ?</say> |
| <say>Vous êtes français ?</say> | <say>Est-ce que vous êtes français ?</say> | <say>Êtes-vous français ?</say> |
| <say>Il a un chien ?</say> | <say>Est-ce qu'il a un chien ?</say> | <say>A-t-il un chien ?</say> |
| <say>Elle parle anglais ?</say> | <say>Est-ce qu'elle parle anglais ?</say> | <say>Parle-t-elle anglais ?</say> |
| <say>Nous travaillons ?</say> | <say>Est-ce que nous travaillons ?</say> | <say>Travaillons-nous ?</say> |

**Which one to use?**
- With friends: **intonation**.
- With strangers, in a shop, in class: **<say>est-ce que</say>**.
- In writing, in formal speech, on TV news: **inversion**.

If you only learn one for now, learn **<say>est-ce que</say>**. It's never wrong.

## Part 6: Question words

Seven essential "bricks". Learn them **with the construction**, not as bare words.

| Word | English | Example (est-ce que) | Example (inversion) |
|------|---------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **<say>qui</say>** | who | <say>Qui est-ce que tu aimes ?</say> | <say>Qui aimes-tu ?</say> |
| **<say>que / quoi</say>** | what | <say>Qu'est-ce que tu manges ?</say> | <say>Que manges-tu ?</say> |
| **<say>où</say>** | where | <say>Où est-ce que tu habites ?</say> | <say>Où habites-tu ?</say> |
| **<say>quand</say>** | when | <say>Quand est-ce que tu arrives ?</say> | <say>Quand arrives-tu ?</say> |
| **<say>comment</say>** | how | <say>Comment est-ce que tu vas ?</say> | <say>Comment vas-tu ?</say> |
| **<say>combien (de)</say>** | how much / how many | <say>Combien est-ce que ça coûte ?</say> | <say>Combien ça coûte ?</say> |
| **<say>pourquoi</say>** | why | <say>Pourquoi est-ce que tu pleures ?</say> | <say>Pourquoi pleures-tu ?</say> |

The pattern: **question word + est-ce que + normal sentence**, or **question word + inversion**.

### "What" is tricky — `que` vs `quoi` vs `qu'est-ce que`

English uses one word — "what". French has three forms depending on position:

- **<say>Que</say>** + inversion: *<say>Que fais-tu ?</say>* — What are you doing?
- **<say>Qu'est-ce que</say>** + normal order: *<say>Qu'est-ce que tu fais ?</say>* — same meaning, more common in speech.
- **<say>Quoi</say>** at the end (very casual) or after a preposition: *<say>Tu fais quoi ?</say>* / *<say>Avec quoi ?</say>* (with what?)

> **Trap #4 — élision:** *<say>que</say>* before a vowel → *<say>qu'</say>*. *<say>Qu'est-ce que tu fais ?</say> <say>Qu'aime-t-elle ?</say>*

> **Trap #5 — question word at the end (very casual):** in everyday speech, the question word often gets shoved to the end: *<say>Tu habites où ?</say> <say>Tu pars quand ?</say> <say>Ça coûte combien ?</say>* This sounds totally normal among friends. In writing or formal speech, put the question word at the front.

> **Answering "why":** the answer to *<say>pourquoi</say>* is *<say>parce que</say>* ("because"; *<say>parce qu'</say>* before a vowel). *— <say>Pourquoi tu pleures ?</say> — <say>Parce que je suis triste.</say>*

### `où` vs `ou` — same sound, different word

| Spelling | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **<say>où</say>** (with grave accent) | where |
| **<say>ou</say>** (no accent) | or |

The accent is the only difference, and it's mandatory. They're pronounced identically.

## Part 7: Vocabulary

### Question words and answers

| French | English | Note |
|--------|---------|------|
| <say>qui</say> | who | subject or object |
| <say>que</say> / <say>quoi</say> | what | *<say>que</say>* before a verb, *<say>quoi</say>* after a preposition or at the end |
| <say>où</say> | where / where to | with the accent! *<say>ou</say>* (no accent) = "or" |
| <say>quand</say> | when | final `d` is silent |
| <say>comment</say> | how | |
| <say>combien (de)</say> | how much / how many | *<say>combien de pommes</say>* — how many apples |
| <say>pourquoi</say> | why | |
| <say>parce que</say> | because | answer to *<say>pourquoi</say>* |

### Yes, no, maybe

| French | English | Note |
|--------|---------|------|
| <say>oui</say> | yes | answer to a normal question |
| **<say>si</say>** | yes (contradicting a negative question) | *— <say>Tu ne viens pas ?</say> — <say>Si !</say>* (Yes I am coming!) |
| <say>non</say> | no | |
| <say>peut-être</say> | maybe | |
| <say>bien sûr</say> | of course | |

> **Trap #6 — `si` vs `oui`:** English has only "yes". French splits it. After a **negative** question, you answer with **<say>si</say>** (not *<say>oui</say>*) to contradict.
> - *<say>Tu parles anglais ?</say>* — *<say>Oui, je parle anglais.</say>*
> - *<say>Tu ne parles pas anglais ?</say>* — *<say>Si, je parle anglais !</say>* (Yes I do — you were wrong!)
>
> German *doch* and Spanish older usage do the same thing; English used to ("yea/yes vs nay/no") but lost it.

### Connectors (the glue of any sentence)

| French | English | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| <say>et</say> | and | <say>Je parle français et anglais.</say> |
| <say>mais</say> | but | <say>J'aime le café mais je préfère le thé.</say> |
| <say>ou</say> | or | <say>Tu veux du thé ou du café ?</say> |
| <say>aussi</say> | also / too | <say>Moi aussi, j'aime Paris.</say> |
| <say>non plus</say> | neither / either (after a negation) | <say>— Je n'aime pas. — Moi non plus.</say> |
| <say>parce que</say> | because | <say>Je travaille parce que j'ai besoin d'argent.</say> |
| <say>alors</say> | so / then | <say>Tu es fatigué, alors repose-toi.</say> |

> **Trap #7 — "me too" vs "me neither":** English uses "me too" only after positives, "me neither" after negatives. French does the same split, but with **different words**:
> - After an affirmative: *— <say>J'aime ça.</say> — <say>Moi aussi.</say>*
> - After a negative: *— <say>Je n'aime pas.</say> — <say>Moi non plus.</say>*
>
> Don't say *<say>moi aussi</say>* after a negation — it sounds like you mean the opposite.

## Part 9: Question-answer matrix

**Mirror principle:** the answer is already wired into the question. Don't build from scratch — just flip.

| Question (est-ce que) | Affirmative answer | Negative answer |
|-----------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| <say>Est-ce que tu parles français ?</say> | <say>Oui, je parle français.</say> | <say>Non, je ne parle pas français.</say> |
| <say>Est-ce que vous avez un chien ?</say> | <say>Oui, j'ai un chien.</say> | <say>Non, je n'ai pas de chien.</say> |
| <say>Est-ce qu'il aime le café ?</say> | <say>Oui, il aime le café.</say> | <say>Non, il n'aime pas le café.</say> |
| <say>Est-ce qu'elle mange de la viande ?</say> | <say>Oui, elle mange de la viande.</say> | <say>Non, elle ne mange pas de viande.</say> |
| <say>Est-ce que vous habitez à Paris ?</say> | <say>Oui, nous habitons à Paris.</say> | <say>Non, nous n'habitons pas à Paris.</say> |
| <say>Est-ce qu'ils sont français ?</say> | <say>Oui, ils sont français.</say> | <say>Non, ils ne sont pas français.</say> |

> **Watch the `de`:** negating *<say>avoir un chien</say>* → *<say>pas de chien</say>* (un disappears). But negating *<say>aimer le café</say>* → *<say>pas le café</say>* (definite article stays). Review Part 3.

(Verbs from Lesson 7.)

2. Tu manges du pain.

<summary>Key</summary>

3. *Il **n'**habite **pas** à Lyon.*
4. *Nous **n'**écoutons **pas** la radio.* (la stays — definite article)
5. *Vous **n'**avez **pas de** voiture.* (une → de)
6. *Elles **n'**aiment **pas** le chocolat.* (le stays)
7. *Marie **ne** travaille **pas** ici.*
8. *Je **n'**ai **pas d'**amis à Paris.* (des → d')

</details>

(Verbs from Lessons 4 and 7.)

Example: *Tu es français.* → *Tu es français ? / Est-ce que tu es français ? / Es-tu français ?*

2. Il aime Marie.

<details>

2. *Il aime Marie ? / Est-ce qu'il aime Marie ? / Aime-**t**-il Marie ?* (insert -t-!)
3. *Elle parle espagnol ? / Est-ce qu'elle parle espagnol ? / Parle-**t**-elle espagnol ?*

</details>

5. **Combien** ça coûte ?
6. **Comment** tu vas ?

</details>

### Exercise 4. Translate into French

(Recycling adjectives from Lesson 6 and nationalities from Lesson 4.)

1. I'm not French.
2. She doesn't eat anything.
3. We never work on Sundays. (*le dimanche*)
4. They don't have a car.
5. I don't smoke anymore. (*fumer*)
6. Nobody understands. (*comprendre* — irregular, use *comprend*)
7. Why don't you answer? (*répondre* — *tu réponds*)
8. — I don't like coffee. — Me neither.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Je **ne** suis **pas** français.* (or *française*, if you're a woman)
2. *Elle **ne** mange **rien**.*

4. *Ils **n'**ont **pas de** voiture.* (une → de!)
5. *Je **ne** fume **plus**.*
6. *Personne **ne** comprend.* (subject — *personne* goes first)
7. *Pourquoi **ne** réponds-tu **pas** ?* (or: *Pourquoi est-ce que tu ne réponds pas ?*)
8. *— Je **n'**aime **pas** le café. — Moi **non plus**.* (after a negation — *non plus*!)

</details>

### Exercise 5. Answer with `si`, not `oui`

A negative question (with `ne … pas`) takes **si** for an affirmative answer, not *oui*.

Answer affirmatively:

1. — Tu ne parles pas anglais ? — ___, je parle anglais.
2. — Vous n'avez pas faim ? — ___, j'ai très faim.
3. — Elle n'aime pas le chocolat ? — ___, elle adore le chocolat.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *— **Si**, je parle anglais.*
2. *— **Si**, j'ai très faim.*
3. *— **Si**, elle adore le chocolat.*

> Remember: *oui* for an ordinary question, *si* to contradict a negative one.

</details>

```
  1. Intonation:  Tu parles français ?     (street)
  2. Est-ce que:  Est-ce que tu parles… ?  (neutral, all-purpose)
  3. Inversion:   Parles-tu français ?     (formal / written)
  Insert -t- in 3rd-person sg. between two vowels:
  aime-t-il, va-t-elle, a-t-on.

QUESTION WORDS:
  qui       — who
  que/quoi  — what  (qu'est-ce que = most common)
  où        — where
  quand     — when
  comment   — how
  combien   — how much / how many
  pourquoi  — why    → answered with parce que…

OUI / SI / NON:
  Tu parles ?       — Oui / Non.
  Tu ne parles pas ? — Si / Non.   (use SI to contradict a negative!)

"ME TOO" / "ME NEITHER":
  J'aime ça.       — Moi aussi.
  Je n'aime pas.   — Moi non plus.

CONNECTORS:
  et — and | mais — but | ou — or
  aussi — also | non plus — neither
  parce que — because | alors — so / then
```
