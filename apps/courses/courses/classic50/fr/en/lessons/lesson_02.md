# Lesson 2: Nouns. Gender and number. Numbers 20–100
**Vocabulary: House, food, animals**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the mechanics of gender and number (5 minutes)
2. **Learn every noun with its article** — never just *table*, always *la table*. The article is part of the word.
3. **Drill out loud** — say *<say>un livre</say> → <say>des livres</say>*, *<say>une table</say> → <say>des tables</say>* until it's automatic. Your mouth has to remember.

> Knowing the rule = 5%. Training your mouth = 95%.
> A wrong gender ripples through the whole sentence — article, adjective, pronoun, past participle. That's why you **never** learn a noun without its article.

---

## Part 1: Gender — two boxes, no logic

French has **two genders**: masculine (*masculin*) and feminine (*féminin*). There is no neuter. Every single noun — animate or inanimate, concrete or abstract — belongs to one of the two boxes.

| Masculine (le / un) | Feminine (la / une) |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| **<say>le livre</say>** (book) | **<say>la table</say>** (table) |
| **<say>le chat</say>** (cat) | **<say>la maison</say>** (house) |
| **<say>un stylo</say>** (pen) | **<say>une chaise</say>** (chair) |

> **The big mental shift for English speakers:** English has no grammatical gender on objects. *The book, the table, the cat* — all just *the*. French slaps a gender on every noun, and you can't dodge it. You can't say "the book" without first choosing *<say>le</say>* or *<say>la</say>*. This is the single biggest difference between English and French at the beginner level. Don't fight it — just accept that every noun comes pre-married to an article.

> **Don't try to reason it out.** *<say>La table</say>* is feminine. *<say>Le livre</say>* is masculine. *<say>La maison</say>* (house) is feminine, *<say>le château</say>* (castle) is masculine. There is **no semantic logic** — just learn each noun with its article, the way you learned to say "an apple" not "a apple".

---

## Part 2: Guess the gender by ending (70% hit rate)

There are endings that almost always point to a specific gender. This is your main tool — and many of these endings exist in English too, because they came over with the Normans in 1066.

### Feminine endings

| Ending | Gender | Examples | English cognate |
|--------|--------|----------|-----------------|
| **-tion / -sion** | f | <say>la nation</say>, <say>la station</say>, <say>la décision</say> | nation, station, decision |
| **-té** | f | <say>la liberté</say>, <say>la beauté</say>, <say>la cité</say> | liberty, beauty, city |
| **-ée** | f | <say>la journée</say>, <say>la soirée</say>, <say>la pensée</say> | (the day, evening — duration sense) |
| **-ette** | f | <say>la baguette</say>, <say>la fourchette</say> | (small/feminine diminutive) |
| **-ance / -ence** | f | <say>la chance</say>, <say>la patience</say>, <say>la France</say> | chance, patience |
| **-ie** | f | <say>la boulangerie</say>, <say>la pharmacie</say>, <say>la vie</say> | pharmacy, biology (= -y in English) |
| **-ure** | f | <say>la voiture</say>, <say>la nature</say>, <say>la lecture</say> | nature, lecture |
| **-eur** (abstract) | f | <say>la couleur</say>, <say>la peur</say>, <say>la chaleur</say> | (color, fear, heat — abstract qualities) |

### Masculine endings

| Ending | Gender | Examples | English cognate |
|--------|--------|----------|-----------------|
| **-ment** | m | <say>le moment</say>, <say>le gouvernement</say> | moment, government |
| **-age** | m | <say>le fromage</say>, <say>le voyage</say>, <say>le garage</say> | voyage, garage, marriage |
| **-eau** | m | <say>le bateau</say>, <say>le gâteau</say>, <say>le bureau</say> | bureau |
| **-isme** | m | <say>le tourisme</say>, <say>le réalisme</say> | tourism, realism |
| **-eur** (person/agent) | m | <say>le professeur</say>, <say>le chanteur</say> | professor, (singer) |
| **-ier** | m | <say>le boulanger</say>, <say>le cahier</say> | (baker, notebook — agent/object) |
| **-on** (often) | m | <say>le poisson</say>, <say>le citron</say> | (fish, lemon) |

> **English-speaker shortcut:** if you see a word that looks like an English `-tion`, `-sion`, `-ty`, or `-ence` word, it's feminine 99% of the time: *<say>nation</say>, <say>station</say>, <say>université</say>, <say>distance</say>*. If you see `-ment` or `-ism`, it's masculine: *<say>gouvernement</say>, <say>tourisme</say>*. You already know a thousand French nouns through English — you just need to bolt on the right article.

### Exceptions worth memorizing

- **-age → m**, BUT: *<say>la plage</say>* (beach), *<say>la cage</say>*, *<say>la page</say>*, *<say>l'image</say>*, *<say>la nage</say>* (swimming)
- **-eau → m**, BUT: *<say>l'eau</say>* (f — water), *<say>la peau</say>* (skin)
- **-ée → f**, BUT: *<say>le musée</say>* (museum), *<say>le lycée</say>* (high school)

## Part 3: Plurals — the marker is silent

The single most important fact: the plural `-s` is **not pronounced**. *<say>Le chat</say>* and *<say>les chats</say>* sound identical except for the article. The article is what tells your ear it's plural.

| Singular | Plural | Pronunciation |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| <say>le livre</say> | **<say>les livres</say>** | "luh LEEVR" → "lay LEEVR" |
| <say>la table</say> | **<say>les tables</say>** | "lah TAHBL" → "lay TAHBL" |
| <say>un chat</say> | **<say>des chats</say>** | "uhn SHAH" → "day SHAH" |
| <say>une maison</say> | **<say>des maisons</say>** | "ewn meh-ZOHN" → "day meh-ZOHN" |

> **English-speaker trap:** in English, the plural is loud and clear — *cat / cats*, *book / books*. The `-s` is what you hear. In French, the `-s` is dead. **Listen to the article**, not the end of the noun. *<say>Le</say>, <say>la</say>, <say>l'</say>* → singular. *<say>Les</say>, <say>des</say>* → plural.

### Liaison saves the plural — sometimes

If the noun starts with a **vowel** or a **silent h**, the final `-s` of the article wakes up as a **z-sound**. This is how you hear plurality with vowel-initial nouns:

- *<say>les amis</say>* → "lay-**z**ah-MEE"
- *<say>des oiseaux</say>* → "day-**z**wah-ZOH"
- *<say>les hommes</say>* → "lay-**z**OHM"
- *<say>un oiseau</say>* → "**uhn**-wah-ZOH" (the n of *<say>un</say>* wakes up too)

When you hear the **z** appear between an article and a noun, you're hearing the plural. With consonant-initial nouns (*<say>les chats</say>*, *<say>des livres</say>*), there's nothing to wake up — the article does all the work.

## Part 4: Irregular plurals

Most nouns just take `+s`. But a handful of endings change shape. These are the ones English speakers stumble on because they look weird on the page.

| Singular ending | Plural ending | Example |
|-----------------|---------------|---------|
| **-s, -x, -z** (already a "hiss") | **no change** | <say>le bras</say> → <say>les bras</say>; <say>le nez</say> → <say>les nez</say>; <say>le prix</say> → <say>les prix</say> |
| **-eau** | **-eaux** | <say>le bateau</say> → <say>les bateaux</say>; <say>le gâteau</say> → <say>les gâteaux</say> |
| **-au** | **-aux** | <say>le tuyau</say> → <say>les tuyaux</say> (pipes) |
| **-eu** | **-eux** | <say>le cheveu</say> → <say>les cheveux</say> (hair) |
| **-al** | **-aux** | <say>le journal</say> → <say>les journaux</say>; <say>le cheval</say> → <say>les chevaux</say> |
| **-ail** (usually) | **-ails** | <say>le détail</say> → <say>les détails</say> |
| **-ail** (special) | **-aux** | <say>le travail</say> → **<say>les travaux</say>** |

> **Pronouncing `-eaux` and `-aux`:** exactly like `-eau` and `-au` — just "oh". The `-x` is silent, same as `-s`. *<say>Les bateaux</say>* → "lay bah-TOH", *<say>les journaux</say>* → "lay zhoor-NOH". On the page it looks dramatic, on your ear it's nothing.

### Sub-exceptions to know

- **-al → +s** (not -aux) for a small group: *<say>le bal</say>, <say>le carnaval</say>, <say>le festival</say>, <say>le récital</say>* → plurals just add `-s`.
- **-eu → +s** (not -eux): *<say>le pneu</say>* (tire) → *<say>les pneus</say>*, *<say>le bleu</say>* (bruise) → *<say>les bleus</say>*.

> **Bonus fully irregular ones:** *<say>l'œil</say>* (eye) → *<say>les yeux</say>* (eyes). *<say>Monsieur</say>* → *<say>messieurs</say>*. *<say>Madame</say>* → *<say>mesdames</say>*. These you just have to know.

## Part 5: Numbers 20–100

### Tens from 20 to 60 — sensible

| 20 | 30 | 40 | 50 | 60 |
|----|----|----|----|----|
| <say>vingt</say> | <say>trente</say> | <say>quarante</say> | <say>cinquante</say> | <say>soixante</say> |

**Pronunciation:** *<say>vingt</say>* "vahn" (nasal; the *gt* is silent), *<say>trente</say>* "trahnt", *<say>quarante</say>* "kah-RAHNT", *<say>cinquante</say>* "sank-AHNT", *<say>soixante</say>* "swah-SAHNT".

### Tens from 70 to 90 — French does math

| 70 | 80 | 90 |
|----|----|----|
| <say>soixante-dix</say> (60+10) | <say>quatre-vingts</say> (4×20) | <say>quatre-vingt-dix</say> (4×20+10) |

> **The infamous French numbers.** Yes, really: 70 is "sixty-ten", 80 is "four-twenties", 90 is "four-twenty-ten". And 97 is *<say>quatre-vingt-dix-sept</say>* — literally "four-twenty-ten-seven". This is a legacy of an old Celtic base-20 counting system. Belgium, Switzerland, and parts of Africa use the saner *<say>septante</say>* (70), *<say>huitante</say> / <say>octante</say>* (80), and *<say>nonante</say>* (90) — but in France, only this arithmetic version is accepted.

### Compound numbers: the hyphen rule

Between a ten and a unit, you use a **hyphen**: *<say>trente-deux</say>* (32), *<say>quarante-cinq</say>* (45), *<say>soixante-huit</say>* (68).

**But!** Before `1` and `11`, French uses **<say>et</say>** ("and") instead of a hyphen:

- 21 = *<say>vingt et un</say>*
- 31 = *<say>trente et un</say>*
- 41 = *<say>quarante et un</say>*
- 51 = *<say>cinquante et un</say>*
- 61 = *<say>soixante et un</say>*
- 71 = *<say>soixante et onze</say>* (because 71 = 60 + 11)

**Exceptions:** 81 and 91 — **no et**: *<say>quatre-vingt-un</say>*, *<say>quatre-vingt-onze</say>*. (Possibly because they're already long enough.)

### Full table 60–99

| 60 <say>soixante</say> | 70 <say>soixante-dix</say> | 80 <say>quatre-vingts</say> | 90 <say>quatre-vingt-dix</say> |
|---|---|---|---|
| 61 <say>soixante et un</say> | 71 <say>soixante et onze</say> | 81 <say>quatre-vingt-un</say> | 91 <say>quatre-vingt-onze</say> |
| 62 <say>soixante-deux</say> | 72 <say>soixante-douze</say> | 82 <say>quatre-vingt-deux</say> | 92 <say>quatre-vingt-douze</say> |
| 65 <say>soixante-cinq</say> | 75 <say>soixante-quinze</say> | 85 <say>quatre-vingt-cinq</say> | 95 <say>quatre-vingt-quinze</say> |
| 69 <say>soixante-neuf</say> | 79 <say>soixante-dix-neuf</say> | 89 <say>quatre-vingt-neuf</say> | 99 <say>quatre-vingt-dix-neuf</say> |

### The little `s` on *quatre-vingts*

- *<say>quatre-vingts</say>* = exactly 80 (with `s`)
- *<say>quatre-vingt-deux</say>* = 82 (no `s`, because another number follows)

Same with *<say>cent</say>*: *<say>cent</say>* = 100, *<say>deux cents</say>* = 200, but *<say>deux cent un</say>* = 201 (no `s`). Rule of thumb: the `s` survives only if the multiplier-word **ends the number**.

### Number 100

**<say>cent</say>** — "sahn" (nasal; the *t* is silent). Used with **no article** before a noun: *<say>cent euros</say>*, not *<say>un cent euros</say>*.

Before a vowel, liaison: *<say>cent ans</say>* → "sahn-**t**-AHN".

## Part 8: The matrix — question and answer

Mirror the answer back from the question. Do both voices.

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>C'est un livre ?</say> | <say>Oui, c'est un livre.</say> |
| <say>C'est une table ?</say> | <say>Oui, c'est une table.</say> |
| <say>Ce sont des chats ?</say> | <say>Oui, ce sont des chats.</say> |
| <say>Ce sont des oiseaux ?</say> | <say>Oui, ce sont des oiseaux.</say> |
| <say>C'est le chien de Marc ?</say> | <say>Oui, c'est le chien de Marc.</say> |
| <say>Ce sont les journaux d'hier ?</say> | <say>Oui, ce sont les journaux d'hier.</say> |

**Negative form:** *<say>Non, ce n'est pas un livre.</say> / <say>Non, ce ne sont pas des chats.</say>*

> *<say>C'est</say>* = "it is / this is" (singular). *<say>Ce sont</say>* = "they are / these are" (plural). You'll learn more about these in Lesson 4 — for now, just mirror them.

    -ance, -ence                -ier
    -ie                         -eur (person: professeur)
    -ure
    -eur (abstract: couleur)

  Cognates help: English -tion / -ty / -ence / -ism = same gender in French.

  Exceptions to know:
    la plage, la page, la cage (NOT -age → m)
    l'eau, la peau (NOT -eau → m)
    le musée, le lycée (NOT -ée → f)

PLURAL:
  Final -s is SILENT. The article does all the work in speech:
    le → les, la → les, un → des, une → des

  Irregular endings:
    -eau → -eaux  (le bateau → les bateaux)
    -eu  → -eux   (le cheveu → les cheveux)
    -al  → -aux   (le journal → les journaux)
    -s/-x/-z     → no change (le bras → les bras)
    Special:     le travail → les TRAVAUX

LIAISON in the plural — you hear "z":
  les_amis [lay-zah-mee], des_oiseaux [day-zwah-zoh]

NUMBERS 20–100:
  20 vingt        60 soixante
  30 trente       70 soixante-DIX (60+10)
  40 quarante     80 QUATRE-VINGTS (4×20)
  50 cinquante    90 quatre-vingt-dix
                 100 cent

  21 = vingt ET un       31 = trente ET un
  71 = soixante ET onze  (60+11)
  81 = quatre-vingt-un   (NO et!)
  91 = quatre-vingt-onze (NO et!)

  "s" on quatre-vingts and cents — only when the number ENDS there:
    quatre-vingts (80) / quatre-vingt-deux (82, no s)
    deux cents (200)   / deux cent un (201, no s)

GOLDEN RULE:
  Never learn a noun without its article.
  Not "table" — "LA table". The article = part of the word.
