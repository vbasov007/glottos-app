# Lesson 4: Personal pronouns and the verb être
**Vocabulary: Nationalities, countries, professions, family members**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the pronoun map and the conjugation (5 minutes, no more)
2. **Say it out loud** — run *être* through all six persons like a musical scale
3. **Drill the matrix** — answer questions without building from scratch: the answer is in the question

> This is your **first verb**. Half the language sits on top of it ("I am", "this is", "they are"). If *<say>être</say>* doesn't fall out of your mouth automatically, everything after this gets harder.

---

## Part 1: Subject pronouns — France has more "you"s than England

In French, **the subject is mandatory**. Unlike Spanish or Italian, you can never drop it — "am tired" is not a sentence. You always need a pronoun.

| Person | French | English | Note |
|--------|--------|---------|------|
| 1st sg. | **<say>je</say>** (<say>j'</say>) | I | élision before a vowel: *<say>j'ai</say>*, *<say>j'écoute</say>*. And — lowercase! Only English capitalizes "I" |
| 2nd sg. | **<say>tu</say>** | you (informal) | one person you'd call by first name |
| 3rd sg. m. | **<say>il</say>** | he / it | also "it" for any masculine noun |
| 3rd sg. f. | **<say>elle</say>** | she / it | also "it" for any feminine noun |
| 3rd sg. | **<say>on</say>** | we / one / people | conversational "we"; conjugates like *<say>il</say>* |
| 1st pl. | **<say>nous</say>** | we | written and formal |
| 2nd pl. / formal | **<say>vous</say>** | you (formal sg. OR plural) | both polite singular and any plural |
| 3rd pl. m. | **<say>ils</say>** | they (m. or mixed) | any mixed group → **<say>ils</say>** |
| 3rd pl. f. | **<say>elles</say>** | they (women / fem. things only) | one man in the room → it's *<say>ils</say>* |

### The four English-speaker traps

> **Trap 1: tu vs vous.** English collapsed this distinction four centuries ago (we lost "thou"). French still uses it every single day. *<say>Tu</say>* = friends, family, kids, pets, God, anyone you're on a first-name basis with. *<say>Vous</say>* = strangers, anyone older, anyone in authority, any business interaction. Get this wrong and you sound either rude or sarcastically stiff. **Default to *<say>vous</say>* with any adult you don't know.** The native will offer *<say>tu</say>* if and when appropriate ("<say>on peut se tutoyer</say>").

> **Trap 2: il and elle are not just "he/she".** Because French nouns have grammatical gender, *<say>il</say>* and *<say>elle</say>* also mean "it". *<say>La table — elle est grande.</say>* "The table — she is big." Don't translate as "she" in your head; translate as "it". Your English brain wants to reserve *<say>il</say>/<say>elle</say>* for people. French uses them for everything.

> **Trap 3: on.** Native French speakers say *<say>on</say>* way more than *<say>nous</say>*. It covers three English ideas at once:
> - "we" (casual): *<say>On y va</say>* = "We're going"
> - "people / they" (generic): *<say>En France, on mange tard</say>* = "In France, people eat late"
> - "one" (formal English): *<say>On ne dit pas ça</say>* = "One doesn't say that"
>
> The catch: *<say>on</say>* conjugates like *<say>il</say>/<say>elle</say>*, not like *<say>nous</say>*. *<say>On est prêts</say>* — never *<say>on sommes</say>*.

> **Trap 4: ils swallows elles.** A room of 99 women + 1 man = *<say>ils</say>*. Masculine wins in the plural, always. Politically uncomfortable but grammatically iron.

## Part 2: Être — the most important verb in French

*<say>Être</say>* = "to be". It's also the most irregular verb in the language — completely unpredictable forms that don't look like the infinitive at all. **Memorize it cold, out loud, today.**

| Pronoun | Form | Pronunciation | English |
|---------|------|---------------|---------|
| je | **<say>suis</say>** | "swee" | I am |
| tu | **<say>es</say>** | "ay" | you are |
| il / elle / on | **<say>est</say>** | "ay" | he / she / it / one is |
| nous | **<say>sommes</say>** | "som" | we are |
| vous | **<say>êtes</say>** | "et" | you are |
| ils / elles | **<say>sont</say>** | "sohn" (nasal) | they are |

### Compare to English "to be"

English already has the wildest verb in its own language: I **am**, you **are**, he **is**, we **are**, they **are**. Four different forms from one infinitive. French does the same thing — different stems for different persons — just with different shapes. You already know the principle; only the surfaces change.

### Pronunciation traps

- *<say>es</say>* and *<say>est</say>* are **pronounced identically** — both "ay". The final -s and -t are silent. The only way to tell them apart is in writing or by the pronoun in front.
- *<say>suis</say>* is **one syllable**: "swee". Not "soo-iss".
- *<say>êtes</say>* is the only form with a circumflex. The ê marks a Latin *s* that disappeared — compare English *be**est***. Read it as "et".
- *<say>sont</say>* is nasal: "sohn", no final t.

### Liaison is mandatory

After *<say>est</say>*, *<say>sont</say>*, *<say>êtes</say>*, *<say>sommes</say>*, a vowel triggers a liaison — a silent consonant springs back to life:

- *<say>vous êtes</say>* → "voo-**z**-et" (the s of *<say>vous</say>* connects)
- *<say>nous sommes</say>* → "noo-**z**-som"
- *<say>ils sont</say>* → "eel-**z**-sohn"
- *<say>il est américain</say>* → "eel-ay-**t**-ah-may-ree-KAN" (the t of *<say>est</say>* wakes up)

### Negation: ne … pas wraps the verb

French negation comes in two pieces — *<say>ne</say>* in front, *<say>pas</say>* after:

- *<say>Je ne suis pas anglais.</say>* — I'm not English.
- *<say>Il n'est pas ici.</say>* — He's not here. (élision: *<say>ne</say>* → *<say>n'</say>* before a vowel)
- *<say>Nous ne sommes pas prêts.</say>* — We're not ready.

> In casual spoken French the *<say>ne</say>* often drops entirely: *<say>Je suis pas anglais.</say>* That's fine for listening, but write it with *<say>ne</say>* until you have native-level instincts.

## Part 3: The four jobs of être

In English, "to be" handles a half-dozen jobs (identity, description, location, age, weather, existence…). French splits some of these off onto other verbs — most famously **<say>avoir</say>** for age and physical states ("I am hungry" = *<say>J'ai faim</say>*, literally "I have hunger"; you'll see this in Lesson 5). But *<say>être</say>* still does the core jobs:

| Job | Example | English |
|-----|---------|---------|
| **Identity** (this is who/what something is) | *<say>C'est Marie.</say>* | This is Marie. |
| **Profession** (no article!) | *<say>Je suis professeur.</say>* | I'm a teacher. |
| **Nationality / origin** | *<say>Tu es américain.</say>* | You're American. |
| **Description** (with an adjective) | *<say>Elle est grande.</say>* | She is tall. |
| **Location** | *<say>Nous sommes à Paris.</say>* | We are in Paris. |
| **Time** | *<say>Il est cinq heures.</say>* | It's five o'clock. |

## Part 4: The "no article" trap — the single biggest English-speaker mistake

Here is the rule that catches every English speaker. **Read it twice.**

> When you state someone's **profession** or **nationality** with *<say>être</say>*, French drops the article. There is no "a" before the noun.

| ✅ French | ❌ Literal English translation | ✅ Real English |
|-----------|--------------------------------|-----------------|
| <say>Je suis professeur.</say> | "I am teacher." | I am **a** teacher. |
| <say>Elle est médecin.</say> | "She is doctor." | She is **a** doctor. |
| <say>Il est étudiant.</say> | "He is student." | He is **a** student. |
| <say>Nous sommes français.</say> | "We are French." | We are French. (same in English) |
| <say>Tu es journaliste ?</say> | "You are journalist?" | Are you **a** journalist? |

Your English instinct says "she is **a** doctor" → *<say>Elle est une médecin</say>*. **Wrong.** Drop the article. *<say>Elle est médecin.</say>*

### Why? Two ways to think about it

1. **"Profession as adjective" hack.** Treat *<say>médecin</say>* / *<say>professeur</say>* / *<say>étudiant</say>* like an adjective in this slot — "she is doctorly", "he is studentish". Adjectives don't take articles, and that's how French sees it here.
2. **The flip side**: French DOES use the article when you frame the same fact as *introducing/identifying* someone. That's the *<say>c'est</say>* construction in the next section.

### Nationalities follow the same rule

| ✅ Without article (adjective) | ✅ With article (noun, capitalized) |
|------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| *<say>Il est anglais.</say>* — He's English. | *<say>C'est un Anglais.</say>* — He's an Englishman. |
| *<say>Elle est française.</say>* — She's French. | *<say>C'est une Française.</say>* — She's a Frenchwoman. |
| *<say>Nous sommes américains.</say>* — We're American. | *<say>Ce sont des Américains.</say>* — They're Americans. |

Notice the **capital letter** when it's a noun referring to a person, lowercase when it's an adjective. English does almost the opposite — we always capitalize "French", whether it's an adjective ("French wine") or a noun ("a Frenchman"). French is stricter: adjective = lowercase, noun-for-person = capital.

## Part 5: c'est vs il est — the introduction/description split

This is the natural follow-up to Part 4. Both translate as "he is / she is / it is" in English, but French splits them.

| Construction | Use it for | Example |
|--------------|------------|---------|
| **<say>c'est</say>** + noun with article | introducing / identifying | *<say>C'est un médecin.</say>* (This is a doctor.) |
| **<say>c'est</say>** + name | pointing at a person | *<say>C'est Marie.</say>* |
| **<say>c'est</say>** + adjective (masc.) | a general / abstract judgment | *<say>C'est bon.</say>* (That's good. / It's good.) |
| **<say>il / elle est</say>** + adjective | describing a specific person/thing | *<say>Il est grand.</say>* (He is tall.) |
| **<say>il / elle est</say>** + profession/nationality (no article!) | classifying | *<say>Elle est médecin.</say>* |

**Plural:** for introduction, *<say>c'est</say>* becomes *<say>ce sont</say>*: *<say>Ce sont des étudiants.</say>* (These are students.)

### The contrast you have to internalize

| ✅ Right | ✅ Also right | ❌ Wrong |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| <say>Il est médecin.</say> | <say>C'est un médecin.</say> | ~~Il est un médecin.~~ |
| <say>Elle est anglaise.</say> | <say>C'est une Anglaise.</say> | ~~Elle est une anglaise.~~ |
| <say>Ils sont étudiants.</say> | <say>Ce sont des étudiants.</say> | ~~Ils sont des étudiants.~~ |

> **The rule in 10 words:** Article = *<say>c'est</say>*. No article = *<say>il/elle est</say>*.

## Part 6: Nationality adjectives — gender agreement

Adjectives in French **agree with the person they describe**. Default move: add **-e** for feminine.

| Masculine | Feminine | English |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| <say>anglais</say> | <say>anglaise</say> | English |
| <say>américain</say> | <say>américaine</say> | American |
| <say>français</say> | <say>française</say> | French |
| <say>allemand</say> | <say>allemande</say> | German |
| <say>espagnol</say> | <say>espagnole</say> | Spanish |
| <say>chinois</say> | <say>chinoise</say> | Chinese |
| <say>japonais</say> | <say>japonaise</say> | Japanese |
| <say>italien</say> | <say>italienne</say> | Italian (double n!) |
| <say>canadien</say> | <say>canadienne</say> | Canadian (double n!) |
| <say>russe</say> | <say>russe</say> | Russian (already ends in -e, no change) |

### The pronunciation effect of the silent -e

This is the **secret** of French adjective agreement. The -e itself is silent. But it stops the previous consonant from being silent — so a consonant that died in the masculine **comes back to life** in the feminine.

- *<say>anglais</say>* → "ahn-**GLAY**" (silent s)
- *<say>anglaise</say>* → "ahn-**GLAYZ**" (the s wakes up and sounds like z, because it's no longer final)
- *<say>français</say>* → "frahn-**SAY**" (silent s)
- *<say>française</say>* → "frahn-**SAYZ**"
- *<say>allemand</say>* → "al-**MAHN**" (silent d, nasal an)
- *<say>allemande</say>* → "al-**MAHND**" (the d wakes up; the nasal is broken because the n is no longer final)

> **Listen for this difference** with native speakers. You can usually hear the gender of a French person by whether the consonant at the end of their nationality is alive or dead.

### Plural — just add -s

- *<say>anglais</say>* (m. sg.) → *<say>anglais</say>* (m. pl.) — no change, already ends in -s
- *<say>française</say>* → *<say>françaises</say>*
- *<say>italien</say>* → *<say>italiens</say>*
- *<say>italienne</say>* → *<say>italiennes</say>*
- *<say>américaine</say>* → *<say>américaines</say>*

The final -s is **silent** in speech. The only way to hear "plural" is the article in front of the noun (*<say>les</say>* vs *<say>le</say>/<say>la</say>*) — which we covered in Lesson 3.

## Part 7: Countries — the en / au / aux trick

Countries have gender in French, and the preposition for "to / in / from" depends on it.

| Country | Gender | "to / in" | "from" |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------|
| <say>la France</say> | f. | <say>en France</say> | <say>de France</say> |
| <say>l'Angleterre</say> | f. | <say>en Angleterre</say> | <say>d'Angleterre</say> |
| <say>l'Italie</say> | f. | <say>en Italie</say> | <say>d'Italie</say> |
| <say>l'Allemagne</say> | f. | <say>en Allemagne</say> | <say>d'Allemagne</say> |
| <say>l'Espagne</say> | f. | <say>en Espagne</say> | <say>d'Espagne</say> |
| <say>la Chine</say> | f. | <say>en Chine</say> | <say>de Chine</say> |
| <say>la Russie</say> | f. | <say>en Russie</say> | <say>de Russie</say> |
| <say>le Japon</say> | m. | <say>au Japon</say> | <say>du Japon</say> |
| <say>le Canada</say> | m. | <say>au Canada</say> | <say>du Canada</say> |
| <say>le Mexique</say> | m. | <say>au Mexique</say> | <say>du Mexique</say> |
| <say>les États-Unis</say> | pl. | <say>aux États-Unis</say> | <say>des États-Unis</say> |

### The quick rule

- Country ends in **-e** → usually **feminine** → **<say>en</say>** / **<say>de</say>**
- Otherwise → **masculine** → **<say>au</say>** / **<say>du</say>**
- Plural country → **<say>aux</say>** / **<say>des</say>**
- The only common exception is *<say>le Mexique</say>* (ends in -e but masculine).

### For cities, always use à

Cities have no gender attached: **<say>à Paris</say>**, **<say>à Londres</say>**, **<say>à New York</say>**, **<say>à Tokyo</say>**.

| ✅ | ❌ |
|---|---|
| <say>Je suis à Paris.</say> | ~~Je suis en Paris.~~ |
| <say>Nous habitons à New York.</say> | ~~Nous habitons en New York.~~ |

So: *<say>Je suis à Paris, en France.</say>* "I'm in Paris, in France." Or: *<say>Il habite à Tokyo, au Japon.</say>*

## Language scales

Run *<say>être</say>* through every person. **Say it out loud, five times in a row, no pauses.** If you stumble, start over.

### Scale 1 — Affirmative

> <say>Je suis étudiant.</say>
> <say>Tu es étudiant.</say>
> <say>Il est étudiant.</say>

## Language matrix

The principle: **the answer is already inside the question**. Don't compose from scratch — mirror it.

### Matrix 1 — you (sg.) / you (formal) / he / they

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Tu es américain ?</say> | <say>Oui, je suis américain.</say> |
| <say>Vous êtes américain ?</say> (formal sg.) | <say>Oui, je suis américain.</say> |
| <say>Il est américain ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, il est américain.</say> |
| <say>Elle est américaine ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, elle est américaine.</say> |
| <say>Vous êtes américains ?</say> (plural) | <say>Oui, nous sommes américains.</say> |
| <say>Ils sont américains ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, ils sont américains.</say> |

### Matrix 2 — profession (no article!)

| Question | Affirmative | Negative |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| <say>Tu es médecin ?</say> | <say>Oui, je suis médecin.</say> | <say>Non, je ne suis pas médecin.</say> |
| <say>Elle est étudiante ?</say> | <say>Oui, elle est étudiante.</say> | <say>Non, elle n'est pas étudiante.</say> |
| <say>Vous êtes professeur ?</say> | <say>Oui, je suis professeur.</say> | <say>Non, je ne suis pas professeur.</say> |
| <say>Ils sont ingénieurs ?</say> | <say>Oui, ils sont ingénieurs.</say> | <say>Non, ils ne sont pas ingénieurs.</say> |

### Matrix 3 — c'est vs il est

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Qui est-ce ?</say> (answer: Marie) | <say>C'est Marie.</say> |
| <say>Et Marie, qui est-elle ?</say> (answer: French, journalist) | <say>Elle est française. Elle est journaliste.</say> |
| <say>C'est un professeur ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, c'est un professeur.</say> (= <say>Oui, il est professeur.</say>) |
| <say>Ce sont des étudiants ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, ce sont des étudiants.</say> |

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
SUBJECT PRONOUNS:
  je (j')  — I            nous       — we
  tu       — you (inf.)   vous       — you (formal sg. + any plural)
  il       — he / it      ils        — they (m. or mixed)
  elle     — she / it     elles      — they (women / fem. only)
  on       — we (casual) / people / one — conjugates like il

ÊTRE — to be (drill like a mantra):
  je suis      [swee]      nous sommes  [som]
  tu es        [ay]        vous êtes    [voo-z-et]
  il est       [ay]        ils sont     [sohn]

NEGATION:  ne … pas
  Je ne suis pas anglais.
  Il n'est pas français.  (n' before a vowel)

THE BIG TRAP — no article with être + profession/nationality:
  ✅ Je suis professeur.       ❌ Je suis UN professeur.
  ✅ Elle est médecin.         ❌ Elle est UNE médecin.
  ✅ Il est américain.         ❌ Il est UN américain.

C'EST vs IL EST:
  C'EST + article / name / general → C'est un médecin. C'est Marie. C'est bon.
  IL/ELLE EST + adjective / profession (NO article) → Elle est médecin. Il est grand.
  Plural: CE SONT des étudiants.

NATIONALITY AGREEMENT (f. = m. + e, silent -e wakes the consonant):
  français → française   ("frahn-SAY" → "frahn-SAYZ")
  italien  → italienne   (double n)
  russe    → russe       (already ends in -e — invariable)
  Adjective (lowercase): il est français
  Noun for a person (capital): c'est un Français

COUNTRIES — PREPOSITIONS:
  f. (la France, -e ending) →  en France / de France
  m. (le Japon)             →  au Japon  / du Japon
  pl. (les États-Unis)      →  aux É.-U. / des É.-U.
  cities (always)           →  à Paris, à Londres, à New York

MANDATORY LIAISON:
  vous_êtes [voo-z-et]   nous_sommes   ils_ont
  il est_américain [eel-ay-t-ah-may-ree-KAN]
```
