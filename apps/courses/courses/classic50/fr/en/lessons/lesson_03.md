# Lesson 3: Articles — definite, indefinite, partitive
**Vocabulary: Food and drinks, places in town, days of the week, months**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the three article systems and the contractions (5–7 minutes)
2. **Say it out loud** — every noun **with its article**, never bare
3. **Drill** — cycle through *un → le → du → pas de* until the switch is automatic

> In French, **a noun without an article is a grammatical error**. English happily says "I love bread" — in French you must say *<say>J'aime le pain</say>*. Always learn a French noun *with* its article: not *<say>pain</say>* but *<say>le pain</say>*.

---

## Part 1: Three article systems — map of the territory

English has a tiny article system: **the** (definite), **a / an** (indefinite singular), **some / any** (loosely, for plural and uncountable). French has three full systems, and you have to pick the right one **every time** you say a noun.

| You mean… | Use | Example | English equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| A specific thing / the whole class / an abstraction | **definite** *<say>le</say> / <say>la</say> / <say>l'</say> / <say>les</say>* | *<say>J'aime le pain.</say>* | I love bread (in general) |
| One countable item, any one | **indefinite** *<say>un</say> / <say>une</say> / <say>des</say>* | *<say>Je mange un pain.</say>* | I'm eating a (loaf of) bread |
| Some / a portion of (uncountable) | **partitive** *<say>du</say> / <say>de la</say> / <say>de l'</say> / <say>des</say>* | *<say>Je mange du pain.</say>* | I'm eating (some) bread |

> **The big shock:** English uses "bread" with no article for both general statements ("I love bread") and partial amounts ("I'm eating bread"). French splits these two meanings into **two completely different articles** — *<say>le pain</say>* vs *<say>du pain</say>*. Same English word, two French articles. The partitive (*<say>du</say>*) is the single biggest concept of this lesson and the one English speakers consistently forget.

---

## Part 2: The definite article *le / la / l' / les*

Used for a **specific** thing, the **whole class**, or an **abstraction**.

| Gender / number | Article | Example | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|---|
| masculine sg. | **<say>le</say>** | <say>le livre</say>, <say>le chat</say> | "luh" |
| feminine sg. | **<say>la</say>** | <say>la table</say>, <say>la voiture</say> | "lah" |
| before a vowel / mute *h* | **<say>l'</say>** | <say>l'ami</say>, <say>l'eau</say>, <say>l'hôtel</say> | élision! |
| plural (both genders) | **<say>les</say>** | <say>les livres</say>, <say>les amis</say> | "lay" / "lay-z" (liaison) |

**When English and French agree** (definite, specific item):

- *<say>Ferme la porte.</say>* — Close **the** door. (the specific door we both know)
- *<say>Où est le chat ?</say>* — Where is **the** cat?

**When English drops the article but French keeps it** — this is the trap:

- *<say>J'aime les chats.</say>* — I love cats. (whole class — English drops "the", French keeps *<say>les</say>*)
- *<say>La liberté est importante.</say>* — Freedom is important. (abstraction — English drops "the")
- *<say>Je déteste le lundi.</say>* — I hate Mondays. (general)
- *<say>Je travaille le lundi.</say>* — I work on Mondays. (= every Monday)

> **Memory hack for English speakers:** if your English sentence has **no article** but the French sentence is talking about a category, an abstraction, or "X in general" — French will use **<say>le</say> / <say>la</say> / <say>les</say>**. "Life is short" → *<say>La vie est courte.</say>* "Children love sweets" → *<say>Les enfants aiment les bonbons.</say>*

> **Callback to Lesson 2:** the article shows the gender. If you don't remember the gender of a word — **learn it with the article**, not on its own. Not *<say>table</say>*, but *<say>la table</say>*. Not *<say>livre</say>*, but *<say>le livre</say>*.

## Part 3: The indefinite article *un / une / des*

"A / an / some" for **countable** nouns. One item, or several unspecified items.

| Gender / number | Article | Example |
|---|---|---|
| masculine sg. | **<say>un</say>** | <say>un livre</say>, <say>un ami</say> |
| feminine sg. | **<say>une</say>** | <say>une table</say>, <say>une amie</say> |
| plural | **<say>des</say>** | <say>des livres</say>, <say>des amis</say> |

**Pronunciation traps:**

- **<say>un</say>** — nasal "uhn", liaison before a vowel: *<say>un ami</say>* → "uhn-nah-MEE"
- **<say>une</say>** — "ewn" (NOT nasal — the *e* after the *n* kills the nasal effect)
- **<say>des</say>** — "day", liaison before a vowel: *<say>des amis</say>* → "day-zah-MEE"

> **English has no plural "a"** — French does. *<say>Des livres</say>* literally means "some books / a few books", but it's **required** even when English would just say "books". You cannot say *<say>Je mange pommes</say>* — only *<say>Je mange des pommes</say>* ("I'm eating [some] apples"). Treat *<say>des</say>* as the plural of *<say>un</say> / <say>une</say>* — it must be there.

---

## Part 4: The partitive *du / de la / de l' / des* — the big new concept

This is the article English doesn't really have. **Master it now**, or you'll be making errors for years.

**The idea:** "some of", "a portion of", "an unspecified amount of". Used for **uncountable** things (water, bread, meat, time, luck, patience) and for **abstractions treated as substance**.

| Gender / number | Article | Example | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| masculine | **<say>du</say>** | <say>du pain</say>, <say>du vin</say> | (some) bread, wine |
| feminine | **<say>de la</say>** | <say>de la viande</say>, <say>de la patience</say> | (some) meat, patience |
| before a vowel / mute *h* | **<say>de l'</say>** | <say>de l'eau</say>, <say>de l'huile</say> | (some) water, oil |
| plural | **<say>des</say>** | <say>des fruits</say>, <say>des légumes</say> | (some) fruit, vegetables |

**The closest English equivalent is "some":**

- *<say>Je veux du pain.</say>* — I want **some** bread.
- *<say>Il boit de l'eau.</say>* — He's drinking **some** water.
- *<say>Elle a de la patience.</say>* — She has **some** patience / She has patience.

**But here's the catch:** English usually **drops "some"** in normal speech. "I want bread", "He's drinking water", "She has patience" all sound perfectly natural without "some". French does **not** allow this drop — the partitive is mandatory.

| English (article often dropped) | French (article required) |
|---|---|
| I'm eating bread. | *<say>Je mange du pain.</say>* |
| He drinks coffee. | *<say>Il boit du café.</say>* |
| She has courage. | *<say>Elle a du courage.</say>* |
| We're buying meat. | *<say>Nous achetons de la viande.</say>* |

**Compare these three sentences side by side — this is the heart of the lesson:**

| Sentence | Meaning |
|---|---|
| *<say>J'aime le café.</say>* | I love coffee. (in general, as a category) |
| *<say>Je commande un café.</say>* | I'm ordering a coffee. (one cup, a unit) |
| *<say>Je bois du café.</say>* | I'm drinking (some) coffee. (an amount of the substance) |

**The verb usually picks the article for you:**

- After *<say>aimer</say>, <say>adorer</say>, <say>détester</say>, <say>préférer</say>* → **definite** (you love/hate the *whole category*): *<say>J'aime le chocolat.</say>*
- After *<say>manger</say>, <say>boire</say>, <say>prendre</say>, <say>vouloir</say>, <say>acheter</say>* → **partitive** (you're consuming *some of* it): *<say>Je mange du chocolat.</say>*
- After a number or counting word → **indefinite** or no article: *<say>Je veux un café.</say>* / *<say>Je veux deux cafés.</say>*

> **Pronunciation:** *<say>du</say>* = "dew" (round your lips for "oo", then say "ee" — same rounded *u* sound as in *<say>tu</say>* from Lesson 1). *<say>de la</say>* = "duh-lah". *<say>de l'</say>* = "duh-l".

> **The biggest mistake English speakers make:** dropping the partitive because English drops "some". *<say>Je mange pain</say>* is **wrong**. *<say>Je mange du pain</say>* is right. Train your mouth: never let a food/drink word leave your mouth without an article in front of it.

## Part 5: Contractions with *à* and *de*

French refuses to say *<say>à le</say>* or *<say>de le</say>* — those two combinations **always contract**. The feminine and élision forms (*<say>à la</say>, <say>à l'</say>, <say>de la</say>, <say>de l'</say>*) stay as they are.

| Combination | Becomes | Example | English |
|---|---|---|---|
| à + le | **<say>au</say>** | *<say>Je vais au cinéma.</say>* | I'm going to the cinema. |
| à + les | **<say>aux</say>** | *<say>Je parle aux enfants.</say>* | I'm talking to the children. |
| à + la | *<say>à la</say>* (no change) | *<say>Je vais à la plage.</say>* | I'm going to the beach. |
| à + l' | *<say>à l'</say>* (no change) | *<say>Je vais à l'école.</say>* | I'm going to school. |
| de + le | **<say>du</say>** | *<say>Je reviens du marché.</say>* | I'm coming back from the market. |
| de + les | **<say>des</say>** | *<say>Le livre des enfants.</say>* | The children's book. |
| de + la | *<say>de la</say>* (no change) | *<say>Je viens de la gare.</say>* | I'm coming from the station. |
| de + l' | *<say>de l'</say>* (no change) | *<say>L'eau de l'océan.</say>* | The water of the ocean. |

> **Heads up:** *<say>du</say>* and *<say>des</say>* look identical in three different roles:
> - **partitive** *<say>du</say> / <say>des</say>*: *<say>du pain</say>* (some bread), *<say>des fruits</say>* (some fruit)
> - **contraction** *<say>de + le → du</say>* / *<say>de + les → des</say>*: *<say>du marché</say>* (from the market), *<say>des enfants</say>* (of the children)
> - **indefinite plural** *<say>des</say>*: *<say>des amis</say>* (some friends / friends)
>
> Context disambiguates. The forms are the same; don't panic.

---

## Part 6: Negation — almost all articles collapse to *de*

The French oddity: after negation *<say>ne … pas</say>*, the indefinite and partitive articles **all become *<say>de</say>*** (or *<say>d'</say>* before a vowel). The thing you don't have / don't eat / don't drink — there's none of it, so French marks it with a stripped-down *<say>de</say>*.

| Affirmative | Negative |
|---|---|
| *<say>Je mange du pain.</say>* | *<say>Je ne mange pas de pain.</say>* |
| *<say>J'ai une voiture.</say>* | *<say>Je n'ai pas de voiture.</say>* |
| *<say>Il y a des pommes.</say>* | *<say>Il n'y a pas de pommes.</say>* |
| *<say>Je bois de l'eau.</say>* | *<say>Je ne bois pas d'eau.</say>* |

**BUT** the definite article **does not change** (because you're still talking about the *category*, whether you like it or not):

| Affirmative | Negative |
|---|---|
| *<say>J'aime le café.</say>* | *<say>Je n'aime pas le café.</say>* |
| *<say>Je vois les enfants.</say>* | *<say>Je ne vois pas les enfants.</say>* |

**Exception:** after the verb *<say>être</say>* (to be), the article **does not change**:

- *<say>C'est un chat.</say>* → *<say>Ce n'est pas un chat.</say>* (NOT *<say>pas de chat</say>*)

> **Mnemonic:** "No bread" = *<say>pas de pain</say>*. No → *<say>de</say>*. End of story (except after *<say>être</say>* and except for definite articles, which stay put).

To say "from" a place, use *<say>de</say> + article*: *<say>de la boulangerie</say>, <say>du marché</say>, <say>de l'école</say>, <say>des toilettes</say>*. This is where you'll use contractions every single sentence.

### Days of the week (all masculine, no capital letter!)

| French | English |
|---|---|
| <say>lundi</say> | Monday |
| <say>mardi</say> | Tuesday |
| <say>mercredi</say> | Wednesday |
| <say>jeudi</say> | Thursday |
| <say>vendredi</say> | Friday |
| <say>samedi</say> | Saturday |
| <say>dimanche</say> | Sunday |

> **English-speaker traps:**
> - Days are **not capitalized** in French. *<say>lundi</say>*, not *Lundi*.
> - The week starts on **Monday**, not Sunday.
> - *<say>Lundi</say>* alone = "(this) Monday". *<say>Le lundi</say>* = "on Mondays / every Monday". The article changes the meaning: *<say>Je travaille lundi</say>* (I'm working this Monday) vs *<say>Je travaille le lundi</say>* (I work on Mondays).

### Months (all masculine, no capital letter!)

| <say>janvier</say> | <say>février</say> | <say>mars</say> | <say>avril</say> | <say>mai</say> | <say>juin</say> |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| January | February | March | April | May | June |

| <say>juillet</say> | <say>août</say> | <say>septembre</say> | <say>octobre</say> | <say>novembre</say> | <say>décembre</say> |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| July | August | September | October | November | December |

> **"In January" = *<say>en janvier</say>*** (NOT *<say>au janvier</say>*, NOT *<say>dans janvier</say>*). For months, French uses *<say>en</say>*, not *<say>au</say>* or *<say>dans</say>*. Same with seasons except spring: *<say>en été</say>, <say>en automne</say>, <say>en hiver</say>,* but *<say>au printemps</say>*.

## Language matrix: the verb picks the article

Question → answer. Notice which article follows which verb.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| *<say>Tu aimes le café ?</say>* | *<say>Oui, j'aime le café.</say>* (aimer → definite) |
| *<say>Tu bois du café ?</say>* | *<say>Oui, je bois du café.</say>* (boire → partitive) |
| *<say>Tu veux un café ?</say>* | *<say>Oui, je veux un café.</say>* (one cup → indefinite) |
| *<say>Tu manges de la viande ?</say>* | *<say>Non, je ne mange pas de viande.</say>* |
| *<say>Il y a des pommes ?</say>* | *<say>Non, il n'y a pas de pommes.</say>* |
| *<say>Vous avez une voiture ?</say>* | *<say>Non, je n'ai pas de voiture.</say>* |
| *<say>C'est un chat ?</say>* | *<say>Non, ce n'est pas un chat.</say>* (être — no change!) |
| *<say>Tu vas au marché ?</say>* | *<say>Oui, je vais au marché.</say>* |
| *<say>Tu viens de l'école ?</say>* | *<say>Oui, je viens de l'école.</say>* |

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
THREE ARTICLE SYSTEMS:

  DEFINITE (specific / whole class / abstraction):
    m. sg.        le pain
    f. sg.        la pomme
    before vowel  l'eau, l'hôtel
    plural        les amis

  INDEFINITE (a / an / some countable):
    m. sg.        un livre
    f. sg.        une table
    plural        des amis

  PARTITIVE (some / a portion of):
    m.            du pain
    f.            de la viande
    before vowel  de l'eau, de l'huile
    plural        des légumes

VERB PICKS THE ARTICLE:
  aimer/adorer/détester/préférer → definite   (J'aime LE café)
  manger/boire/prendre/vouloir   → partitive  (Je bois DU café)
  counting / a single unit       → indefinite (Je veux UN café)

CONTRACTIONS:
  à + le  = au         (au cinéma)
  à + les = aux        (aux enfants)
  de + le = du         (du marché)
  de + les = des       (des enfants)
  à/de + la, l'         — NO CONTRACTION

NEGATION:
  un / une / des / du / de la / de l'  →  PAS DE  (pas d' before vowel)
  le / la / les                        →  unchanged
  after être (c'est un…)               →  unchanged

THREE "DES" (same form, three roles):
  des amis      = indefinite plural
  des fruits    = partitive plural
  des enfants (livre des enfants) = de + les contraction

A NOUN WITHOUT AN ARTICLE = A MISTAKE.
Learn every noun WITH its article: le livre, la table, l'eau.

DAYS AND MONTHS (no capitals!):
  lundi, mardi, mercredi, jeudi, vendredi, samedi, dimanche
  le lundi = on Mondays
  en janvier, en mars … (NOT "au janvier"!)
```
