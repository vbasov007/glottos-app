# Lesson 9: -IR verbs. The verb aller. Futur proche
**Vocabulary: Travel, movement, transport, countries**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** the rules — get the logic of the two -IR "families" and the irregular *aller* (5 minutes)
2. **Run the scales** — conjugate each verb out loud through all 6 persons, no pauses
3. **Catch the contrast** — *je finis* (present) ↔ *je vais finir* (futur proche). One form, one job.

> *<say>Aller</say>* isn't just "to go." It's a **time machine**: *<say>je vais</say> + infinitive* = "I'm going to do it" in the near future. You get a whole future tense for free, without learning new endings.

---

## Part 1: Regular -IR verbs (the *finir* family)

The second big "regular" group. Smaller than -ER, but the members are high-frequency: *<say>finir</say>, <say>choisir</say>, <say>réussir</say>, <say>grandir</say>, <say>obéir</say>, <say>remplir</say>, <say>réfléchir</say>*.

**The signature feature:** in the plural, French inserts **-iss-** into the stem. That's the badge of the group — and the only way to tell these verbs apart from the *other* -IR group (we'll meet that one below).

### Conjugating **finir** (to finish)

| Person | Form | Pronunciation |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | <say>finis</say> | "fee-NEE" |
| tu | <say>finis</say> | "fee-NEE" |
| il / elle | <say>finit</say> | "fee-NEE" |
| nous | <say>finissons</say> | "fee-nee-SOHN" |
| vous | <say>finissez</say> | "fee-nee-SAY" |
| ils / elles | <say>finissent</say> | "fee-NEESS" |

**The endings:** -is, -is, -it, -issons, -issez, -issent.

> **Pronunciation trap.** All three singular forms sound **identical**: *<say>je finis</say> = <say>tu finis</say> = <say>il finit</say>* → "fee-NEE". The final `-s` and `-t` are silent (the CaReFuL rule from Lesson 1 doesn't cover `s` or `t`). In speech you tell the persons apart only by the pronoun in front.

### Conjugating **choisir** (to choose)

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| <say>je choisis</say> | <say>tu choisis</say> |
| <say>il choisit</say> | <say>nous choisissons</say> |
| <say>vous choisissez</say> | <say>ils choisissent</say> |

### Other verbs in this family

| Verb | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| <say>réussir</say> | to succeed, to pass (an exam) | *<say>Je réussis l'examen.</say>* — I pass the exam. |
| <say>grandir</say> | to grow up | *<say>Les enfants grandissent vite.</say>* — Kids grow up fast. |
| <say>réfléchir</say> | to think, to reflect | *<say>Nous réfléchissons.</say>* — We're thinking it over. |
| <say>obéir (à)</say> | to obey | *<say>Tu obéis à ta mère.</say>* — You obey your mother. |
| <say>remplir</say> | to fill | *<say>Il remplit le verre.</say>* — He fills the glass. |
| <say>choisir</say> | to choose | *<say>Vous choisissez ?</say>* — Are you choosing? |

> **English-speaker hack:** several of these have transparent English cognates — *<say>finir</say>* / finish, *<say>choisir</say>* / choice, *<say>réussir</say>* / "re-succeed", *<say>remplir</say>* / replenish. Lean on the family resemblance.

## Part 2: False friends — -IR verbs WITHOUT -iss-

Not every verb ending in -IR is regular. There's a second, **irregular** -IR group: *<say>partir</say>, <say>sortir</say>, <say>dormir</say>, <say>sentir</say>, <say>servir</say>, <say>courir</say>, <say>venir</say>, <say>tenir</say>, <say>ouvrir</say>, <say>offrir</say>*. They look the same in the infinitive but conjugate differently.

> **The test:** if the *<say>nous</say>* form has **no -iss-**, the verb is **not** a regular -IR. It belongs to the irregular -IR family and you have to learn it separately.

| Group | nous-form | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Regular (*<say>finir</say>*) | <say>nous finissons</say> | *<say>choisir</say>, <say>réussir</say>, <say>grandir</say>, <say>remplir</say>* |
| Irregular (*<say>partir</say>*) | <say>nous partons</say> (no -iss-) | *<say>sortir</say>, <say>dormir</say>, <say>sentir</say>, <say>servir</say>* |

**Conjugation of *partir* (to leave)** — useful in this lesson for travel:

| <say>je pars</say> | <say>tu pars</say> | <say>il part</say> |
|---------|---------|---------|
| <say>nous partons</say> | <say>vous partez</say> | <say>ils partent</say> |

> **Trap.** *<say>Je finis</say>* (regular) vs *<say>je pars</say>* (irregular). The singular looks similar, but the plural splits them: *<say>finissons</say>* vs *<say>partons</say>*. Same with *<say>sors</say>, <say>dors</say>, <say>sers</say>* — singular forms drop the last consonant of the stem.

## Part 3: The verb **aller** — the king of irregulars

*<say>Aller</say>* means "to go" (on foot, by car, by plane — French doesn't care, it's one verb). But it's the **most irregular** verb in French. Just learn it cold.

| Person | Form | Pronunciation |
|--------|------|---------------|
| je | <say>vais</say> | "vay" |
| tu | <say>vas</say> | "vah" |
| il / elle | <say>va</say> | "vah" |
| nous | <say>allons</say> | "ah-LOHN" |
| vous | <say>allez</say> | "ah-LAY" |
| ils / elles | <say>vont</say> | "vohn" |

> **Memorize the rhythm:** *<say>vais, vas, va</say> — <say>allons, allez, vont</say>*. The stem changes twice: `v-` in the singular and *<say>vont</say>*, `all-` in *<say>nous</say>/<say>vous</say>*. There's literally nothing of the infinitive "<say>aller</say>" left in the singular — it's that irregular.

> **English parallel:** *<say>aller</say>* is doing what English *to be* does — completely suppletive (go / went / gone are three unrelated roots, same with *<say>aller</say> / <say>vais</say> / <say>irai</say>*). When a verb is this central, languages love to mangle it.

### Aller + place

After *<say>aller</say>* you almost always see a **preposition of place** (see Part 5):

- *<say>Je vais à Paris.</say>* — I'm going to Paris.
- *<say>Nous allons au cinéma.</say>* — We're going to the movies.
- *<say>Ils vont en France.</say>* — They're going to France.

### Aller for "how are you"

Remember from Lesson 1: *<say>Comment ça va ?</say>* — that's *<say>aller</say>*. Literally "How does it go?" *<say>Ça va bien</say>* = "It goes well." French uses *<say>aller</say>*, where English uses *to be doing*.

## Part 4: Futur proche — future tense, no homework

The easiest way to talk about the future in French: **aller (in present tense) + infinitive**. Used for **imminent** or **planned** actions. This is **identical** to the English "going to" future:

> *I am **going to** eat* = *<say>Je vais manger</say>*.

Same logic, same structure, same use. If you can say "I'm going to do it" in English, you already speak futur proche.

### The formula

> **[aller in present] + [infinitive of the action verb] = futur proche**

| Person | Example | Translation |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| je | <say>je vais parler</say> | I'm going to speak |
| tu | <say>tu vas finir</say> | you're going to finish |
| il / elle | <say>elle va partir</say> | she's going to leave |
| nous | <say>nous allons manger</say> | we're going to eat |
| vous | <say>vous allez choisir</say> | you're going to choose |
| ils / elles | <say>ils vont voyager</say> | they're going to travel |

### Negation

*<say>ne … pas</say>* wraps **only** *<say>aller</say>*, never the infinitive:

- *<say>Je ne vais pas travailler demain.</say>* — I'm not going to work tomorrow.
- *<say>Nous n'allons pas partir.</say>* — We're not going to leave.

The infinitive stays naked outside the *<say>ne … pas</say>* sandwich. English speakers sometimes try to put "pas" after the infinitive — don't. *<say>Aller</say>* is the only conjugated verb in the sentence; negation always grips the conjugated verb.

### Present vs futur proche

| Now (présent) | Soon (futur proche) |
|---------------|---------------------|
| *<say>Je mange.</say>* — I'm eating. | *<say>Je vais manger.</say>* — I'm going to eat. |
| *<say>Tu choisis ?</say>* — Are you choosing? | *<say>Tu vas choisir ?</say>* — Are you going to choose? |
| *<say>Nous partons.</say>* — We're leaving. | *<say>Nous allons partir.</say>* — We're going to leave. |

> **Futur proche time markers:** *<say>bientôt</say>* (soon), *<say>demain</say>* (tomorrow), *<say>ce soir</say>* (tonight), *<say>la semaine prochaine</say>* (next week), *<say>dans deux heures</say>* (in two hours), *<say>tout à l'heure</say>* (later today).

> **Why "proche"?** *<say>Proche</say>* means "near, close." There's also a *<say>futur simple</say>* (one-word future, like *<say>je parlerai</say>*) that comes in Lesson 27. For now, *<say>futur proche</say>* covers 80% of everyday future talk — including planned events years from now: *<say>Je vais me marier en 2030</say>* is perfectly fine.

## Part 5: Place prepositions — à, en, au, aux

This is the headache of every beginner. The logic depends on **gender and number** of the country/place.

### Cities — always `à`

- *<say>Je vais à Paris.</say>* — I'm going to Paris.
- *<say>Elle habite à Londres.</say>* — She lives in London.
- *<say>Nous allons à New York.</say>* — We're going to New York.

One preposition, no choices. *<say>À</say>* covers both "to" and "in" for cities — English splits them (*to Paris* / *in Paris*), French doesn't bother.

### Countries — pick by gender

| Country type | Gender | Preposition | Example |
|--------------|--------|-------------|---------|
| <say>la France</say>, <say>l'Italie</say>, <say>la Russie</say>, <say>l'Espagne</say>, <say>la Chine</say> | **feminine** (usually -e) | **<say>en</say>** | *<say>en France</say>, <say>en Italie</say>* |
| <say>le Japon</say>, <say>le Canada</say>, <say>le Portugal</say>, <say>le Brésil</say>, <say>le Maroc</say> | **masculine** | **<say>au</say>** | *<say>au Japon</say>, <say>au Canada</say>* |
| <say>les États-Unis</say>, <say>les Pays-Bas</say>, <say>les Philippines</say> | **plural** | **<say>aux</say>** | *<say>aux États-Unis</say>* |
| <say>Cuba</say>, <say>Israël</say>, <say>Chypre</say> (no article) | — | **<say>à</say>** | *<say>à Cuba</say>* |
| countries starting with a vowel — <say>l'Iran</say>, <say>l'Irak</say> | use **<say>en</say>** | **<say>en</say>** | *<say>en Iran</say>, <say>en Irak</say>* |

> **The 90% rule:** if a country ends in **-e**, it's feminine → use **<say>en</say>**. Exceptions to memorize: *<say>le Mexique</say>, <say>le Cambodge</say>, <say>le Mozambique</say>, <say>le Zimbabwe</say>* — masculine despite the -e.

> **The vowel override:** any country starting with a vowel takes **<say>en</say>**, regardless of gender. *<say>L'Iran</say>* is masculine, but you still say *<say>en Iran</say>* (not "au Iran" — that vowel clash is forbidden). Same for *<say>en Afghanistan</say>, <say>en Angola</say>*.

### Continents — always `en` (they're all feminine)

*<say>en Europe</say>, <say>en Asie</say>, <say>en Afrique</say>, <say>en Amérique</say>, <say>en Australie</say>*

### Places in a city

| Place | With preposition | Translation |
|-------|------------------|-------------|
| <say>le cinéma</say> | <say>au cinéma</say> | to/at the movies |
| <say>la gare</say> | <say>à la gare</say> | to/at the train station |
| <say>l'aéroport</say> | <say>à l'aéroport</say> | to/at the airport |
| <say>les toilettes</say> | <say>aux toilettes</say> | to the restroom |
| <say>la maison</say> | <say>à la maison</say> | home, at home |
| <say>le travail</say> | <say>au travail</say> | to/at work |
| <say>l'école</say> | <say>à l'école</say> | to/at school |

> **Contractions to memorize:** `à + le = au`, `à + les = aux`. `à + la` and `à + l'` do **not** contract (the vowel makes contraction unnecessary). This is exactly the same logic as Lesson 3's *<say>du</say> / <say>de la</say> / <say>des</say>*.

### "From" — preposition `de`

The mirror pair: "to" → "from."

| To | From |
|----|------|
| <say>à Paris</say> | <say>de Paris</say> |
| <say>en France</say> | <say>de France</say> |
| <say>au Japon</say> | <say>du Japon</say> |
| <say>aux États-Unis</say> | <say>des États-Unis</say> |

- *<say>Je viens de Russie.</say>* — I come from Russia.
- *<say>Il rentre du Canada.</say>* — He's coming back from Canada.
- *<say>Le train arrive des Pays-Bas.</say>* — The train arrives from the Netherlands.

Same contraction rule: *<say>de + le = du</say>*, *<say>de + les = des</say>*.

### Verbs of movement and travel

| French | English | Note |
|--------|---------|------|
| <say>aller</say> | to go | irreg., vais/vas/va |
| <say>venir</say> | to come | irreg., viens/vient (Lesson 10) |
| <say>partir</say> | to leave, depart | <say>partir de</say> + place |
| <say>arriver</say> | to arrive | -ER, regular |
| <say>rentrer</say> | to come back home | -ER |
| <say>voyager</say> | to travel | -ER, *<say>nous voyageons</say>* (extra -e-) |
| <say>visiter</say> | to visit (a place) | NOT used for people! |
| <say>sortir</say> | to go out | irreg., sors/sort |
| <say>entrer</say> | to enter | + dans |
| <say>monter</say> | to go up; to get on (transport) | *<say>monter dans le train</say>* |

| <say>traverser</say> | to cross | *<say>traverser la rue</say>* |

> **Trap:** *<say>visiter</say>* is for places, not people. "I'm visiting my grandmother" is **not** *<say>je visite ma grand-mère</say>* (that sounds like a museum visit). Use *<say>je rends visite à ma grand-mère</say>* or just *<say>je vais chez ma grand-mère</say>*.

| <say>la campagne</say> (f.) | countryside |

| <say>la mer</say> (f.) | sea |

### Transport

| French | English | With preposition |
|--------|---------|------------------|
| <say>le train</say> (m.) | train | <say>en train</say> |
| <say>la voiture</say> (f.) | car | <say>en voiture</say> |
| <say>l'avion</say> (m.) | airplane | <say>en avion</say> |
| <say>le bus</say> (m.) | bus | <say>en bus</say> |
| <say>le métro</say> (m.) | metro/subway | <say>en métro</say> |
| <say>le vélo</say> (m.) | bike | <say>à vélo</say> |
| <say>le taxi</say> (m.) | taxi | <say>en taxi</say> |
| <say>à pied</say> | on foot | — |

> **Transport rule:** "inside" (enclosed) vehicles → **<say>en</say>** (<say>en voiture</say>, <say>en avion</say>, <say>en bus</say>). "On" (open) vehicles or your own legs → **<say>à</say>** (<say>à vélo</say>, <say>à pied</say>, <say>à cheval</say>). Same intuition as English *in a car* vs *on a bike*.

## Language scales

Run each chain out loud, **no pauses**. The goal is reflex, not understanding — you already understand.

### Scale 1: *finir* in the present

> <say>Je finis le livre. Tu finis le livre. Il finit le livre. Nous finissons le livre. Vous finissez le livre. Ils finissent le livre.</say>

### Scale 2: *aller* in the present

> <say>Je vais à Paris. Tu vas à Paris. Elle va à Paris. Nous allons à Paris. Vous allez à Paris. Ils vont à Paris.</say>

### Scale 3: Futur proche with *voyager*

> <say>Je vais voyager. Tu vas voyager. Il va voyager. Nous allons voyager. Vous allez voyager. Elles vont voyager.</say>

### Scale 4: Negation in futur proche

> <say>Je ne vais pas partir. Tu ne vas pas partir. Il ne va pas partir. Nous n'allons pas partir. Vous n'allez pas partir. Ils ne vont pas partir.</say>

### Scale 5: Present ↔ futur proche contrast

> <say>Je choisis maintenant → Je vais choisir demain.</say>
> <say>Tu finis maintenant → Tu vas finir demain.</say>
> <say>Nous partons maintenant → Nous allons partir demain.</say>
> <say>Vous arrivez maintenant → Vous allez arriver demain.</say>

## Language matrix

Mirror principle: the answer is already inside the question. Run all three scenarios.

### Scenario A: you (tu)

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Tu vas où ce soir ?</say> | <say>Je vais au cinéma.</say> |
| <say>Tu pars quand ?</say> | <say>Je pars demain.</say> |
| <say>Tu finis à quelle heure ?</say> | <say>Je finis à six heures.</say> |
| <say>Tu choisis le train ou l'avion ?</say> | <say>Je choisis l'avion.</say> |
| <say>Tu vas voyager cet été ?</say> | <say>Oui, je vais voyager en Italie.</say> |

### Scenario B: you (vous, plural)

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Vous allez où ?</say> | <say>Nous allons à la gare.</say> |
| <say>Vous partez d'où ?</say> | <say>Nous partons de Paris.</say> |
| <say>Vous finissez le travail ?</say> | <say>Oui, nous finissons.</say> |
| <say>Vous allez prendre le bus ?</say> | <say>Non, nous allons prendre le métro.</say> |
| <say>Vous voyagez en voiture ?</say> | <say>Non, nous voyageons en train.</say> |

### Scenario C: third persons (il / elle / ils)

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Où va Marc ?</say> | <say>Il va au Japon.</say> |
| <say>D'où vient Sophie ?</say> | <say>Elle vient de France.</say> |
| <say>Les enfants finissent l'école ?</say> | <say>Oui, ils finissent à trois heures.</say> |
| <say>Elles vont rentrer ce soir ?</say> | <say>Non, elles vont rentrer demain.</say> |
| <say>Pierre choisit quoi ?</say> | <say>Il choisit le bus.</say> |

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
REGULAR -IR VERBS (FINIR family):
  je finis        nous finiSSons
  tu finis        vous finiSSez
  il finit        ils finiSSent

  Endings: -is, -is, -it, -issons, -issez, -issent
  ID tag: the nous-form carries -ISS-.
  No -ISS- = irregular (partir, sortir, dormir).
  Trap: je finis = tu finis = il finit (all sound the same).

ALLER (irregular — memorize):
  je vais    nous allons
  tu vas     vous allez
  il va      ils vont

FUTUR PROCHE = aller (present) + INFINITIVE:
  Je vais manger.        — I'm going to eat.
  Nous allons partir.    — We're going to leave.
  Negation: Je NE vais PAS manger. (pas hugs aller, not the infinitive)
  = English "going to" future, same logic.

PLACE PREPOSITIONS:
  City             → à        (à Paris, à New York)
  Country fem.     → en       (en France, en Italie, en Russie)
  Country masc.    → au       (au Japon, au Canada, au Brésil)
  Country plural   → aux      (aux États-Unis, aux Pays-Bas)
  Country vowel    → en       (en Iran, en Irak, en Angola)
  Continent        → en       (en Europe, en Asie)

  à + le = AU,  à + les = AUX
  à + la = à la, à + l' = à l' (no contraction)

  "From":
    de Paris / de France / DU Japon / DES États-Unis

TRANSPORT:
  Enclosed → EN  (en voiture, en train, en avion, en bus, en métro)
  Open     → À   (à pied, à vélo, à cheval)

FUTUR PROCHE TIME MARKERS:
  demain, bientôt, ce soir, la semaine prochaine, dans deux heures,
  tout à l'heure
```
