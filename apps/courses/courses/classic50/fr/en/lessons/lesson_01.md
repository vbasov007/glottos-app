# Lesson 1: Reading French. Numbers 0–20
**Vocabulary: Alphabet, pronunciation, greetings**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — slowly, consciously, analyzing every sound
3. **Speed up** — repeat until the phrases fly out on their own

> Knowing the rule = 5%. Training your mouth = 95%.
> French is a sport for the tongue and lips. Drop English articulation completely — French uses muscles English never asks for.

---

## Part 1: The one rule that changes everything

French is **not written the way it's read**. Unlike German or Spanish, there are lots of "extra" letters that look important and stay completely silent. Good news: the rules are real and you only need a handful. Bad news: you have to accept them without "why".

**The single biggest rule:**

> Final consonants are usually **silent**. Especially `-s`, `-t`, `-d`, `-x`, `-z`, `-p`.

- *<say>Paris</say>* → "pah-REE" (silent s)
- *<say>petit</say>* → "puh-TEE" (silent t)
- *<say>trop</say>* → "troh" (silent p)
- *<say>chez</say>* → "shay" (silent z)
- *<say>deux</say>* → "duh" (silent x)

English has silent letters too — *knight*, *lamb*, *psalm*, *write* — but French does it more systematically. Once you accept "the last consonant is dead", a huge percentage of French spelling stops scaring you.

**The exception:** `c`, `r`, `f`, `l` usually **do** get pronounced at the end. Memorize the English word **"CaReFuL"** — those four consonants stay alive at word end.

- *<say>avec</say>* → "ah-VEK" (c sounds)
- *<say>bonjour</say>* → "bohn-ZHOOR" (r sounds)
- *<say>neuf</say>* → "nuhf" (f sounds)
- *<say>seul</say>* → "suhl" (l sounds)

## Part 2: Ten reading rules that cover 90% of French

| What you see | How you read it | Example |
|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| **ou** | "oo" (like English *food*) — always | *<say>bonjour</say>*, *<say>vous</say>*, *<say>nous</say>* |
| **u** (alone) | no English equivalent — round lips for "oo", then say "ee" | *<say>tu</say>*, *<say>une</say>*, *<say>salut</say>* |
| **eu / œu** | between "uh" and "eh" — round lips for "oh", then say "eh" | *<say>deux</say>*, *<say>peur</say>*, *<say>cœur</say>* |
| **oi** | "wah" | *<say>moi</say>* "mwah", *<say>toi</say>* "twah", *<say>trois</say>* "twah" |
| **au / eau** | "oh" | *<say>au revoir</say>*, *<say>beau</say>*, *<say>eau</say>* |
| **ai / ei** | "eh" (like English *bed*) | *<say>j'ai</say>*, *<say>seize</say>*, *<say>Seine</say>* |
| **ch** | "sh" (NOT "ch" like English *chair*) | *<say>chez</say>* "shay", *<say>chocolat</say>*, *<say>chien</say>* |
| **gn** | "ny" (like English *canyon*) | *<say>campagne</say>*, *<say>Espagne</say>* |
| **qu** | just "k" (the u is silent) | *<say>que</say>*, *<say>qui</say>*, *<say>pourquoi</say>* |
| **ille** | "ee" at word end | *<say>fille</say>* "fee", *<say>travaille</say>* "trah-VYE" |

> **The English-speaker's biggest reading mistake:** treating French vowels like English ones. French **a** = "ah" (like *father*), NEVER "ay". French **i** = "ee", NEVER "eye". French **e** at word end is usually silent. One letter, one sound — except where the rules above explicitly say otherwise.

## Part 3: Nasal vowels — the signature sound of French

This is **the** distinctive flavor of French. A nasal vowel happens when `m` or `n` follows a vowel and the air goes out through your nose instead of your mouth. The `m` or `n` itself **disappears** — it only "nasalizes" the vowel before it.

| What you see | How you read it (roughly) | Example |
|--------------|---------------------------|---------|
| **an / en** | nasal "ahn" (like English *aunt* with the n dropped) | *<say>France</say>*, *<say>enfant</say>*, *<say>temps</say>* |
| **on** | nasal "ohn" (round your lips for "oh", let air through nose) | *<say>bon</say>*, *<say>non</say>*, *<say>maison</say>* |
| **in / ain / ein** | nasal "ahn" (open mouth wider than for *on*) | *<say>vin</say>*, *<say>pain</say>*, *<say>plein</say>* |
| **un** | nasal between "uhn" and "ohn" | *<say>un</say>*, *<say>brun</say>* |

> **English-speaker trap:** do NOT pronounce the `n` like in English *bon* (with a hard n at the end). The n is part of the vowel — your tongue should never touch the roof of your mouth. Practice: say "song" but stop right before the "g". That nasal hum is what French wants.

> **Trap 2 — when nasal effect is cancelled:** if a vowel follows the n/m, or if it's doubled (nn/mm), the nasal effect dies and the n/m becomes a normal consonant: *<say>bonne</say>* → "bun" (normal n), *<say>ami</say>* → "ah-mee", *<say>femme</say>* → "fahm".

## Part 4: Accents — diacritics are new letters, not stress marks

Important: French accents are **not stress marks** like Spanish or Russian. They're separate letters with their own sound or function. Don't ignore them.

| Mark | Name | How to read it | Example |
|------|------|----------------|---------|
| **é** | accent aigu | closed "ay" — mouth narrow, like English *day* without the y-glide | *<say>café</say>*, *<say>écouter</say>*, *<say>été</say>* |
| **è** | accent grave | open "eh" — mouth wider, like English *bed* | *<say>père</say>*, *<say>mère</say>*, *<say>très</say>* |
| **ê** | accent circonflexe | long "eh"; usually marks a lost Latin s | *<say>fête</say>* (festival, was *feste*), *<say>forêt</say>* (forest), *<say>hôpital</say>* (hospital) |
| **ç** | cédille | makes c sound like "s" instead of "k" before a, o, u | *<say>ça</say>*, *<say>français</say>*, *<say>garçon</say>* |
| **ë / ï** | tréma | "read the two vowels separately" | *<say>Noël</say>* "no-EL", *<say>naïf</say>* "nah-EEF" |

The circumflex hack is a free vocabulary helper — words that have `ê` in French often had `s` in Old French and still do in English: *<say>fête</say>* ↔ feast, *<say>forêt</say>* ↔ forest, *<say>hôpital</say>* ↔ hospital, *<say>île</say>* ↔ isle, *<say>côte</say>* ↔ coast. Drop the circumflex, add an `s`, and you've often got the English cognate.

## Part 5: Liaison and élision — what makes French sound French

These are the two glue-ups that connect words. Without them, French sounds like a robot; with them, it sounds like France.

### Liaison — silent consonants wake up

A final consonant is usually silent. **But** if the next word starts with a vowel (or a silent h), that consonant comes back to life and connects to the next word.

- *<say>les amis</say>* → "lay-**z**ah-MEE" (the s came back as a z-sound)
- *<say>un homme</say>* → "**uhn**-OHM" (the n came back)
- *<say>vous avez</say>* → "voo-**z**ah-VAY"
- *<say>petit ami</say>* → "puh-tee-**t**ah-MEE" (the t came back)

English speakers already do this instinctively: "an apple" vs "a banana" — the n appears specifically to bridge a vowel. French does the same thing, more aggressively, with more letters.

**The "liaison zone"** — after these short words, the connection is automatic and required: *<say>les</say>, <say>des</say>, <say>mes</say>, <say>tes</say>, <say>ses</say>, <say>nos</say>, <say>vos</say>, <say>leurs</say>, <say>un</say>, <say>mon</say>, <say>ton</say>, <say>son</say>, <say>deux</say>, <say>trois</say>, <say>six</say>, <say>dix</say>, <say>vous</say>, <say>nous</say>, <say>est</say>, <say>sont</say>*.

### Élision — final vowels disappear before a vowel

If a small word ends in a vowel (usually `e`, `a`, or `i`) and the next word starts with a vowel, the final vowel of the first word **drops** and is replaced by an apostrophe.

- *<say>je</say>* + *<say>ai</say>* = **<say>j'ai</say>** (NOT "zhuh-ay")
- *<say>le</say>* + *<say>ami</say>* = **<say>l'ami</say>**
- *<say>la</say>* + *<say>école</say>* = **<say>l'école</say>**
- *<say>si</say>* + *<say>il</say>* = **<say>s'il</say>** (only before il/ils — never before other vowels)
- *<say>que</say>* + *<say>est</say>* = **<say>qu'est</say>**

This is **mandatory**, not optional. *<say>Le ami</say>* is a mistake. English contracts the same way — *can not* → *can't*, *I am* → *I'm* — except the French version is required, not just casual.

## Part 6: Numbers 0–20

### Numbers 0–10 — memorize cold

| 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| <say>zéro</say> | <say>un</say> | <say>deux</say> | <say>trois</say> | <say>quatre</say> | <say>cinq</say> |

| 6 | 7 | 8 | 9 | 10 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| <say>six</say> | <say>sept</say> | <say>huit</say> | <say>neuf</say> | <say>dix</say> |

**Pronunciation traps:**

- **<say>un</say>** — nasal "uhn" (NOT "oon" like Spanish *uno*)
- **<say>deux</say>** — "duh" (eu = round lips for "oh", say "eh")
- **<say>six</say>** — three pronunciations depending on context: "sees" at sentence end; "see" before a consonant (*<say>six livres</say>* → "see LEEVR"); "seez" before a vowel (*<say>six amis</say>* → "seez ah-MEE" — that's liaison!)
- **<say>sept</say>** — "set" (p is silent, t is pronounced)
- **<say>huit</say>** — "weet" (h is silent, "uit" sounds like "weet")
- **<say>neuf</say>** — usually "nuhf"; but before *<say>ans</say>* (years) and *<say>heures</say>* (hours), the **f becomes v**: *<say>neuf ans</say>* → "nuh-**v**-AHN", *<say>neuf heures</say>* → "nuh-**v**-UHR"
- **<say>dix</say>** — same three rules as six: "deess", "dee", "deez"

### Numbers 11–16 (their own words)

| 11 | 12 | 13 | 14 | 15 | 16 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| <say>onze</say> | <say>douze</say> | <say>treize</say> | <say>quatorze</say> | <say>quinze</say> | <say>seize</say> |

### Numbers 17–19 (compounds: 10 + digit)

| 17 | 18 | 19 |
|---|---|---|
| <say>dix-sept</say> | <say>dix-huit</say> | <say>dix-neuf</say> |

> **Trap!** French switches systems mid-stream: 11–16 are their own words, but 17–19 are literally "ten-seven, ten-eight, ten-nine". Don't try to keep going with the 11–16 pattern.

### Number 20

**<say>vingt</say>** → "vahn" (nasal; the gt is silent at the end, but liaison wakes it up: *<say>vingt ans</say>* → "vahn-**t**-AHN").

## Part 7: Vocabulary — greetings and basic politeness

### Greetings

| French | English | When to use |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| <say>Bonjour</say> | Hello / Good day | Standard daytime greeting, formal or casual |
| <say>Salut</say> | Hi / Bye | Informal, between friends |
| <say>Bonsoir</say> | Good evening | After roughly 6 p.m. |
| <say>Au revoir</say> | Goodbye | Standard goodbye, formal or casual |
| <say>Bonne nuit</say> | Good night | Only when going to bed (not "goodbye after dark") |
| <say>À bientôt</say> | See you soon | Casual |
| <say>À demain</say> | See you tomorrow | Casual |

### Essentials

| French | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>Oui</say> | Yes |
| <say>Non</say> | No |
| <say>Merci</say> | Thank you |
| <say>Merci beaucoup</say> | Thank you very much |
| <say>S'il vous plaît</say> | Please (formal) |
| <say>S'il te plaît</say> | Please (informal) |
| <say>De rien</say> | You're welcome |
| <say>Pardon</say> | Excuse me / Sorry |
| <say>Excusez-moi</say> | Excuse me (formal) |

### Introducing yourself

| French | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>Je m'appelle…</say> | My name is… (lit. "I call myself…") |
| <say>Comment t'appelles-tu ?</say> | What's your name? (informal) |
| <say>Comment vous appelez-vous ?</say> | What's your name? (formal) |
| <say>Enchanté</say> / <say>Enchantée</say> | Nice to meet you (add -e if you're a woman speaking) |
| <say>Comment ça va ?</say> | How are you? (informal) |
| <say>Comment allez-vous ?</say> | How are you? (formal) |
| <say>Ça va bien</say> | I'm doing well |
| <say>Ça va, et toi ?</say> | I'm fine, and you? (informal) |

> **The tu vs vous thing:** French has two "you"s. *<say>Tu</say>* is informal (friends, family, kids, anyone you'd call by first name). *<say>Vous</say>* is formal (strangers, anyone older or in authority, business). It's exactly like the old English thou/you distinction — except French still uses it daily. With adults you don't know, **always start with *<say>vous</say>***. They'll offer *<say>tu</say>* when appropriate ("<say>on peut se tutoyer</say>"). Getting this wrong sounds rude in a way English doesn't have an equivalent for.

## Lesson cheat sheet

- **Silent final consonants** — except CaReFuL (c, r, f, l)
- **ou = "oo"**, **u = (rounded "ee")**, **eu = (rounded "eh")**, **oi = "wah"**, **au/eau = "oh"**
- **ch = "sh"**, **qu = "k"**, **gn = "ny"**, **ille = "ee"**
- **Nasal vowels**: an/en/on/in/un — vowel only, no consonant sound
- **Accents**: é (closed "ay"), è (open "eh"), ê (lost-s marker), ç (s-sound), ¨ (read separately)
- **Liaison**: silent consonant wakes up before a vowel — *les_amis* "lay-zah-mee"
- **Élision**: vowel + vowel = apostrophe — *j'ai*, *l'ami*
- **CaReFuL** = c/r/f/l stay alive at word end
- **Numbers**: un, deux, trois, quatre, cinq, six, sept, huit, neuf, dix; onze, douze, treize, quatorze, quinze, seize; dix-sept, dix-huit, dix-neuf, vingt
- **tu** = informal you, **vous** = formal you. When in doubt, start with *vous*.
