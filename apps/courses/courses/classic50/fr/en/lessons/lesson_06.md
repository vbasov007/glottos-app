# Lesson 6: Adjectives — agreement and position
**Vocabulary: colors, size, character, appearance**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — slowly, consciously, running through all four forms
3. **Speed up** — repeat until the agreement endings fire automatically

> Knowing the rule = 5%. Training your mouth = 95%.
> Goal: when you see "small table" in your head, the `-e` (or its absence) should already be lit up before the noun arrives.

---

## Part 1: The two big differences from English

English adjectives are dead simple. *Small dog, small dogs, big house, big houses* — one form, always before the noun. French does the opposite on **both** counts:

**1. French adjectives agree with the noun** in gender (masc. / fem.) and number (sg. / pl.). One adjective, four possible forms.

**2. French adjectives mostly go AFTER the noun.** *<say>La maison blanche</say>* — "the house white". Only a small set of very common adjectives goes before — the **BAGS** group, below.

Both shifts feel weird at first. Both become automatic with drilling.

---

## Part 2: The agreement rules — four forms

The default pattern:

|              | Singular   | Plural      |
|--------------|------------|-------------|
| **Masculine** | <say>grand</say> | <say>grands</say> |
| **Feminine**  | <say>grande</say> | <say>grandes</say> |

**The base rule:**

> Feminine = + **e**. Plural = + **s**. Feminine plural = + **es**.

**Pronunciation — the main trap:**

- *<say>grand</say>* → "grahn" (silent d)
- *<say>grande</say>* → "grahnd" (the e wakes the d up!)
- *<say>grands</say>* → "grahn" (silent s — sounds like masculine singular)
- *<say>grandes</say>* → "grahnd" (sounds like feminine singular)

> **Listen for gender, not number.** *<say>Un grand chat</say>* and *<say>des grands chats</say>* sound identical — only the article tells you it's plural. But *<say>grand</say>* (masc.) and *<say>grande</say>* (fem.) sound clearly different — the final consonant comes alive in the feminine.

That silent-`e`-wakes-the-consonant pattern is your best friend: *<say>petit</say>* "puh-TEE" → *<say>petite</say>* "puh-TEET". *<say>vert</say>* "vehr" → *<say>verte</say>* "VEHRT". *<say>gris</say>* "gree" → *<say>grise</say>* "GREEZ".

---

## Part 3: When `-e` changes nothing

If a masculine adjective **already ends in a silent -e** (no accent), the feminine looks and sounds identical: *<say>jeune</say> / <say>jeune</say>*, *<say>rouge</say> / <say>rouge</say>*, *<say>jaune</say> / <say>jaune</say>*, *<say>triste</say> / <say>triste</say>*, *<say>sympathique</say> / <say>sympathique</say>*. The plural just adds a silent `-s`: *<say>jeunes</say>*, *<say>rouges</say>*.

---

## Part 4: Irregular feminine forms — the productive patterns

These endings repeat across many adjectives. Learn the pattern, not each word.

| Masc. ending | Feminine ending | Example |
|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| **-eux**     | **-euse**       | <say>heureux</say> → <say>heureuse</say> (happy) |
| **-eur**     | **-euse**       | <say>menteur</say> → <say>menteuse</say> (lying) |
| **-f**       | **-ve**         | <say>sportif</say> → <say>sportive</say>, <say>neuf</say> → <say>neuve</say> |
| **-er**      | **-ère**        | <say>cher</say> → <say>chère</say>, <say>premier</say> → <say>première</say> |
| **-on / -en / -el / -et** | **doubles the consonant + e** | <say>bon</say> → <say>bonne</say>, <say>italien</say> → <say>italienne</say>, <say>naturel</say> → <say>naturelle</say>, <say>muet</say> → <say>muette</say> |
| **-c**       | **-che** or **-que** | <say>blanc</say> → <say>blanche</say>, <say>public</say> → <say>publique</say> |
| **-x** (other) | **-sse**      | <say>faux</say> → <say>fausse</say>, <say>roux</say> → <say>rousse</say> |

**Fully irregular — memorize as pairs:**

| Masc. | Fem. | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| <say>beau</say> | <say>belle</say> | beautiful |
| <say>nouveau</say> | <say>nouvelle</say> | new |
| <say>vieux</say> | <say>vieille</say> | old |
| <say>blanc</say> | <say>blanche</say> | white |
| <say>frais</say> | <say>fraîche</say> | fresh |
| <say>long</say> | <say>longue</say> | long |
| <say>gentil</say> | <say>gentille</say> | nice, kind |
| <say>doux</say> | <say>douce</say> | soft, gentle |

> **English-speaker shortcut:** the `-eux → -euse` pattern is huge — it covers most "-ous" English cognates: *<say>fameux</say>/<say>fameuse</say>* (famous), *<say>curieux</say>/<say>curieuse</say>* (curious), *<say>sérieux</say>/<say>sérieuse</say>* (serious), *<say>furieux</say>/<say>furieuse</say>* (furious). Lock that pattern in early and you've covered dozens of words.

---

## Part 5: Plural — the traps

Default: + `s`. But:

| If masculine ends in… | Plural is… | Example |
|-----------------------|------------|---------|
| **-s, -x, -z**        | unchanged  | *<say>un gros chat</say>* → *<say>des gros chats</say>* |
| **-eau**              | + **x**    | *<say>beau</say>* → *<say>beaux</say>*, *<say>nouveau</say>* → *<say>nouveaux</say>* |
| **-al**               | → **-aux** | *<say>national</say>* → *<say>nationaux</say>*, *<say>normal</say>* → *<say>normaux</say>* |

Feminine plurals always just take regular `-s`: *<say>belles</say>*, *<say>nationales</say>*, *<say>grosses</say>*.

---

## Part 6: Position — adjective AFTER the noun (the default)

**Default rule:** the adjective comes **after** the noun.

- *<say>une voiture rouge</say>* — a red car (lit. "a car red")
- *<say>un homme intelligent</say>* — an intelligent man
- *<say>des chats noirs</say>* — black cats
- *<say>une femme française</say>* — a French woman

This is the opposite of English — not "red car" but "car red". Burn it in by saying it out loud; the rhythm is what you need to internalize. After-position is used for **all** colors, nationalities, shapes, and most descriptive adjectives.

---

## Part 7: BAGS adjectives — BEFORE the noun

A small but extremely high-frequency group sits **before** the noun. The English mnemonic is **BAGS**:

| Letter | Meaning | Adjectives |
|--------|---------|------------|
| **B** — Beauty   | beauty / appearance | <say>beau</say>, <say>joli</say> |
| **A** — Age      | age / newness | <say>jeune</say>, <say>vieux</say>, <say>nouveau</say> |
| **G** — Goodness | evaluation | <say>bon</say>, <say>mauvais</say>, <say>gentil</say> |
| **S** — Size     | size | <say>grand</say>, <say>petit</say>, <say>gros</say>, <say>long</say> |

Examples:
- *<say>une belle maison</say>* — a beautiful house
- *<say>un jeune homme</say>* — a young man
- *<say>un bon film</say>* — a good movie
- *<say>une petite table</say>* — a small table
- *<say>un grand livre</say>* — a big book

> **Trap!** Position can change meaning. *<say>Un grand homme</say>* (before) = "a great man". *<say>Un homme grand</say>* (after) = "a tall man". *<say>Un ancien professeur</say>* (before) = "a former teacher". *<say>Un professeur ancien</say>* (after) = "an old teacher". For now, just stick to the BAGS-before / everything-else-after split — you'll meet the meaning-shift cases in later lessons.

---

## Part 8: beau / nouveau / vieux — the fifth form

These three have a **special form** before a **masculine** noun starting with a **vowel** or **silent h**, to avoid two vowels colliding (same instinct as English *a apple* → *an apple*).

| M. before consonant | M. before vowel | Fem. | M. plural | F. plural |
|---------------------|-----------------|------|-----------|-----------|
| <say>beau</say> | **<say>bel</say>** | <say>belle</say> | <say>beaux</say> | <say>belles</say> |
| <say>nouveau</say> | **<say>nouvel</say>** | <say>nouvelle</say> | <say>nouveaux</say> | <say>nouvelles</say> |
| <say>vieux</say> | **<say>vieil</say>** | <say>vieille</say> | <say>vieux</say> | <say>vieilles</say> |

- *<say>un beau garçon</say>* / *<say>un bel homme</say>* / *<say>un bel arbre</say>*
- *<say>un nouvel ami</say>* / *<say>un vieil hôtel</say>*

The feminine already ends in a consonant, so no problem there: *<say>une belle amie</say>*.

> *<say>bel</say>*, *<say>nouvel</say>*, *<say>vieil</say>* sound identical to *<say>belle</say>*, *<say>nouvelle</say>*, *<say>vieille</say>*. The fifth form is just "use the feminine pronunciation, but keep the noun masculine".

---

## Part 9: Article `des` → `de` before a BAGS adjective

When a BAGS adjective sits **before** a **plural** noun, the indefinite article `des` shortens to `de`:

- *<say>des amis</say>* → *<say>de bons amis</say>* (good friends)
- *<say>des films</say>* → *<say>de grands films</say>* (big movies)
- *<say>des maisons</say>* → *<say>de belles maisons</say>* (beautiful houses)

If the adjective comes **after** the noun, `des` stays:
- *<say>des films intéressants</say>* (interesting movies)
- *<say>des yeux bleus</say>* (blue eyes)

> In casual spoken French you'll often hear *<say>des bons amis</say>* — the rule is slipping. But in writing and careful speech, stick with *<say>de bons amis</say>*.

## Part 11: Language scales — run it through four forms

Take an adjective and drill: M.sg → F.sg → M.pl → F.pl. Out loud.

**heureux:** *<say>un homme heureux</say> → <say>une femme heureuse</say> → <say>des hommes heureux</say> → <say>des femmes heureuses</say>*

**petit (BAGS — before):** *<say>un petit chat</say> → <say>une petite voiture</say> → <say>de petits chats</say> → <say>de petites voitures</say>* (des → de!)

**beau (BAGS + fifth form):** *<say>un beau livre</say> → <say>un bel arbre</say> (vowel!) → <say>une belle maison</say> → <say>de beaux livres</say> → <say>de belles maisons</say>*

**sportif (-f → -ve):** *<say>un garçon sportif</say> → <say>une fille sportive</say> → <say>des garçons sportifs</say> → <say>des filles sportives</say>*

> Feel the way the `-e` lights up the final consonant. That muscle memory is the whole point.

## Part 12: Language matrix — description via être and avoir

Connect this lesson to Lesson 4 (*être*) and Lesson 5 (*avoir*): *être* gives the trait, *avoir* gives the body part or feature.

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Comment est ton frère ?</say> | <say>Il est grand et sympa.</say> |
| <say>Comment sont tes amis ?</say> | <say>Ils sont sportifs et gentils.</say> |
| <say>Comment est ta sœur ?</say> | <say>Elle est petite et drôle.</say> |
| <say>Tu as les yeux de quelle couleur ?</say> | <say>J'ai les yeux verts.</say> |
| <say>Elle a les cheveux longs ?</say> | <say>Non, elle a les cheveux courts.</say> |
| <say>Il y a une nouvelle voiture ?</say> | <say>Oui, il y a une belle voiture rouge.</say> |

Run it with your own people — describe yourself, a friend, a family member.

## Part 13: Exercises

### Exercise 1. Put the adjective in the right form

Make the adjective agree with the noun:

1. une voiture (rouge) → …
2. un (beau) homme → …
3. des amis (sportif) → …
4. une fille (heureux) → …
5. des maisons (blanc) → …
6. un (vieux) ami → …
7. une (nouveau) idée → …
8. des chats (gris) → …
9. une histoire (long) → …
10. de (gentil) enfants → …

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *une voiture **rouge*** (no change — already ends in -e)
2. *un **bel** homme* (before a vowel — fifth form!)
3. *des amis **sportifs*** (just + s; -if stays -if)
4. *une fille **heureuse*** (-eux → -euse)
5. *des maisons **blanches*** (blanc → blanche → blanches)
6. *un **vieil** ami* (before a vowel!)
7. *une **nouvelle** idée* (irregular feminine)
8. *des chats **gris*** (already ends in -s, plural unchanged)
9. *une **longue** histoire* (BAGS — before; irregular fem.)
10. *de **gentils** enfants* (des → de before BAGS; gentil + s)

</details>

### Exercise 4. Spot the mistake

Each sentence has one error of agreement or position. Fix it:

1. *J'ai une voiture rouges.*
2. *C'est un beau homme.*
3. *Elle est sportif.*
4. *Des bons amis sont importants.*
5. *Mes parents sont vieilles.*
6. *Il a les cheveux noir.*
7. *Une fille française et grande.*

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *J'ai une voiture **rouge**.* (singular)
2. *C'est un **bel** homme.* (fifth form — before vowel)
3. *Elle est **sportive**.* (-f → -ve)
4. ***De** bons amis sont importants.* (des → de before BAGS)
5. *Mes parents sont **vieux**.* (M plural — mixed/default group is masculine)
6. *Il a les cheveux **noirs**.* (plural agreement)

</details>

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
FOUR FORMS:
              M.sg    F.sg     M.pl     F.pl
  grand   →   grand   grande   grands   grandes
              "grahn" "grahnd" "grahn"  "grahnd"

  F  = + e (wakes the final consonant)
  Pl = + s (silent)
  Already on -e → no change (jeune, rouge, triste)
  Already on -s/-x → unchanged in plural (gris, vieux)

IRREGULAR FEMININES:
  -eux → -euse   (heureux → heureuse, sérieux → sérieuse)
  -f   → -ve     (sportif → sportive)
  -er  → -ère    (cher → chère)
  -on/-en/-el/-et → double + e (bon → bonne, italien → italienne)
  -c   → -che    (blanc → blanche)
  PAIRS: beau/belle, nouveau/nouvelle, vieux/vieille, long/longue

FIFTH FORM (M.sg before vowel / silent h):
  beau → bel, nouveau → nouvel, vieux → vieil
  (un bel homme, un nouvel ami, un vieil hôtel)

PLURAL TRAPS:
  -eau → -eaux   |   -al → -aux   |   -s/-x/-z unchanged

POSITION:
  AFTER (default): colors, shapes, nationalities, most descriptions
    une voiture rouge, un homme intelligent
  BEFORE — BAGS:
    B — Beauty:   beau, joli
    A — Age:      jeune, vieux, nouveau
    G — Goodness: bon, mauvais, gentil
    S — Size:     grand, petit, gros, long

DES → DE before a BAGS adjective:
  de bons amis (before)  vs  des amis sympas (after)
```
