# Lesson 5: The Verb avoir
**Vocabulary: Personal items, body parts, physical state, age**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** the rule on *avoir* and its idioms (5 minutes).
2. **Conjugate aloud** all six forms ten times. *<say>Avoir</say>* is the second load-bearing verb after *<say>être</say>*.
3. **Run the matrix** to automaticity: *<say>J'ai faim</say> → <say>Tu as faim ?</say> → <say>Il a faim</say>…*

> *<say>Avoir</say>* means "to have" — but the French "have" hunger, thirst, fear, age. This is the single biggest English-speaker trap of the A1 level.
> *<say>Avoir</say>* is also verb #2 in the entire language. The whole past tense (*<say>passé composé</say>*, Lesson 21) sits on top of it. Learn it now, permanently.

---

## Part 1: The big idea — French "has" what English "is"

In English, you **are** hungry, you **are** cold, you **are** 20 years old. In French, you **have** hunger, you **have** cold, you **have** 20 years.

| English | French | Literal French |
|---------|--------|----------------|
| I am hungry. | **<say>J'ai faim.</say>** | "I have hunger." |
| I am thirsty. | **<say>J'ai soif.</say>** | "I have thirst." |
| I am cold. | **<say>J'ai froid.</say>** | "I have cold." |
| I am 20 years old. | **<say>J'ai vingt ans.</say>** | "I have 20 years." |
| I am scared. | **<say>J'ai peur.</say>** | "I have fear." |
| I am right. | **<say>J'ai raison.</say>** | "I have reason." |

> **Trap:** the instinct to say *<say>Je suis faim</say>* ("I am hunger") is wrong and unmistakably English-speaker. Worse: *<say>Je suis chaud(e)</say>* doesn't mean "I'm hot" (temperature) — it means "I'm turned on / drunk". Use *<say>J'ai chaud</say>* for temperature.

There's no shortcut here. The list is closed (a dozen or so expressions), so learn each as a chunk: *<say>avoir faim</say>*, *<say>avoir soif</say>*, *<say>avoir froid</say>*. Don't translate, just substitute.

## Part 2: Conjugation of avoir in the present

| Person | Form | Pronunciation |
|--------|------|---------------|
| j' | **<say>ai</say>** | "zhay" (élision is required!) |
| tu | **<say>as</say>** | "tew ah" |
| il / elle / on | **<say>a</say>** | "eel ah / el ah / ohn ah" |
| nous | **<say>avons</say>** | "noo-**z**ah-VOHN" (liaison!) |
| vous | **<say>avez</say>** | "voo-**z**ah-VAY" (liaison!) |
| ils / elles | **<say>ont</say>** | "eel-**z**OHN / el-**z**OHN" (liaison!) |

**Pronunciation traps:**

- *<say>j'ai</say>* — never "zhuh-ay" with two syllables. It's one syllable: "zhay". Élision is mandatory.
- *<say>nous avons</say>*, *<say>vous avez</say>*, *<say>ils ont</say>* — the liaison **z** is non-negotiable. "Noo-zah-VOHN", never "noo-ah-VOHN".
- **<say>ils ont</say>** "eel-ZOHN" (they have) vs **<say>ils sont</say>** "eel SOHN" (they are) — same five letters of difference, completely different verbs. The liaison **z** is your only audible cue between *<say>avoir</say>* and *<say>être</say>* in the third-person plural.

> **The ont / sont trap:** *<say>Ils ont des amis</say>* "they have friends" vs *<say>Ils sont des amis</say>* "they are friends". Listen for the **z**.

---

## Part 3: avoir for possession

The basic meaning is "to have, to own". English has the same verb — easy half.

| French | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>J'ai un chien.</say> | I have a dog. |
| <say>Tu as une voiture.</say> | You have a car. |
| <say>Il a deux frères.</say> | He has two brothers. |
| <say>Nous avons une grande maison.</say> | We have a big house. |
| <say>Vous avez un passeport ?</say> | Do you have a passport? |
| <say>Elles ont des amis à Paris.</say> | They have friends in Paris. |

This is where French and English line up perfectly. The trap is only on the next page — in the negation.

### Negation: avoir → ne … pas **de**

This is the second classic A1 trap, after the "I have hunger" thing. After **ne … pas**, the indefinite articles **un / une / des** all collapse to **de** (or **d'** before a vowel).

| Affirmative | Negative |
|-------------|----------|
| <say>J'ai un chien.</say> | <say>Je n'ai pas de chien.</say> |
| <say>Tu as une voiture.</say> | <say>Tu n'as pas de voiture.</say> |
| <say>Il a des amis.</say> | <say>Il n'a pas d'amis.</say> |
| <say>Nous avons du pain.</say> | <say>Nous n'avons pas de pain.</say> |
| <say>Il y a des étudiants.</say> | <say>Il n'y a pas d'étudiants.</say> |

> **Why?** Think of *<say>de</say>* here as the negative version of "any" in English: *I don't have any dog*. French collapses *<say>un</say>/<say>une</say>/<say>des</say>/<say>du</say>/<say>de la</say>* to a single neutral *<say>de</say>* in the negative — because you're saying "none of it", not "not one specific item".

> **The exception:** with the verb **<say>être</say>**, the article does **not** change.
> *<say>C'est un chien.</say>* → *<say>Ce n'est pas un chien.</say>* (correct)
> *<say>Ce n'est pas de chien</say>* — wrong.
> The *<say>un</say>/<say>une</say>/<say>des</say> → <say>de</say>* rule only applies to verbs of having / quantity (*<say>avoir</say>*, *<say>manger</say>*, *<say>boire</say>*, *<say>acheter</say>*, *<say>vouloir</say>*, *<say>il y a</say>*…).

## Part 4: avoir idioms — physical and emotional state

These are the expressions where English uses **be + adjective** but French uses **avoir + noun**. The list below is the closed set — memorize it whole.

| French | Literal | English meaning |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| <say>avoir faim</say> | to have hunger | to be hungry |
| <say>avoir soif</say> | to have thirst | to be thirsty |
| <say>avoir froid</say> | to have cold | to be cold |
| <say>avoir chaud</say> | to have heat | to be hot (temperature) |
| <say>avoir peur (de)</say> | to have fear (of) | to be afraid (of) |
| <say>avoir sommeil</say> | to have sleep | to be sleepy |
| <say>avoir mal (à)</say> | to have pain (at) | to hurt / to ache |
| <say>avoir raison</say> | to have reason | to be right |
| <say>avoir tort</say> | to have wrong | to be wrong |
| <say>avoir besoin (de)</say> | to have need (of) | to need |
| <say>avoir envie (de)</say> | to have desire (of) | to want / to feel like |
| <say>avoir de la chance</say> | to have luck | to be lucky |

**In action:**

- *<say>J'ai faim.</say>* — I'm hungry. (NOT *<say>Je suis faim</say>*!)
- *<say>Tu as soif ?</say>* — Are you thirsty?
- *<say>Elle a peur des chiens.</say>* — She's afraid of dogs.
- *<say>Nous avons sommeil.</say>* — We're sleepy.
- *<say>J'ai mal à la tête.</say>* — My head hurts / I have a headache. (à + definite article before the body part)
- *<say>Vous avez raison.</say>* — You're right.
- *<say>Il a tort.</say>* — He's wrong.
- *<say>J'ai besoin d'eau.</say>* — I need water.
- *<say>Il a envie d'un café.</say>* — He feels like a coffee.
- *<say>Tu as de la chance !</say>* — You're lucky!

> **Trap #1:** there is **no article** in these expressions. *<say>J'ai faim</say>*, not *<say>j'ai la faim</say>*. The noun loses its article and behaves almost like an adjective. (Compare English "I have a headache" — but "I'm hungry" with no article either; same instinct.)

> **Trap #2:** don't translate "I am cold" word by word. *<say>Je suis froid</say>* means "I am a cold person / I am cold-hearted". Temperature is *<say>J'ai froid</say>*.

> **Trap #3:** *<say>avoir besoin de</say>* and *<say>avoir envie de</say>* are always followed by *<say>de</say>*, even before a noun. *<say>J'ai besoin de temps.</say>* "I need time." *<say>J'ai envie d'un café.</say>* "I feel like a coffee." The *<say>de</say>* contracts to *<say>d'</say>* before a vowel.

## Part 5: Age — avoir … ans

Age in French is **how many years you have**.

| French | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>Quel âge avez-vous ?</say> | How old are you? (formal) |
| <say>Quel âge as-tu ?</say> | How old are you? (informal) |
| <say>J'ai vingt ans.</say> | I'm 20 (years old). |
| <say>Il a trente-cinq ans.</say> | He's 35. |
| <say>Elle a deux ans.</say> | She's 2. |
| <say>Mon père a soixante ans.</say> | My father is 60. |

**Traps:**

- The word **<say>ans</say>** is **mandatory**. English drops "years old" all the time ("I'm 20"). French never drops *<say>ans</say>* — *<say>J'ai vingt</say>* is meaningless ("I have twenty… what?").
- Liaison wakes up before *<say>ans</say>*: *<say>deux ans</say>*, *<say>trois ans</say>*, *<say>six ans</say>*, *<say>dix ans</say>*, *<say>vingt ans</say>*.
- *<say>neuf ans</say>* is read "nuh-**v**-AHN" — the f turns into a v. Same trick as *<say>neuf heures</say>*.

---

## Part 6: il y a — "there is / there are"

This is a fixed phrase meaning "there is" or "there are". The wonderful thing for English speakers: **il y a never changes**, whether what follows is singular or plural. One form covers everything English needs two forms for.

| French | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>Il y a un livre sur la table.</say> | There's a book on the table. |
| <say>Il y a des étudiants dans la classe.</say> | There are students in the class. |
| <say>Il y a vingt jours dans le mois.</say> | There are twenty days in the month. |
| <say>Il n'y a pas de café.</say> | There's no coffee. |
| <say>Y a-t-il un problème ?</say> | Is there a problem? |
| <say>Qu'est-ce qu'il y a ?</say> | What's wrong? / What's there? |

> **The big win:** *<say>il y a</say>* is the same for singular and plural. No "is/are" choice. *<say>Il y a un livre</say>* (one book) and *<say>il y a cent livres</say>* (a hundred books) use the exact same verb form. English speakers waste effort here; French gives you a freebie.

> **Pronunciation:** "eel-ya". In casual speech the *<say>il</say>* often drops entirely — you'll hear just **"ya"** (written *<say>y a</say>*): *<say>Y a quelqu'un ?</say>* "Anyone here?"

**Negation:** same rule as plain *<say>avoir</say>* — *<say>un</say>/<say>une</say>/<say>des</say> → <say>de</say>*:
*<say>Il y a un café</say>* → *<say>Il n'y a pas de café.</say>*

**Question:** three options, as usual — intonation (*<say>Il y a un café ?</say>*), est-ce que (*<say>Est-ce qu'il y a un café ?</say>*), inversion (*<say>Y a-t-il un café ?</say>*, with a euphonic *-t-*).

---

## Part 7: avoir vs être — the contrast (callback to Lesson 4)

| être | avoir |
|------|-------|
| <say>Je suis étudiant.</say> | <say>J'ai un livre.</say> |
| <say>Tu es français.</say> | <say>Tu as une voiture.</say> |
| <say>Il est fatigué.</say> | <say>Il a faim.</say> |
| <say>Nous sommes ici.</say> | <say>Nous avons vingt ans.</say> |

**Rule of thumb:**
- **<say>être</say>** + adjective or noun describing identity → "to be" (profession, nationality, state via adjective)
- **<say>avoir</say>** + noun → "to have" (possession, plus the closed list of idioms above)

> *<say>Je suis fatigué.</say>* (adjective → être) "I'm tired."
> *<say>J'ai sommeil.</say>* (noun → avoir) "I'm sleepy."
> Same English "I'm…" — but French splits the work by what comes after.

### avoir mal à — body part pain

The preposition *<say>à</say>* contracts with the definite article: **<say>à + le = au</say>**, **<say>à + les = aux</say>** (à + la / à + l' stay separate).

| French | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>J'ai mal à la tête.</say> | I have a headache. |
| <say>J'ai mal aux dents.</say> | My teeth hurt. |
| <say>J'ai mal au dos.</say> | My back hurts. |
| <say>J'ai mal au ventre.</say> | I have a stomachache. |
| <say>J'ai mal à l'oreille.</say> | My ear hurts. |
| <say>J'ai mal aux pieds.</say> | My feet hurt. |

## Language scales

Run aloud, no pauses. Aim for sound, not thought.

### Scale 1: avoir — base conjugation

> <say>J'ai un livre.</say> → <say>Tu as un livre.</say> → <say>Il a un livre.</say> → <say>Elle a un livre.</say> → <say>Nous avons un livre.</say> → <say>Vous avez un livre.</say> → <say>Ils ont un livre.</say> → <say>Elles ont un livre.</say>

### Scale 2: avoir + state idiom

> <say>J'ai faim.</say> → <say>Tu as soif.</say> → <say>Il a froid.</say> → <say>Elle a chaud.</say> → <say>Nous avons peur.</say> → <say>Vous avez sommeil.</say> → <say>Ils ont raison.</say> → <say>Elles ont tort.</say>

### Scale 3: age

> <say>J'ai vingt ans.</say> → <say>Tu as quinze ans.</say> → <say>Il a trente ans.</say> → <say>Elle a dix-huit ans.</say> → <say>Nous avons quarante ans.</say> → <say>Vous avez cinquante ans.</say> → <say>Ils ont soixante ans.</say>

### Scale 4: negation (un/une/des → de)

> <say>J'ai un chien.</say> → <say>Je n'ai pas de chien.</say>
> <say>Tu as une voiture.</say> → <say>Tu n'as pas de voiture.</say>
> <say>Il a des amis.</say> → <say>Il n'a pas d'amis.</say>
> <say>Nous avons du pain.</say> → <say>Nous n'avons pas de pain.</say>

### Scale 5: il y a — variations

> <say>Il y a un livre.</say> → <say>Il y a des livres.</say> → <say>Il n'y a pas de livre.</say> → <say>Y a-t-il un livre ?</say> → <say>Qu'est-ce qu'il y a ?</say>

## Language matrix

Principle: the answer is already embedded in the question. Just flip the person.

### Matrix 1: possession Q&A across persons

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Tu as un chien ?</say> | <say>Oui, j'ai un chien.</say> / <say>Non, je n'ai pas de chien.</say> |
| <say>Il a une voiture ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, il a une voiture.</say> |
| <say>Vous avez des enfants ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, nous avons des enfants.</say> |
| <say>Ils ont une maison ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, ils ont une maison.</say> |
| <say>Tu as quel âge ?</say> (answer: 25 years old) | <say>J'ai vingt-cinq ans.</say> |
| <say>Quel âge a-t-elle ?</say> (answer: 30 years old) | <say>Elle a trente ans.</say> |

### Matrix 2: state

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Tu as faim ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, j'ai faim.</say> |
| <say>Vous avez soif ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, nous avons soif.</say> |
| <say>Il a froid ?</say> (answer: no, he's hot) | <say>Non, il a chaud.</say> |
| <say>Tu as peur ?</say> (answer: no) | <say>Non, je n'ai pas peur.</say> |
| <say>Vous avez sommeil ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, j'ai sommeil.</say> |

### Matrix 3: il y a in places

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>Il y a un café ici ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, il y a un café.</say> |
| <say>Il y a des étudiants ?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Oui, il y a des étudiants.</say> |
| <say>Il y a du pain ?</say> (answer: no) | <say>Non, il n'y a pas de pain.</say> |
| <say>Qu'est-ce qu'il y a sur la table ?</say> (answer: a book and a key) | <say>Il y a un livre et une clé.</say> |

## Exercises

### Exercise 1. Conjugate avoir

Fill in the right form of *avoir*:

1. Je ___ un chat.
2. Tu ___ vingt ans.
3. Elle ___ faim.
4. Nous ___ deux enfants.
5. Vous ___ un passeport ?
6. Ils ___ peur.
7. On ___ soif.
8. Marc et Sophie ___ une grande maison.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *ai* — J'**ai** un chat.
2. *as* — Tu **as** vingt ans.
3. *a* — Elle **a** faim.
4. *avons* — Nous **avons** deux enfants.
5. *avez* — Vous **avez** un passeport ?
6. *ont* — Ils **ont** peur. (liaison: "eel-ZOHN")
7. *a* — On **a** soif.
8. *ont* — Marc et Sophie **ont** une grande maison.

</details>

### Exercise 2. avoir idioms vs être (the big trap)

Pick the verb (avoir or être), conjugate it, and translate.

1. I'm hungry. → Je ___ faim.
2. I'm tired. → Je ___ fatigué.
3. Are you cold? → Tu ___ froid ?
4. She is French. → Elle ___ française.
5. He's 20 years old. → Il ___ vingt ans.
6. We are right. → Nous ___ raison.
7. Are you a doctor? → Vous ___ médecin ?
8. They have a headache. → Ils ___ mal à la tête.
9. I am sleepy. → Je ___ sommeil.
10. She is afraid of dogs. → Elle ___ peur des chiens.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *J'ai faim.* (noun → avoir)
2. *Je suis fatigué.* (adjective → être)
3. *Tu as froid ?* (noun → avoir)
4. *Elle est française.* (nationality → être)
5. *Il a vingt ans.* (age → avoir)
6. *Nous avons raison.* (idiom → avoir)
7. *Vous êtes médecin ?* (profession → être)
8. *Ils ont mal à la tête.* (pain → avoir)
9. *J'ai sommeil.* (noun → avoir)
10. *Elle a peur des chiens.* (idiom → avoir; *de + les = des*)

</details>

### Exercise 3. Negation (un/une/des → de)

Make each sentence negative:

1. J'ai un frère.
2. Tu as une sœur.
3. Il a des amis.
4. Nous avons un chien.
5. Vous avez des enfants.
6. Il y a un café ici.
7. Elle a de la chance.
8. Ils ont des lunettes.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Je n'ai pas de frère.*
2. *Tu n'as pas de sœur.*
3. *Il n'a pas d'amis.* (élision before vowel!)
4. *Nous n'avons pas de chien.*
5. *Vous n'avez pas d'enfants.*
6. *Il n'y a pas de café ici.*
7. *Elle n'a pas de chance.*
8. *Ils n'ont pas de lunettes.*

Reminder: *un/une/des/du/de la* all collapse to *de* (or *d'* before a vowel) under negation with *avoir*. With *être*, the article would stay.

</details>

### Exercise 4. Age and numbers

Translate into French:

1. I'm 18.
2. How old are you? (informal)
3. She's 25.
4. We're 40.
5. He's 9. (mind the *neuf ans* pronunciation!)

7. How old is your sister?
8. The dog is 2 years old.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *J'ai dix-huit ans.*

### Exercise 5. il y a — describe the scene

Build a sentence with *il y a* for each setting:

1. In the bag: a book, a key, a phone.
2. In the classroom: students, a teacher, a board.
3. On the table: a cup of coffee, two pieces of bread, a knife.
4. In Paris: the Eiffel Tower, museums, cafés.
5. In my family: a brother, two sisters, a dog.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Dans le sac, il y a un livre, une clé et un téléphone.*
2. *Dans la classe, il y a des étudiants, un professeur et un tableau.*
3. *Sur la table, il y a une tasse de café, deux pains et un couteau.* (or with partitive: *du café*, *du pain*)
4. *À Paris, il y a la tour Eiffel, des musées et des cafés.*
5. *Dans ma famille, il y a un frère, deux sœurs et un chien.*

Notice: *il y a* never inflects. One *livre* or twenty *livres* — same form.

</details>

### Exercise 6. Mini-dialogue — assemble and recite

  vous avez      [voo-zah-VAY]     (liaison z!)
  ils/elles ont  [eel-ZOHN/el-ZOHN] (liaison z!)

THE BIG ENGLISH-SPEAKER TRAP:
  French "has" what English "is":
  J'ai faim.        I am hungry.      ("I have hunger")
  J'ai froid.       I am cold.        ("I have cold")
  J'ai 20 ans.      I am 20.          ("I have 20 years")
  J'ai peur.        I am scared.      ("I have fear")
  J'ai raison.      I am right.       ("I have reason")
  
  NEVER: Je suis faim / Je suis 20 / Je suis froid (= cold-hearted)

AVOIR vs ÊTRE — the split:
  être  + adjective:    Je suis fatigué.   (I'm tired)
  avoir + noun:         J'ai sommeil.      (I'm sleepy)
  être  + profession:   Il est médecin.
  avoir + possession:   Il a une voiture.
  
  ⚠ ils ONT (have, liaison "z") ≠ ils SONT (are, no liaison)

NEGATION OF AVOIR — the article trap:
  un / une / des → de (d' before vowel)
  J'ai un chien.    → Je n'ai pas DE chien.
  Il a des amis.    → Il n'a pas D'amis.
  Il y a du pain.   → Il n'y a pas DE pain.
  
  ⚠ With être the article does NOT change: Ce n'est pas UN chien.

AVOIR IDIOMS (no article!):
  avoir faim         hungry
  avoir soif         thirsty
  avoir froid        cold
  avoir chaud        hot
  avoir sommeil      sleepy
  avoir peur (de)    afraid (of)
  avoir mal à        ache / hurt
  avoir raison       right
  avoir tort         wrong
  avoir besoin de    need
  avoir envie de     feel like / want
  avoir … ans        … years old
  avoir de la chance lucky

AGE:
  Quel âge as-tu ?      — J'ai vingt ans.
  Quel âge a-t-il ?     — Il a trente ans.
  ⚠ the word ans is mandatory.
  ⚠ neuf ans → "nuh-V-AHN" (f→v before a vowel)

IL Y A (there is / there are):
  Il y a un livre.        (singular)
  Il y a des livres.      (plural — same form!)
  Il n'y a pas de café.   (negation: un/une/des → de)
  Y a-t-il … ?            (inversion question, with euphonic -t-)
  Qu'est-ce qu'il y a ?   (what's there? / what's wrong?)

BODY + AVOIR MAL À:
  J'ai mal à la tête.    (à + la — head)
  J'ai mal aux dents.    (à + les — teeth)
  J'ai mal au dos.       (à + le — back)
  J'ai mal au ventre.    (à + le — stomach)
