# Lesson 1: Reading German. Numbers 1–20
**Vocabulary: Alphabet, pronunciation, greetings**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — slowly, consciously, analyzing every sound
3. **Speed up** — repeat until the phrases fly out on their own

> Knowing the rule = 5%. Training your mouth = 95%.
> Language is a sport. You won't learn to swim by reading a book about swimming. Open your mouth and speak.

---

## Part 1: German reading in 10 minutes

Good news: German reads almost exactly the way it's written. None of the chaos of English (think of how "though, through, tough, thought" all use the same letters). Learn 15 rules and you can read any German word.

### The rules, hidden inside numbers

Look at the numbers 1–12. Almost every German reading rule is hiding in them:

| Number | Spelling | What you learn |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| 1 | <say>**ei**ns</say> | **ei** = "eye" (like in *Einstein*). Always. No exceptions. |
| 2 | <say>**z**wei</say> | **z** = "ts" (like *pizza*). Always. |
| 3 | <say>dr**ei**</say> | again **ei** = "eye" |
| 4 | <say>**v**ier</say> | **v** = "f". And **ie** = long "ee" sound. |
| 5 | <say>f**ü**nf</say> | **ü** = no English equivalent. Round your lips for "oo", then say "ee". |
| 6 | <say>se**chs**</say> | **s** before a vowel = "z". And **chs** = "ks" (like *fox*). |
| 7 | <say>s**ie**ben</say> | **ie** = long "ee" (NOT "ee-eh"!) |
| 8 | <say>a**ch**t</say> | **ch** after a, o, u = harsh "kh" (like Scottish *loch*) |
| 9 | <say>n**eu**n</say> | **eu** = "oy" (like *boy*). Always. |
| 10 | <say>ze**h**n</say> | **h** after a vowel = silence. It just lengthens the vowel. |
| 11 | <say>elf</say> | nothing tricky here |
| 12 | <say>zw**ö**lf</say> | **ö** = no English equivalent. Round your lips for "oh", then say "eh". |

### 5 more key rules

| Letter/combo | Sound | Example |
|--------------|-------|---------|
| **w** | "v" (like English *vine*) | <say>**w**as</say> = "vahs" |
| **j** | "y" (like English *yes*) | <say>**j**a</say> = "yah" |
| **sch** | "sh" | <say>**Sch**ule</say> = "shoo-leh" |
| **st-/sp-** at word start | "sht-/shp-" | <say>**St**raße</say> = "shtrah-seh" |
| **ß** (Eszett) | long "ss" | <say>Stra**ß**e</say> = "shtrah-seh" |

> **The English-speaker's biggest reading mistake:** treating German vowels like English ones. German **a** = "ah" (like *father*), NEVER "ay" (like *cake*). German **e** = "eh", NEVER "ee". German **i** = "ee", NEVER "eye". One letter, one sound. Trust the spelling.

## Part 2: The umlauts (ä, ö, ü) — pronunciation hack

The umlauts have no English equivalent. Use this trick:

> **Position your mouth for one sound, but say a different one.**

| Letter | Mouth position | Say | Like in |
|--------|---------------|-----|---------|
| **ä** | mouth says "ah" | say "eh" | Roughly like the **e** in *bed* |
| **ö** | mouth says "oh" | say "eh" | No English equivalent — French **eu** in *peur* |
| **ü** | mouth says "oo" | say "ee" | No English equivalent — French **u** in *tu* |

Practice by saying English "ee" → then round your lips → that's **ü**.

## Part 3: Numbers 1–20

| 1 | <say>eins</say> | 11 | <say>elf</say> |
|---|------|----|-----|
| 2 | <say>zwei</say> | 12 | <say>zwölf</say> |
| 3 | <say>drei</say> | 13 | <say>dreizehn</say> |
| 4 | <say>vier</say> | 14 | <say>vierzehn</say> |
| 5 | <say>fünf</say> | 15 | <say>fünfzehn</say> |
| 6 | <say>sechs</say> | 16 | <say>sech**z**ehn</say> (drop the s!) |
| 7 | <say>sieben</say> | 17 | <say>sieb**z**ehn</say> (drop the en!) |
| 8 | <say>acht</say> | 18 | <say>achtzehn</say> |
| 9 | <say>neun</say> | 19 | <say>neunzehn</say> |
| 10 | <say>zehn</say> | 20 | <say>zwanzig</say> |

> **Trap!** *<say>sechs</say>* (6) loses its **s** in *<say>sech**z**ehn</say>* (16). And *<say>sieben</say>* (7) drops its **en** in *<say>sieb**z**ehn</say>* (17). The Germans are lazy — they don't want to say "<say>sechszehn</say>" or "<say>siebenzehn</say>", too many consonants.

Pattern from 13–19: **digit + zehn**. Like English "thir-teen, four-teen". Direct cognate of "teen"!

## Part 4: Vocabulary — Greetings and basic phrases

### Greetings

| German | English | When to use |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| <say>Hallo</say> | Hi | Anytime, informal |
| <say>Guten Morgen</say> | Good morning | Until ~10 a.m. |
| <say>Guten Tag</say> | Good day / Hello | Standard daytime greeting |
| <say>Guten Abend</say> | Good evening | After ~6 p.m. |
| <say>Tschüss</say> | Bye | Informal goodbye |
| <say>Auf Wiedersehen</say> | Goodbye | Formal goodbye (lit. "until seeing again") |
| <say>Gute Nacht</say> | Good night | When parting at night |

### Essentials

| German | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>Ja</say> | Yes |
| <say>Nein</say> | No |
| <say>Danke</say> | Thank you |
| <say>Bitte</say> | Please / You're welcome |
| <say>Entschuldigung</say> | Excuse me / Sorry |
| <say>Gut</say> | Good |
| <say>Schlecht</say> | Bad |

### Introducing yourself

| German | English |
|--------|---------|
| <say>Ich heiße…</say> | My name is… (lit. "I am called…") |
| <say>Wie heißt du?</say> | What's your name? (informal) |
| <say>Wie heißen Sie?</say> | What's your name? (formal) |
| <say>Freut mich</say> | Nice to meet you |
| <say>Wie geht es dir?</say> | How are you? (informal) |
| <say>Wie geht es Ihnen?</say> | How are you? (formal) |
| <say>Mir geht es gut</say> | I'm doing well |

> **The du vs Sie thing:** German has two "you"s. *<say>Du</say>* is informal (friends, family, kids, anyone you'd call by first name). *<say>Sie</say>* is formal (strangers, business, anyone you'd call Mr./Ms.). It's exactly like the old English thou/you distinction — except Germans still use it. When in doubt with adults, start with *<say>Sie</say>*. They'll offer "<say>du</say>" if appropriate.

## Part 5: Exercises

### Exercise 1. Read out loud — apply the rules

Pronounce each word. Write down what sound each highlighted letter makes.

1. **ei**ns
2. dr**ei**
3. **v**ier
4. **z**wei
5. f**ü**nf
6. n**eu**n
7. a**ch**t
8. ze**h**n
9. **w**as
10. **sch**ön

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *eins → "eye-nss"* (ei = "eye")
2. *drei → "dry"* (ei = "eye")
3. *vier → "feer"* (v = "f", ie = "ee")
4. *zwei → "tsvai"* (z = "ts", ei = "eye")
5. *fünf → "fünf"* (ü = rounded "ee")
6. *neun → "noyn"* (eu = "oy")
7. *acht → "akht"* (ch after a = harsh "kh")
8. *zehn → "tsayn"* (h after vowel = silent, just lengthens)
9. *was → "vahs"* (w = "v")
10. *schön → "shurn"* (sch = "sh", ö = rounded "eh")

</details>
