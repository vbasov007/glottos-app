## Part 1: Two types of questions in German

German has two question types. Two — not twenty. Learn the word order for each and you can ask anything.

Good news for English speakers: structurally, German questions are SIMPLER than English. English needs the dummy verb "do" ("Where **do** you live?", "**Do** you speak German?"). German doesn't bother — it just flips the verb. Direct.

### W-Fragen (open questions)

Start with a question word (<say>W-Wort</say>). Word order:

```
W-Wort + Verb + Subject + ...?
```

| Position 1 | Position 2 | Position 3 | |
|---|---|---|---|
| <say>**Wo**</say> | <say>**wohnst**</say> | <say>**du**?</say> | |
| <say>**Was**</say> | <say>**machst**</say> | <say>**du**</say> | <say>beruflich?</say> |
| <say>**Woher**</say> | <say>**kommst**</say> | <say>**du**?</say> | |

Verb ALWAYS on second position. Question word on first.

Compare with English: "Where DO you live?" (3 elements before the main verb). German: "<say>Wo wohnst du?</say>" (just 3 elements, verb is the main verb itself). Cleaner.

### Ja/Nein-Fragen (yes/no questions)

Answer = yes or no. No question word. Word order:

```
Verb + Subject + ...?
```

| Position 1 | Position 2 | | |
|---|---|---|---|
| <say>**Wohnst**</say> | <say>**du**</say> | <say>in Berlin?</say> | |
| <say>**Sprichst**</say> | <say>**du**</say> | <say>Deutsch?</say> | |
| <say>**Hast**</say> | <say>**du**</say> | <say>Geschwister?</say> | |

Verb jumps to FIRST position. Everything else as usual.

Note: English used to do this too. "**Speakest** thou English?" was once correct. We dropped it and added "do". German never bothered.

## Part 2: The question words (W-Wörter)

Here's the full set. Memorize all of them — they cover 99% of questions.

Cognate alert: every German W-word starts with **W**, just like every English question word (who, what, when, where, why, how is the only odd one out). The W-cluster is Germanic.

| W-Wort | English | Cognate | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| <say>**Wer?**</say> | Who? | ≈ "who" | <say>Wer ist das?</say> — Who is that? |
| <say>**Was?**</say> | What? | ≈ "what" | <say>Was machst du?</say> — What are you doing? |
| <say>**Wo?**</say> | Where? | ≈ "where" | <say>Wo wohnst du?</say> — Where do you live? |
| <say>**Woher?**</say> | Where from? | <say>wo</say> + <say>her</say> | <say>Woher kommst du?</say> — Where are you from? |
| <say>**Wohin?**</say> | Where to? | <say>wo</say> + <say>hin</say> | <say>Wohin fährst du?</say> — Where are you going? |
| <say>**Wie?**</say> | How? | (no direct cognate) | <say>Wie heißt du?</say> — What's your name? |
| <say>**Wie viel?**</say> | How much? | <say>wie</say> + "much" | <say>Wie viel kostet das?</say> — How much does that cost? |
| <say>**Wie viele?**</say> | How many? | <say>wie</say> + "many" | <say>Wie viele Zimmer hast du?</say> — How many rooms do you have? |
| <say>**Wann?**</say> | When? | ≈ "when" | <say>Wann kommst du?</say> — When are you coming? |
| <say>**Warum?**</say> | Why? | | <say>Warum lernst du Deutsch?</say> — Why are you learning German? |
| <say>**Welcher/Welche/Welches?**</say> | Which? | ≈ "which" | <say>Welche Farbe?</say> — Which color? |

> **Trap: "Who" trap.** German <say>**wer**</say> = English "who". But German <say>**wo**</say> = English "where". A native English speaker, hearing <say>**wo**</say>, instinctively wants to translate as "who". Wrong. <say>**Wer**</say> = who, <say>**wo**</say> = where. Drill this contrast.

## Part 3: The hack — Wo / Woher / Wohin trio

Three words English speakers blur into one. English handles direction with prepositions: "Where are you?" vs "Where are you from?" vs "Where are you going?". German splits it into three distinct words.

| W-Wort | Meaning | Question | Answer | Preposition |
|--------|---------|----------|--------|-------------|
| <say>**Wo?**</say> | where (location) | <say>Wo bist du?</say> | <say>In Berlin</say> | <say>**in**</say> |
| <say>**Woher?**</say> | from where (origin) | <say>Woher kommst du?</say> | <say>Aus Russland</say> | <say>**aus**</say> |
| <say>**Wohin?**</say> | to where (direction) | <say>Wohin fährst du?</say> | <say>Nach Hamburg</say> | <say>**nach**</say> |

The logic:
- <say>**Wo**</say> = point on the map. You're standing. Not moving.
- <say>**Wo-her**</say> = "where-from". Movement TOWARD you (origin).
- <say>**Wo-hin**</say> = "where-to". Movement AWAY from you (destination).

Mnemonic: <say>**her**</say> = "hither" (old English for "to here"). <say>**hin**</say> = "thither" (to there). Same Germanic roots, German just kept them productive.

> **Trap:** "Where are you going?" — that's <say>**Wohin gehst du?**</say>, NOT ~~<say>Wo gehst du?</say>~~. <say>**Wo**</say> is for static location only. Movement = <say>wohin</say>.

More examples:

```
Wo arbeitest du? — Ich arbeite in einem Büro
Woher hast du das? — Aus dem Internet
Wohin gehst du heute Abend? — Ins Kino
```

## Part 4: Word order — recap

Here's the main rule, big and bold one more time:

### W-Fragen: verb on SECOND position

```
[1 W-Wort] [2 VERB] [3 subject] [rest]

Wo          wohnst    du?
Was         macht     er          beruflich?
Wann        kommst    du          nach Hause?
Wie viele   Zimmer    hat         deine Wohnung?
```

### Ja/Nein-Fragen: verb on FIRST position

```
[1 VERB] [2 subject] [rest]

Wohnst      du           in Berlin?
Hast        du           einen Balkon?
Ist         das          dein Zimmer?
```

> You already know this rule from statements: verb is always on position 2. In W-questions, nothing changes. In yes/no questions, the verb just takes position 1 because there's no question word in front.

This is the famous German **V2 rule**. In English, the verb sits where it sits. In German, the verb always plants itself on position 2 (in main clauses). Get this in your bones; it's the #1 word-order rule of the language.

## Part 5: Vocabulary — Furniture and home

German loves compound nouns. Look how many of these are just two words glued together.

| German | Article | English | Cognate / breakdown |
|--------|---------|---------|---------------------|
| <say>Tisch</say> | <say>der</say> | table | (no cognate — just learn) |
| <say>Stuhl</say> | <say>der</say> | chair | ≈ "stool" |
| <say>Bett</say> | <say>das</say> | bed | ≈ "bed" |
| <say>Schrank</say> | <say>der</say> | wardrobe / cupboard | |
| <say>Sofa</say> | <say>das</say> | sofa | direct steal |
| <say>Regal</say> | <say>das</say> | shelf | |
| <say>Lampe</say> | <say>die</say> | lamp | ≈ "lamp" |
| <say>Teppich</say> | <say>der</say> | carpet / rug | |
| <say>Spiegel</say> | <say>der</say> | mirror | |
| <say>Küche</say> | <say>die</say> | kitchen | ≈ "kitchen" |
| <say>Bad</say> / <say>Badezimmer</say> | <say>das</say> | bathroom | <say>Bad</say> ≈ "bath". <say>Bade-zimmer</say> = bath-room (literal!) |
| <say>Schlafzimmer</say> | <say>das</say> | bedroom | <say>schlaf</say> (sleep) + <say>Zimmer</say> (room) = "sleep-room" |
| <say>Wohnzimmer</say> | <say>das</say> | living room | <say>wohn</say> (live) + <say>Zimmer</say> = "living-room" — literal! |
| <say>Kinderzimmer</say> | <say>das</say> | children's room | <say>Kinder</say> + <say>Zimmer</say> |
| <say>Flur</say> | <say>der</say> | hallway / corridor | |
| <say>Balkon</say> | <say>der</say> | balcony | ≈ "balcony" |
| <say>Fenster</say> | <say>das</say> | window | ≈ "fenester" (Latin root, same as "defenestrate") |
| <say>Tür</say> | <say>die</say> | door | (related to "thoroughfare"?) |
| <say>Wohnung</say> | <say>die</say> | apartment / flat | from <say>wohnen</say> = to live |
| <say>Haus</say> | <say>das</say> | house | ≈ "house" — identical |
| <say>Zimmer</say> | <say>das</say> | room | |
| <say>Etage</say> / <say>Stock</say> | <say>die</say> / <say>der</say> | floor / story | <say>Etage</say> from French |

> **Remember:** <say>**Zimmer**</say> = room. But <say>Schlaf**zimmer**</say>, <say>Wohn**zimmer**</say>, <say>Kinder**zimmer**</say>, <say>Bade**zimmer**</say> — compound nouns. Germans glue nouns together. Get used to breaking long words into pieces. The longest German word ever (officially): *<say>Rindfleischetikettierungsüberwachungsaufgabenübertragungsgesetz</say>*. (Don't worry. You'll never need it.)

## Part 6: Language scales

### Scale 1 — Wo + wohnen

Run it out loud. Each person — separate and clear:

```
Wo wohne ich? — Ich wohne in einer Wohnung
Wo wohnst du? — Ich wohne in einem Haus
Wo wohnt er? — Er wohnt in Berlin
Wo wohnt sie? — Sie wohnt in München
Wo wohnen wir? — Wir wohnen in Deutschland
Wo wohnt ihr? — Wir wohnen in Hamburg
Wo wohnen sie? — Sie wohnen in London
```

### Scale 2 — Was + machen

```
Was mache ich? — Ich lerne Deutsch
Was machst du? — Ich arbeite
Was macht er? — Er kocht
Was macht sie? — Sie liest ein Buch
Was machen wir? — Wir essen zusammen
Was macht ihr? — Wir spielen Schach
Was machen sie? — Sie schlafen
```

### Scale 3 — Mixed W-questions through all persons

```
Wer bin ich? — Ich bin Student
Wo wohnst du? — In Berlin
Woher kommt er? — Aus Amerika
Wohin fährt sie? — Nach Hamburg
Was machen wir? — Wir lernen Deutsch
Wann kommt ihr? — Um acht Uhr
Warum lernen sie Deutsch? — Sie arbeiten in Deutschland
Wie heißt du? — Ich heiße Anna
Wie viele Zimmer hat deine Wohnung? — Drei Zimmer
```

Repeat each scale 3 times. Then close the text and do it from memory.

## Part 7: Language matrix (Q-A)

### Matrix 1 — "You ↔ I"

```
Wo wohnst du? — Ich wohne in einer Wohnung in Berlin
Was machst du beruflich? — Ich bin Ingenieur
Woher kommst du? — Ich komme aus England
Wie heißt du? — Ich heiße Alex
Wann stehst du auf? — Um sieben Uhr
Hast du Geschwister? — Ja, ich habe einen Bruder
```

### Matrix 2 — Third persons

```
Wo wohnt er? — Er wohnt in Hamburg
Was macht sie beruflich? — Sie ist Lehrerin
Woher kommt dein Freund? — Er kommt aus Irland
Wie heißt deine Schwester? — Sie heißt Emma
Wann kommt er nach Hause? — Um sechs Uhr
Spricht dein Bruder Deutsch? — Ja, er spricht gut Deutsch
```

### Matrix 3 — Situational (apartment and home)

```
Wie viele Zimmer hat deine Wohnung? — Drei Zimmer: Schlafzimmer, Wohnzimmer und Kinderzimmer
Hast du einen Balkon? — Ja, ich habe einen großen Balkon
Wo steht dein Bett? — Im Schlafzimmer
In welcher Etage wohnst du? — Im dritten Stock
Ist deine Küche groß? — Nein, die Küche ist klein
Hast du einen Spiegel im Bad? — Ja, über dem Waschbecken
```

Drill each matrix until you answer without a pause.

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
TWO QUESTION TYPES:

1. W-Fragen (open):
   W-Wort + VERB + subject + ...?
   Wo wohnst du? / Was machst du?

2. Ja/Nein-Fragen (yes/no):
   VERB + subject + ...?
   Wohnst du in Berlin? / Hast du einen Balkon?

(English needs "do"; German doesn't — just flip the verb.)

QUESTION WORDS (W-Wörter):
  Wer (who), Was (what), Wo (where), Woher (where from),
  Wohin (where to), Wie (how), Wie viel (how much),
  Wann (when), Warum (why)

WO / WOHER / WOHIN TRIO:
  Wo = static (where) → in Berlin
  Woher = from where → aus Russland
  Wohin = to where → nach Hamburg

DON'T MIX UP: wer = WHO, wo = WHERE (false friends!)

FURNITURE AND HOME:
  Tisch, Stuhl, Bett, Schrank, Sofa, Regal, Lampe
  Teppich, Spiegel, Fenster, Tür
  Küche, Bad, Schlafzimmer, Wohnzimmer, Kinderzimmer
  Flur, Balkon, Wohnung, Haus, Zimmer, Etage

Compound nouns: Schlaf+zimmer = sleep-room. Get used to gluing!
```
