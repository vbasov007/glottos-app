# Lesson 3: The verbs haben and sein
**Vocabulary: Family**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — slowly, consciously, analyzing every form
3. **Speed up** — repeat until the phrases fly out on their own

> You can count and introduce yourself. Time to actually talk. The first two verbs — haben and sein — are the foundation of everything. Without them you can't build a single sentence.

---

## Part 1: sein (to be)

In Russian people say "I student" — they drop "to be". In German — you can't. Every sentence needs a verb, just like in English.

> <say>**Ich bin Student.**</say> — I am a student.

Conjugation of <say>**sein**</say> is irregular. Just like "to be" in English (I am / you are / he is). Don't look for logic — drill it.

| Pronoun | sein | English |
|---|---|---|
| <say>**ich**</say> | <say>**bin**</say> | I am |
| <say>**du**</say> | <say>**bist**</say> | you are (sg.) |
| <say>**er / sie / es**</say> | <say>**ist**</say> | he/she/it is |
| <say>**wir**</say> | <say>**sind**</say> | we are |
| <say>**ihr**</say> | <say>**seid**</say> | you are (pl.) |
| <say>**Sie**</say> (formal) | <say>**sind**</say> | you are |

Notice: <say>**ist**</say> ≈ "is". Free cognate. <say>**bin**</say> is unique — but English used to have "be-est" (Shakespeare's "thou be-est"). Same family.

### Examples

- <say>Ich **bin** aus London.</say> — I am from London.
- <say>Du **bist** Ingenieur.</say> — You are an engineer.
- <say>Er **ist** zwanzig Jahre alt.</say> — He is twenty years old.
- <say>Wir **sind** Freunde.</say> — We are friends.
- <say>Ihr **seid** nett.</say> — You guys are nice.
- <say>Sie **sind** Herr Braun?</say> — You are Mr. Braun?

> **Trap!** "I am 20 years old" — Germans literally say "I am 20 years old": <say>Ich **bin** zwanzig Jahre alt.</say> Same as English. NOT "<say>Ich habe 20 Jahre</say>" (that's French). Age = <say>**sein**</say>.

## Part 2: haben (to have)

Cognate alert: <say>**haben**</say> ≈ "to have". <say>**hat**</say> ≈ "has". You already know half this verb.

> <say>**Ich habe einen Bruder.**</say> — I have a brother.

Conjugation of <say>**haben**</say> — also irregular, but simpler:

| Pronoun | haben | English |
|---|---|---|
| <say>**ich**</say> | <say>**habe**</say> | I have |
| <say>**du**</say> | <say>**hast**</say> | you have |
| <say>**er / sie / es**</say> | <say>**hat**</say> | he/she/it has |
| <say>**wir**</say> | <say>**haben**</say> | we have |
| <say>**ihr**</say> | <say>**habt**</say> | you (pl.) have |
| <say>**Sie**</say> (formal) | <say>**haben**</say> | you have |

### Examples

- <say>Ich **habe** eine Schwester.</say> — I have a sister.
- <say>Du **hast** einen Hund.</say> — You have a dog.
- <say>Sie **hat** zwei Kinder.</say> — She has two children.
- <say>Wir **haben** ein Problem.</say> — We have a problem.
- <say>Ihr **habt** eine große Familie.</say> — You guys have a big family.
- <say>Haben **Sie** Zeit?</say> — Do you have time?

> **Trap!** <say>du ha**st**</say>, <say>er ha**t**</say> — the **b** drops out. NOT "<say>du habst</say>" or "<say>er habt</say>" — say <say>**du hast**</say>, <say>**er hat**</say>. The same thing happens in English: "I have / he has" loses the "ve". German loses the "b". Lazy mouths everywhere.

### The object form (a heads-up)

You'll notice <say>**einen Bruder**</say> (with -en) but <say>**eine Schwester**</say> (with -e). That's the *Akkusativ* — the "object" form of the article. Don't worry about it yet. Just learn the phrases as chunks. Lesson 9 explains the whole system. Promise.

For now, remember: masculine "a" → <say>**einen**</say> after <say>haben</say>. Feminine "a" → <say>**eine**</say>. Neuter "a" → <say>**ein**</say>.

## Part 3: Vocabulary — Family

A goldmine of cognates. English and German split off the same Germanic root — family words are nearly identical.

| German | English | Cognate hint |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| <say>der Vater</say> | father | <say>**Vater**</say> ≈ "father" (Old Eng. *fæder*) |
| <say>die Mutter</say> | mother | <say>**Mutter**</say> ≈ "mother" |
| <say>der Bruder</say> | brother | <say>**Bruder**</say> ≈ "brother" |
| <say>die Schwester</say> | sister | <say>**Schwester**</say> ≈ "sister" |
| <say>das Kind</say> | child | "kind" in "kindergarten" (kids' garden) |
| <say>die Eltern</say> | parents | |
| <say>die Großeltern</say> | grandparents | "<say>groß</say>" = great. Lit. "great-parents" |
| <say>der Großvater</say> | grandfather | <say>groß</say> + <say>Vater</say> |
| <say>die Großmutter</say> | grandmother | <say>groß</say> + <say>Mutter</say> |
| <say>der Sohn</say> | son | <say>**Sohn**</say> ≈ "son" |
| <say>die Tochter</say> | daughter | <say>**Tochter**</say> ≈ "daughter" (Old Eng. *dohtor*) |
| <say>der Mann</say> | man / husband | <say>**Mann**</say> ≈ "man" |
| <say>die Frau</say> | woman / wife | |
| <say>der Ehemann</say> | husband (formal) | <say>Ehe</say> = marriage |
| <say>die Ehefrau</say> | wife (formal) | |
| <say>die Geschwister</say> | siblings | bro + sis, lumped together |
| <say>der Onkel</say> | uncle | <say>**Onkel**</say> ≈ "uncle" |
| <say>die Tante</say> | aunt | <say>**Tante**</say> ≈ "auntie" |
| <say>der Cousin</say> | cousin (male) | borrowed from French — same as English |
| <say>die Cousine</say> | cousin (female) | |
| <say>das Baby</say> | baby | direct steal from English |
| <say>die Familie</say> | family | <say>**Familie**</say> ≈ "family" |

> 22 words. Half of them you already know thanks to shared Germanic roots. Run your eyes through, say each one out loud. Then cover the table and recall in German: mother, brother, grandfather, aunt, son.

## Part 4: Language scales

Scales work like piano scales. Run through them out loud, changing only the pronoun. Goal: automatic recall.

### Scale 1 — sein + profession

```
Ich bin Student
Du bist Student
Er ist Student
Sie ist Studentin
Wir sind Studenten
Ihr seid Studenten
Sie sind Studenten
```

Repeat 3 times. On the fourth — with eyes closed.

### Scale 2 — haben + family member

```
Ich habe einen Bruder
Du hast einen Bruder
Er hat einen Bruder
Sie hat einen Bruder
Wir haben einen Bruder
Ihr habt einen Bruder
Sie haben einen Bruder
```

Repeat 3 times. On the fourth — swap "<say>einen Bruder</say>" for "<say>eine Schwester</say>" and run it again.

### Scale 3 — sein + adjective

```
Ich bin müde
Du bist müde
Er ist müde
Sie ist müde
Wir sind müde
Ihr seid müde
Sie sind müde
```

> Swap <say>**müde**</say> (tired) for <say>**hungrig**</say> (hungry — cognate: hungry!), then <say>**glücklich**</say> (happy). Three runs, three adjectives.

## Part 5: Language matrix (Q-A drills)

The matrix is ready-made question→answer blocks. Drill until you answer before you think.

### Matrix 1 — "You ↔ I"

```
Bist du Student? – Ja, ich bin Student
Bist du müde? – Ja, ich bin müde
Bist du aus Berlin? – Ja, ich bin aus Berlin
Hast du einen Bruder? – Ja, ich habe einen Bruder
Hast du Kinder? – Ja, ich habe Kinder
Hast du Zeit? – Ja, ich habe Zeit
```

### Matrix 2 — Third persons

```
Ist er Arzt? – Ja, er ist Arzt
Ist sie aus Hamburg? – Ja, sie ist aus Hamburg
Hat er eine Schwester? – Ja, er hat eine Schwester
Hat sie Kinder? – Ja, sie hat zwei Kinder
Sind sie verheiratet? – Ja, sie sind verheiratet
Haben sie einen Sohn? – Ja, sie haben einen Sohn
```

### Matrix 3 — Situational (family)

```
Hast du Geschwister? – Ja, ich habe einen Bruder und eine Schwester
Wie alt ist dein Bruder? – Er ist fünfzehn Jahre alt
Ist deine Mutter Lehrerin? – Nein, sie ist Ärztin
Haben deine Großeltern einen Hund? – Ja, sie haben einen Hund
Hast du einen Onkel? – Ja, ich habe einen Onkel in München
Ist dein Vater aus Deutschland? – Nein, er ist aus Österreich
```

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
SEIN (to be)             HABEN (to have)
ich    bin               ich    habe
du     bist              du     hast
er/sie ist               er/sie hat       (ist ≈ is, hat ≈ has)
wir    sind              wir    haben
ihr    seid              ihr    habt
Sie    sind              Sie    haben

Age = sein!         Ich bin 20 Jahre alt.   (same as English: "I AM 20")
"I have" = haben!   Ich habe einen Bruder.

FAMILY (all near-cognates):
Vater/father  Mutter/mother  Bruder/brother  Schwester/sister
Sohn/son  Tochter/daughter  Kind/child  Eltern/parents
Großeltern (great-parents)  Onkel/uncle  Tante/auntie
Cousin/Cousine  Familie/family

Watch: einen Bruder (masc. object form) — Akkusativ. Full story in Lesson 9.
```
