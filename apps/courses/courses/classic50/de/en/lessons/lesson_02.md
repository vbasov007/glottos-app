# Lesson 2: Personal pronouns. Numbers 21–1000
**Vocabulary: Introductions — name, age, country, city**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — slowly, consciously, analyzing every sound
3. **Speed up** — repeat until the phrases fly out on their own

> By now you can read German and count to 20. Time to add pronouns and numbers up to 1000.
> Spoiler: Germans count backwards. Seriously. 25 = "five-and-twenty". Remember the old English nursery rhyme "four and twenty blackbirds baked in a pie"? Same logic. English dropped it. German kept it.

---

## Part 1: The eight pronouns

Eight words. Learn them — and you can talk about anyone.

| Pronoun | English | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| <say>**ich**</say> | I | <say>**Ich** bin Katja.</say> |
| <say>**du**</say> | you (singular, informal) | <say>**Du** bist jung.</say> |
| <say>**er**</say> | he | <say>**Er** wohnt in Berlin.</say> |
| <say>**sie**</say> | she | <say>**Sie** kommt aus London.</say> |
| <say>**es**</say> | it | <say>**Es** ist kalt.</say> |
| <say>**wir**</say> | we | <say>**Wir** lernen Deutsch.</say> |
| <say>**ihr**</say> | you (plural, informal — "y'all") | <say>**Ihr** seid schnell.</say> |
| <say>**Sie**</say> | you (formal, sing. or plural) | <say>**Sie** sprechen gut Deutsch.</say> |

Cognate alert: <say>**ich**</say> ≈ "I" (via Old English *ic*). <say>**wir**</say> ≈ "we". <say>**mein**</say> later = "mine". You share more DNA with German than you think.

### du or Sie?

Simple. <say>**Du**</say> = friends, kids, family, peers. <say>**Sie**</say> = strangers, your boss, the cashier, anyone in a tie.

Think of it as the old "thou vs you" English used to have. Quakers still say "thou". Germans never gave it up — they just split it into <say>du</say>/<say>Sie</say>. When in doubt, say <say>**Sie**</say>. A German will offer "<say>du</say>" when they're ready.

> **Trap!** The word <say>**sie**</say> shows up THREE times with different meanings:
> — <say>**sie**</say> (lowercase s) = she: *<say>Sie trinkt Kaffee.</say>* — She drinks coffee.
> — <say>**sie**</say> (lowercase s, plural) = they: *<say>Sie trinken Kaffee.</say>* — They drink coffee.
> — <say>**Sie**</say> (uppercase S) = you (formal): *<say>Sie trinken Kaffee.</say>* — You drink coffee.
> How do you tell "they" from formal "you"? In writing — the capital letter. In speech — context. Don't panic; you'll feel it after a week.

## Part 2: Numbers 21–100

### The main principle: Germans count backwards

In English: twenty-five. Tens first, then units.

In German: <say>**fünf**und**zwanzig**</say>. Units first, then tens. With <say>**und**</say> (and) in between.

25 = <say>fünf</say> + <say>und</say> + <say>zwanzig</say> = "five-and-twenty"

Not a joke. Real. And you'll get used to it in a couple of days. Remember the nursery rhyme: "Sing a song of sixpence... four and twenty blackbirds." That's literally how Germans still talk.

### The tens

| Number | Word | What you see |
|--------|------|--------------|
| 20 | <say>**zwanzig**</say> | special word |
| 30 | <say>**dreißig**</say> | drei + ßig (NOT -zig!) |
| 40 | <say>**vierzig**</say> | vier + **zig** |
| 50 | <say>**fünfzig**</say> | fünf + **zig** |
| 60 | <say>**sechzig**</say> | sech + **zig** (no s!) |
| 70 | <say>**siebzig**</say> | sieb + **zig** (no en!) |
| 80 | <say>**achtzig**</say> | acht + **zig** |
| 90 | <say>**neunzig**</say> | neun + **zig** |

> **Trap!** 30 is <say>drei**ßig**</say>, not <say>dreizig</say>. The only ten ending in **-ßig** instead of **-zig**.
> 60 and 70 drop letters again: <say>sech**zig**</say> (not <say>sechszig</say>), <say>sieb**zig**</say> (not <say>siebenzig</say>). Same lazy Germans from Lesson 1 — they don't want to pile up consonants.

Cognate: German **-zig** ≈ English "-ty" (twenty, thirty, forty). Same Germanic root.

### Compound numbers 21–99

Formula: **unit + und + ten**

| Number | Breakdown | Word |
|--------|-----------|------|
| 21 | 1 + und + 20 | <say>**ein**und**zwanzig**</say> |
| 34 | 4 + und + 30 | <say>**vier**und**dreißig**</say> |
| 47 | 7 + und + 40 | <say>**sieben**und**vierzig**</say> |
| 56 | 6 + und + 50 | <say>**sechs**und**fünfzig**</say> |
| 68 | 8 + und + 60 | <say>**acht**und**sechzig**</say> |
| 73 | 3 + und + 70 | <say>**drei**und**siebzig**</say> |
| 85 | 5 + und + 80 | <say>**fünf**und**achtzig**</say> |
| 99 | 9 + und + 90 | <say>**neun**und**neunzig**</say> |

Round tens (20, 30, 40...) — no <say>und</say>. Just the word alone.

## Part 3: Numbers 100–1000

### Hundreds

| Number | Word |
|--------|------|
| 100 | <say>**(ein)hundert**</say> |
| 200 | <say>**zweihundert**</say> |
| 300 | <say>**dreihundert**</say> |
| 400 | <say>**vierhundert**</say> |
| 500 | <say>**fünfhundert**</say> |
| 600 | <say>**sechshundert**</say> |
| 700 | <say>**siebenhundert**</say> |
| 800 | <say>**achthundert**</say> |
| 900 | <say>**neunhundert**</say> |
| 1000 | <say>**(ein)tausend**</say> |

For 100 just say <say>**hundert**</say>. "<say>Einhundert</say>" — only when you want to stress "exactly one hundred, not two".

Cognates: <say>**hundert**</say> ≈ "hundred". <say>**tausend**</say> ≈ "thousand". Free vocabulary.

### Compound numbers — building blocks

Order: **hundreds + unit + und + ten**

Yes, units still come before tens. Hundreds go at the very start.

| Number | Breakdown | Word |
|--------|-----------|------|
| 115 | 100 + 15 | <say>hundert**fünfzehn**</say> |
| 243 | 200 + 3-und-40 | <say>zweihundert**dreiundvierzig**</say> |
| 671 | 600 + 1-und-70 | <say>sechshundert**einundsiebzig**</say> |
| 999 | 900 + 9-und-90 | <say>neunhundert**neunundneunzig**</say> |

> **Anchor.** Memorize the order: hundreds — unit — und — ten.
> 738 = <say>sieben</say> (hundreds) + <say>hundert</say> + <say>acht</say> (unit) + <say>und</say> + <say>dreißig</say> (ten) = <say>siebenhundertachtunddreißig</say>.
> Long? Yes. But logical. Build it piece by piece.

## Part 4: Vocabulary — Introductions

| German | English | Note |
|--------|---------|------|
| <say>Wie heißt du?</say> | What's your name? | informal |
| <say>Ich heiße…</say> | My name is… | lit. "I am called…" |
| <say>Wie alt bist du?</say> | How old are you? | "How old are you" — literal |
| <say>Ich bin ... Jahre alt</say> | I am ... years old | <say>Jahre</say> = years (≈ "years"!) |
| <say>Woher kommst du?</say> | Where are you from? | <say>woher</say> = "from where" |
| <say>Ich komme aus…</say> | I come from… | + country |
| <say>Wo wohnst du?</say> | Where do you live? | |
| <say>Ich wohne in…</say> | I live in… | + city. <say>wohnen</say> ≈ "to dwell" |
| <say>das Land</say> | country | ≈ "land" |
| <say>die Stadt</say> | city | |
| <say>England</say> | England | |
| <say>Amerika</say> / <say>die USA</say> | America / the USA | |
| <say>Deutschland</say> | Germany | lit. "land of the Germans" |
| <say>der Beruf</say> | profession | |
| <say>Was machst du?</say> | What do you do? | <say>machen</say> ≈ "make" |
| <say>Ich bin Student</say> | I'm a student | |
| <say>Ich arbeite</say> | I work | <say>arbeiten</say> = work |
| <say>und</say> | and | ≈ "and" (free!) |
| <say>oder</say> | or | ≈ "or" |
| <say>auch</say> | also, too | |
| <say>Wie bitte?</say> | Pardon? Come again? | when you didn't catch it |

## Part 5: Exercises

### Exercise 1. Write the numbers in words

Spell out the digit as a German word. No peeking — build it yourself first:

1. 27
2. 53
3. 98
4. 41
5. 66
6. 115
7. 382
8. 740

10. 999

### Exercise 2. Translate sentences about yourself

Translate from English to German:

1. What's your name? (informal)
2. My name is Sarah.
3. How old are you?
4. I am 24 years old.
5. Where are you from?
6. I'm from England.
7. Where do you live?
8. I live in Berlin.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Wie heißt du?*
2. *Ich heiße Sarah.*
3. *Wie alt bist du?*
4. *Ich bin vierundzwanzig Jahre alt.*
5. *Woher kommst du?*
6. *Ich komme aus England.*
7. *Wo wohnst du?*
8. *Ich wohne in Berlin.*

</details>

### Exercise 3. Read the numbers out loud

Read each one aloud in German. Don't rush — pronounce each part:

> 45, 73, 21, 88, 36, 150, 512, 67, 99, 304, 261, 830, 1000

Then go in reverse order. Then call out your phone number in German, two digits at a time.

### Exercise 4. Fill in the right pronoun

Choose ich, du, er, sie, es, wir, ihr, or Sie:

1. ___ komme aus Amerika. (I)
2. ___ wohnst in Hamburg. (you, informal)
3. ___ ist Studentin. (she)
4. ___ lernen zusammen Deutsch. (we)
5. ___ ist warm heute. (impersonal — "it")
6. ___ sprechen sehr gut Deutsch, Herr Braun. (you, formal)
7. ___ seid aus Österreich. (you, plural)
8. ___ arbeitet in München. (he)

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Ich* komme aus Amerika.
2. *Du* wohnst in Hamburg.
3. *Sie* ist Studentin.
4. *Wir* lernen zusammen Deutsch.
5. *Es* ist warm heute.
6. *Sie* sprechen sehr gut Deutsch, Herr Braun.
7. *Ihr* seid aus Österreich.
8. *Er* arbeitet in München.

</details>

### Exercise 5. Translate the dialog

Translate the whole dialog into German. Then say it out loud:

— Hi! What's your name?
— My name is Max. And you?
— I'm Lena. Where are you from, Max?
— I'm from Germany. I live in Cologne. And you?
— I'm from America. I am 23 years old.
— And I'm 27. Nice to meet you!
— You too!

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

— *Hallo! Wie heißt du?*
— *Ich heiße Max. Und du?*
— *Ich bin Lena. Woher kommst du, Max?*
— *Ich komme aus Deutschland. Ich wohne in Köln. Und du?*
— *Ich komme aus Amerika. Ich bin dreiundzwanzig Jahre alt.*
— *Und ich bin siebenundzwanzig. Freut mich!*
— *Mich auch!*

</details>
