# Lesson 7: Regular -ARE verbs
**Vocabulary: first-conjugation everyday-action verbs**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — the main point: one model, six endings, **~80% of all Italian verbs**.
2. **Train the scales** — run one verb through every person until it's automatic. The six endings should fly out in the right order without a single thought.
3. **Notice the spelling adjustments** — verbs in *-care/-gare* add an **h** to keep the hard sound; verbs in *-ciare/-giare* drop an extra **i**. This is spelling, not new grammar.
4. **Don't forget pro-drop** — the pronoun can (and usually should) be left out. The ending already tells you who's doing the action.

> This is the **engine** of Italian. Automate the six *-are* endings and you can already speak most Italian verbs. It's the single most profitable investment of time in the whole of Block 1.

---

## Part 1: What -ARE conjugation is

The infinitive of a regular first-conjugation verb ends in **-are**: <say>parlare</say> (to speak), <say>lavorare</say> (to work), <say>mangiare</say> (to eat), <say>abitare</say> (to live, to dwell), <say>comprare</say> (to buy).

To conjugate, **chop off -are** (you get the stem: *parl-, lavor-, mangi-*) and **add six endings**:

| Person | Ending | *parlare* | *lavorare* |
|---|---|---|---|
| io (I) | **-o** | <say>parlo</say> | <say>lavoro</say> |
| tu (you sg.) | **-i** | <say>parli</say> | <say>lavori</say> |
| lui/lei (he/she) | **-a** | <say>parla</say> | <say>lavora</say> |
| noi (we) | **-iamo** | <say>parliamo</say> | <say>lavoriamo</say> |
| voi (you pl.) | **-ate** | <say>parlate</say> | <say>lavorate</say> |
| loro (they) | **-ano** | <say>parlano</say> | <say>lavorano</say> |

> **Memorise the six endings as a chant:** **-o, -i, -a, -iamo, -ate, -ano**.

> **The English-speaker windfall:** English barely conjugates at all — "I speak, you speak, he speak**s**, we speak, they speak" — one tiny *-s* on third-person singular and that's it. Italian gives each person its own ending. Good news: that means the ending tells you who's doing the action, so the pronoun is normally dropped (see Part 2). Bad news: you have to drill six forms instead of two. Worth it — six endings unlock thousands of verbs.

### Stress — where the weight of the word sits

Stress in *-are* verbs **doesn't follow English intuition**: it stays where it was in the infinitive, and in the *loro* form it lands three syllables from the end:

- <say>PARlo, PARli, PARla, parLIAmo, parLAte, PARlano</say>
- <say>laVOro, laVOri, laVOra, lavoRIAmo, lavoRAte, laVOrano</say>

> **Watch out:** the *loro* form (*parlano, lavorano*) has stress far from the end, on the third-to-last syllable — the "double-tail" effect. The classic English-speaker trap is to say *parlAno* — but the right pronunciation is *PARlano*. (Compare: <say>TElephono</say>-style three-from-end stress you met in L1.)

## Part 2: Pro-drop — the pronoun gets dropped

In Italian the subject pronoun **is normally unnecessary** — the verb ending has already said it:

| With pronoun (allowed) | Without pronoun (usual) |
|---|---|
| <say>Io parlo italiano.</say> | <say>Parlo italiano.</say> |
| <say>Tu lavori a Roma.</say> | <say>Lavori a Roma.</say> |
| <say>Lei lavora qui?</say> | <say>Lavora qui?</say> |

> **For English speakers this is the opposite of your native habit.** English forces a subject: "speak Italian" alone is wrong. "Parlo italiano" alone is the **normal** way to say "I speak Italian". Don't bolt *io* onto the front out of habit — sounds clunky.

> **When you do still use the pronoun:** for **contrast** ("**I** work, **she** studies" = <say>Io lavoro, lei studia</say>) or for emphasis. In neutral speech — drop it.

---

## Part 3: Question and negation — no auxiliary verbs

Unlike English, Italian **does not use an auxiliary** for questions and negatives:

### Question — just **intonation**

| Statement | Question |
|---|---|
| <say>Parli italiano.</say> | <say>Parli italiano?</say> |
| <say>Anna lavora a Roma.</say> | <say>Anna lavora a Roma?</say> |
| <say>Mangiate la pizza.</say> | <say>Mangiate la pizza?</say> |

No *do/does*. No word-order inversion. Just a question mark in writing and a rising voice in speech.

> **The English speaker's gift:** you can throw away the whole *do/does* machine. "Do you speak Italian?" — for English it's natural, for Italian it would feel weirdly elaborate. <say>Parli italiano?</say> — done. This shortcut alone makes Italian questions 30% lighter than English ones.

### Negation — the word **non** before the verb

| Statement | Negation |
|---|---|
| <say>Parlo italiano.</say> | <say>Non parlo italiano.</say> |
| <say>Lavora a Roma.</say> | <say>Non lavora a Roma.</say> |
| <say>Mangiamo carne.</say> | <say>Non mangiamo carne.</say> |

Again, nothing "auxiliary": just <say>non</say> before the conjugated verb. (More detail in L9.)

## Part 4: Spelling adjustments

This isn't new grammar — it's spelling. But if you ignore it, words come out wrong.

### Verbs in *-care* and *-gare* — add **h** before **e** or **i**

Take <say>cercare</say> (to look for): the stem is *cerc-*. To keep the hard "k" sound where the ending starts with **e** or **i** (i.e. in *tu* and *noi*), you insert an **h**:

| Person | *cercare* | *pagare* |
|---|---|---|
| io | <say>cerco</say> | <say>pago</say> |
| tu | <say>cerchi</say> ← **h**! | <say>paghi</say> ← **h**! |
| lui/lei | <say>cerca</say> | <say>paga</say> |
| noi | <say>cerchiamo</say> ← **h**! | <say>paghiamo</say> ← **h**! |
| voi | <say>cercate</say> | <say>pagate</say> |
| loro | <say>cercano</say> | <say>pagano</say> |

> **Why:** without the **h**, *cerci, cerciamo* would read "cher-chee, cher-chee-AH-mo" (soft *c*) — but the verb needs "ker-kee, ker-kee-AH-mo" (hard *c*). Back to L1: *ce/ci* is soft, *che/chi* is hard.

### Verbs in *-ciare* and *-giare* — drop the extra **i**

Take <say>mangiare</say> (to eat): the stem is *mangi-*. The *tu* ending is *-i*; the *noi* ending is *-iamo*. Italian doesn't write two **i**s in a row:

| Person | *mangiare* | *cominciare* |
|---|---|---|
| io | <say>mangio</say> | <say>comincio</say> |
| tu | <say>mangi</say> (not *mangii*) | <say>cominci</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>mangia</say> | <say>comincia</say> |
| noi | <say>mangiamo</say> (not *mangiiamo*) | <say>cominciamo</say> |
| voi | <say>mangiate</say> | <say>cominciate</say> |
| loro | <say>mangiano</say> | <say>cominciano</say> |

> **Notice:** the *i* of the stem is already keeping the *g* or *c* soft — nothing else for that *i* to do. So it gets "swallowed" by the ending.

### Verbs in *-iare* without a soft consonant — same behaviour

<say>studiare</say> (to study): *studio, studi, studia, studiamo, studiate, studiano* — one **i**, never two.

## Language scales

### Scale 1 — *parlare* through every person

> <say>Io parlo italiano.</say>
> <say>Tu parli italiano.</say>
> <say>Lui parla italiano.</say>
> <say>Lei parla italiano.</say>
> <say>Noi parliamo italiano.</say>
> <say>Voi parlate italiano.</say>
> <say>Loro parlano italiano.</say>

Now repeat without the pronouns (the normal way): <say>Parlo italiano. Parli italiano. Parla italiano…</say>

### Scale 2 — *lavorare* through every person

> <say>Lavoro a Roma.</say>
> <say>Lavori a Roma.</say>
> <say>Lavora a Roma.</say>
> <say>Lavoriamo a Roma.</say>
> <say>Lavorate a Roma.</say>
> <say>Lavorano a Roma.</say>

Target: 10 seconds. No pronouns.

### Scale 3 — *mangiare* (note the single **i**)

> <say>Mangio la pizza.</say>
> <say>Mangi la pizza.</say>
> <say>Mangia la pizza.</say>
> <say>Mangiamo la pizza.</say>
> <say>Mangiate la pizza.</say>
> <say>Mangiano la pizza.</say>

### Scale 4 — *cercare* (note the **h**)

> <say>Cerco un libro.</say>
> <say>Cerchi un libro.</say> ← **h**!
> <say>Cerca un libro.</say>
> <say>Cerchiamo un libro.</say> ← **h**!
> <say>Cercate un libro.</say>
> <say>Cercano un libro.</say>

### Scale 5 — negation with *non*

> <say>Non parlo francese.</say>
> <say>Non lavori il sabato.</say>
> <say>Non mangia carne.</say>
> <say>Non studiamo il tedesco.</say>
> <say>Non comprate vino.</say>
> <say>Non ascoltano musica.</say>

### Exercise 3. Read aloud — watch the stress

Say each, emphasising the stressed syllable (especially in the *loro* form):

1. parlano
2. lavorano
3. abitano
4. mangiano
5. studiano
6. cantano
7. ascoltano
8. arrivano

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *PARlano* — on the first syllable!
2. *laVOrano* — second
3. *ABitano* — first
4. *MANgiano* — first
5. *STUdiano* — first
6. *CANtano* — first
7. *aSCOLtano* — second
8. *arRIvano* — second

### Exercise 4. Translate

1. I speak Italian.
2. Do you work in Rome?
3. Anna lives in Milan.
4. We eat pasta in the evening.
5. The children play football.
6. What are you (pl.) studying?
7. I'm looking for a good restaurant.
8. Marco doesn't pay for the coffee.
9. They listen to music.
10. I don't smoke.

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *Parlo italiano.* (no *io*)
2. *Lavori a Roma?*
3. *Anna abita a Milano.*
4. *Mangiamo la pasta la sera.* (or: *…di sera*)
5. *I bambini giocano a calcio.*
6. *Cosa studiate?* (or: *Che cosa studiate?*)
7. *Cerco un buon ristorante.*
8. *Marco non paga il caffè.*
9. *Ascoltano musica.* (or: *Loro ascoltano la musica.*)
10. *Non fumo.*

Notice: no *do/does*. No "-ing" form (Italian "I am eating" = simple *mangio*, you'll meet the continuous form much later). The English helper machinery just isn't there.

</details>

### Exercise 5. Spot the mistake

1. Io parli italiano.
2. Anna lavorano a Roma.
3. Noi mangiiamo la pizza.
4. Tu cerci un libro.
5. Voi parlate italiano? — Si, parlate italiano.
6. Loro parla francese.
7. Io paga il conto.

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *Io **parlo** italiano.* (1sg → *-o*)
2. *Anna **lavora** a Roma.* (3sg → *-a*)
3. *Noi **mangiamo** la pizza.* (one **i**!)
4. *Tu **cerchi** un libro.* (*-care* → **h**!)
5. *Voi parlate italiano? — Sì, **parliamo** italiano.* (answering for "we": *voi → noi*)
6. *Loro **parlano** francese.* (3pl → *-ano*)
7. *Io **pago** il conto.* (1sg → *-o*, no **h** because the ending isn't *e/i*)

</details>

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
REGULAR -ARE VERBS — ~80% OF ALL ITALIAN VERBS

  Chop off -are from the infinitive, add:
    io       -o
    tu       -i
    lui/lei  -a
    noi      -iamo
    voi      -ate
    loro     -ano

  PARLARE → parlo, parli, parla, parliamo, parlate, parlano

STRESS:
  Does not drift to the end. In LORO form — third from end:
    PARlano, laVOrano, ABitano, MANgiano

PRO-DROP:
  The pronoun is dropped — the ending already says who's acting.
  Use the pronoun only for contrast or emphasis.
  (English forces "I", "you" — Italian does not.)

QUESTION AND NEGATION:
  Question — just intonation:        Parli italiano?
  Negation — non before the verb:    Non parlo italiano.
  NO do/does, no auxiliary, no inversion.

SPELLING ADJUSTMENTS:
  -care / -gare:  add h before e or i (in tu and noi)
                  cercare → cerchi, cerchiamo
                  pagare  → paghi, paghiamo
                  giocare → giochi, giochiamo
                  Why: keep the hard "k" / "g".

  -ciare / -giare / -iare: drop the extra i (in tu and noi)
                  mangiare    → mangi, mangiamo (not mangii, mangiiamo)
                  cominciare  → cominci, cominciamo
                  studiare    → studi, studiamo

KEY -ARE VERBS:
  parlare, lavorare, abitare, studiare, mangiare, comprare,
  cercare, trovare, guardare, ascoltare, cantare, ballare,
  giocare, aiutare, chiamare, amare, imparare, insegnare,
  pagare, arrivare, tornare, cucinare, viaggiare, incontrare.

FALSE FRIENDS:
  camminare = to walk (not "cram"!)
  fumare    = to smoke (not "to fume" in anger)
```
