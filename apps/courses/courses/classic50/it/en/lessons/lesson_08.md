# Lesson 8: -ERE and -IRE verbs; six key irregulars
**Vocabulary: second- and third-conjugation verbs; *fare, andare, venire, stare, dare, dire***

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — three conjugations now covered: *-are* (L7), and now *-ere* and *-ire*. *-Ire* has two subtypes: the "normal" one (*partire*) and the **-isc-** type (*finire*).
2. **Train the scales** — for each group, run one verb through every person.
3. **Memorise six irregulars** — *fare, andare, venire, stare, dare, dire*. These are the most frequent verbs in Italian. You can't say much without them.
4. **Notice** — the *io* form is the base from which the subjunctive will grow in L31. Especially for irregular verbs. So learn the *io* forms well now — the investment pays off later.

> The *io* form of the present is the foundation. Half the future tenses will sprout from it. Strong memory here pays interest in Blocks 4 and 5.

---

## Part 1: Regular -ERE

Infinitives in **-ere**: <say>prendere</say> (to take), <say>scrivere</say> (to write), <say>vedere</say> (to see), <say>leggere</say> (to read). Chop off *-ere*, add:

| Person | Ending | *prendere* | *scrivere* |
|---|---|---|---|
| io | **-o** | <say>prendo</say> | <say>scrivo</say> |
| tu | **-i** | <say>prendi</say> | <say>scrivi</say> |
| lui/lei | **-e** | <say>prende</say> | <say>scrive</say> |
| noi | **-iamo** | <say>prendiamo</say> | <say>scriviamo</say> |
| voi | **-ete** | <say>prendete</say> | <say>scrivete</say> |
| loro | **-ono** | <say>prendono</say> | <say>scrivono</say> |

> **Compare with -are:** the endings are almost the same, with three differences: *lui/lei* — **-e** (not **-a**), *voi* — **-ete** (not **-ate**), *loro* — **-ono** (not **-ano**).

### Stress

Same logic: in the *loro* form, stress lives far from the end:

- <say>PRENdo, PRENdi, PRENde, prenDIAmo, prenDEte, PRENdono</say>
- <say>SCRIvo, SCRIvi, SCRIve, scriVIAmo, scriVEte, SCRIvono</say>

> **Notice:** *prendono* — PREN-do-no, not "pren-OH-no". Likewise *scrivono*, *leggono*, *vedono*.

## Part 2: Regular -IRE — normal type

Infinitives in **-ire**: <say>partire</say> (to leave), <say>dormire</say> (to sleep), <say>aprire</say> (to open), <say>sentire</say> (to hear, to feel). Chop off *-ire*, add:

| Person | Ending | *partire* | *dormire* |
|---|---|---|---|
| io | **-o** | <say>parto</say> | <say>dormo</say> |
| tu | **-i** | <say>parti</say> | <say>dormi</say> |
| lui/lei | **-e** | <say>parte</say> | <say>dorme</say> |
| noi | **-iamo** | <say>partiamo</say> | <say>dormiamo</say> |
| voi | **-ite** | <say>partite</say> | <say>dormite</say> |
| loro | **-ono** | <say>partono</say> | <say>dormono</say> |

> **Notice:** the only difference from *-ere* is in *voi*: **-ite** (not **-ete**). The other five endings are identical.

---

## Part 3: Regular -IRE — the *-isc-* type

The majority of *-ire* verbs (about two-thirds) conjugate with an **inserted *-isc-*** in the *io, tu, lui/lei, loro* forms. In *noi* and *voi* there is **no insert**.

> **English speakers, brace yourselves:** English has **nothing remotely like this**. There's no English verb that grows an extra syllable in four forms and not in two. You just have to drill it. The good news: once you know it, it's predictable.

The verb <say>finire</say> (to finish):

| Person | *finire* | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| io | <say>finisco</say> | stem + **-isc** + **-o** |
| tu | <say>finisci</say> | stem + **-isc** + **-i** |
| lui/lei | <say>finisce</say> | stem + **-isc** + **-e** |
| noi | <say>finiamo</say> | like a normal *-ire* |
| voi | <say>finite</say> | like a normal *-ire* |
| loro | <say>finiscono</say> | stem + **-isc** + **-ono** |

The same pattern for:

- <say>capire</say> (to understand) → *capisco, capisci, capisce, capiamo, capite, capiscono*
- <say>preferire</say> (to prefer) → *preferisco, preferisci, preferisce, preferiamo, preferite, preferiscono*
- <say>pulire</say> (to clean) → *pulisco, pulisci, pulisce, puliamo, pulite, puliscono*
- <say>spedire</say> (to send) → *spedisco…*

> **How do you know which type?** A dictionary marks *-ire* verbs: either "(isc)" — meaning insert is required — or unmarked, meaning it's the normal type (*partire, dormire*). The most common "normal" *-ire* verbs are: *partire, dormire, sentire, aprire, offrire, soffrire, servire, seguire*. Almost every other *-ire* verb is *-isc-*.

> **Pronunciation:** in the forms *finisci, finisce* the letters *sc* before *i/e* are soft: "fi-NEE-shee, fi-NEE-sheh". In *finisco, finiscono* — hard: "fi-NEES-koh, fi-NEES-koh-noh". Same L1 rule: *sc* before *e/i* is soft (English "sh"); before *o/a/u* — hard (English "sk").

## Part 4: Six key irregulars

These six verbs are the skeleton of spoken Italian. Without them you can barely say anything meaningful.

### *fare* (to do, to make)

| Person | Form |
|---|---|
| io | <say>faccio</say> |
| tu | <say>fai</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>fa</say> |
| noi | <say>facciamo</say> |
| voi | <say>fate</say> |
| loro | <say>fanno</say> |

Usage: <say>Cosa fai?</say> — "What are you doing?"; <say>Faccio il medico</say> — "I'm a doctor" (literally "I make/do the doctor"); <say>Fa freddo</say> — "It's cold"; <say>Facciamo una passeggiata</say> — "We're going for a walk".

> **Note on profession:** English says "I'm a doctor" — *to be*. Italian commonly says <say>Faccio il medico</say> — *to do* + the article. You can also say <say>Sono medico</say>; both work. Italian leans on *fare* here.

### *andare* (to go)

| Person | Form |
|---|---|
| io | <say>vado</say> |
| tu | <say>vai</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>va</say> |
| noi | <say>andiamo</say> |
| voi | <say>andate</say> |
| loro | <say>vanno</say> |

> **Notice:** *io, tu, lui/lei, loro* — the stem changes to *va-/vad-*. *Noi, voi* — regular forms from *andare*. The same split happens with *venire, dare, stare*.

<say>Vado a Roma. Vai al lavoro? Va bene! Andiamo!</say> ("Let's go!")

### *venire* (to come)

| Person | Form |
|---|---|
| io | <say>vengo</say> |
| tu | <say>vieni</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>viene</say> |
| noi | <say>veniamo</say> |
| voi | <say>venite</say> |
| loro | <say>vengono</say> |

<say>Da dove vieni?</say> — "Where are you from?"; <say>Vengo dalla Russia</say> — "I'm from Russia".

### *stare* (to be, to stay, to feel)

| Person | Form |
|---|---|
| io | <say>sto</say> |
| tu | <say>stai</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>sta</say> |
| noi | <say>stiamo</say> |
| voi | <say>state</say> |
| loro | <say>stanno</say> |

> **Compare with *essere* (L4):** *stare* means "to be" in the sense of "to feel, to be located, to be doing" (temporary state, place). *Essere* means "to be" in the sense of "to be (something permanently)".
>
> - <say>Come stai?</say> — "How are you?" (feeling)
> - <say>Sto bene.</say> — "I'm fine."
> - <say>Sono italiano.</say> — "I'm Italian." (essere — permanent)
>
> English collapses both meanings into "to be". Italian splits them — like Spanish *ser/estar*.

### *dare* (to give)

| Person | Form |
|---|---|
| io | <say>do</say> |
| tu | <say>dai</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>dà</say> (with the accent, to distinguish it from *da* — "from") |
| noi | <say>diamo</say> |
| voi | <say>date</say> |
| loro | <say>danno</say> |

<say>Do un libro a Marco.</say> — "I give a book to Marco."

### *dire* (to say, to tell)

| Person | Form |
|---|---|
| io | <say>dico</say> |
| tu | <say>dici</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>dice</say> |
| noi | <say>diciamo</say> |
| voi | <say>dite</say> |
| loro | <say>dicono</say> |

> **Notice:** *dire* conjugates like an *-ere* verb (even though its infinitive ends in *-ire*). That's because the Latin original was *dicere*. Historical memory.

> **The difference between *parlare* and *dire*:**
> - *parlare* = "to speak" (in general, a language, with someone): <say>Parlo italiano. Parlo con Anna.</say>
> - *dire* = "to say, to tell" (conveying content): <say>Dico la verità. Dico che…</say>
>
> English smashes both into "speak/say/tell" with subtle distinctions; Italian is cleaner here.

## Part 5: Comparison table of endings

| Person | -ARE | -ERE | -IRE (normal) | -IRE (-isc-) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| io | -o | -o | -o | -**isco** |
| tu | -i | -i | -i | -**isci** |
| lui/lei | -a | -e | -e | -**isce** |
| noi | -iamo | -iamo | -iamo | -iamo |
| voi | -**ate** | -**ete** | -**ite** | -ite |
| loro | -ano | -ono | -ono | -**iscono** |

> **Three things to memorise:** *lui/lei* — *-a* for *-are*, *-e* for the rest. *Voi* — vowel matches the infinitive (a/e/i). *Loro* — *-ano* for *-are*, *-ono* for the rest.

## Language scales

### Scale 1 — *prendere* (-ere)

> <say>Prendo un caffè.</say>
> <say>Prendi un caffè.</say>
> <say>Prende un caffè.</say>
> <say>Prendiamo un caffè.</say>
> <say>Prendete un caffè.</say>
> <say>Prendono un caffè.</say>

### Scale 2 — *partire* (-ire normal)

> <say>Parto domani.</say>
> <say>Parti domani.</say>
> <say>Parte domani.</say>
> <say>Partiamo domani.</say>
> <say>Partite domani.</say>
> <say>Partono domani.</say>

### Scale 3 — *finire* (-isc-)

> <say>Finisco alle sei.</say> ← isc
> <say>Finisci alle sei.</say> ← isc
> <say>Finisce alle sei.</say> ← isc
> <say>Finiamo alle sei.</say> ← no isc!
> <say>Finite alle sei.</say> ← no isc!
> <say>Finiscono alle sei.</say> ← isc

### Scale 4 — *andare* (irregular)

> <say>Vado a Roma.</say>
> <say>Vai a Roma.</say>
> <say>Va a Roma.</say>
> <say>Andiamo a Roma.</say>
> <say>Andate a Roma.</say>
> <say>Vanno a Roma.</say>

### Scale 5 — *fare* (irregular)

> <say>Faccio il medico.</say>
> <say>Fai il medico.</say>
> <say>Fa il medico.</say>
> <say>Facciamo i medici.</say>
> <say>Fate i medici.</say>
> <say>Fanno i medici.</say>

### Scale 6 — *stare* and "how are you"

> <say>Come stai? — Sto bene, grazie.</say>
> <say>Come sta Anna? — Sta benissimo.</say>
> <say>Come state? — Stiamo bene.</say>
> <say>Come stanno i bambini? — Stanno male, sono malati.</say>

```
ENDINGS — ALL THREE CONJUGATIONS:

           -ARE     -ERE     -IRE          -IRE (isc)
  io       -o       -o       -o            -ISCO
  tu       -i       -i       -i            -ISCI
  lui/lei  -a       -e       -e            -ISCE
  noi      -iamo    -iamo    -iamo         -iamo

  prendere, scrivere, vedere, leggere, vendere,
  chiudere, perdere, vivere, credere, ricevere,
  mettere, rispondere, chiedere.

  partire, dormire, aprire, sentire, offrire,
  servire, seguire, soffrire.
  Most other -ire verbs take -ISC-.

REGULAR -IRE WITH -ISC- (examples):
  finire, capire, preferire, pulire, spedire,
  costruire, restituire.
  -isc- ONLY in io/tu/lui/loro; NO -isc- in noi/voi.

SIX KEY IRREGULARS:

  FARE:    faccio, fai, fa, facciamo, fate, fanno
  ANDARE:  vado, vai, va, andiamo, andate, vanno
  VENIRE:  vengo, vieni, viene, veniamo, venite, vengono
  STARE:   sto, stai, sta, stiamo, state, stanno
  DARE:    do, dai, dà, diamo, date, danno
  DIRE:    dico, dici, dice, diciamo, dite, dicono

ESSERE vs STARE:
  essere = "to be" (permanent): Sono italiano. È medico.
  stare  = "to be, to feel, to be located": Sto bene. Sta a Roma.
  (English has one "to be"; Italian has two — like Spanish ser/estar.)

PARLARE vs DIRE:
  parlare = to speak (a language, with someone): Parlo italiano.
  dire    = to say/tell (content):                Dico la verità.

-ISC- PRONUNCIATION:
  finisco   hard "sk"
  finisci   soft "shee"
  finisce   soft "sheh"
  finiscono hard "sko-no"
```
