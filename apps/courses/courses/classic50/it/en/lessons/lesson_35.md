# Lesson 35: The imperative. Commands, requests, advice — and why the formal imperative IS congiuntivo
**Vocabulary: instructions, requests, advice, directions; polite formulas**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** the derivation rule — it's one beautiful rule: the formal *Lei* is **literally** the congiuntivo you learned in L31 (3 minutes).
2. **Run the forms out loud** — *tu, Lei, noi, voi, Loro* — until the five-row sequence flows on its own.
3. **Drill with pronouns** — *dimmelo, glielo dica, fammelo vedere*. Pronoun attachment is the main muscle work of this lesson.

> This lesson is the most practical one in Block 4. Buying a coffee, asking directions, hurrying a friend along, giving advice — all of it is imperative.
> The headline Italian fact: half of the imperative forms ARE the congiuntivo you already learned. One set of forms, one more job.
> **For English speakers** there's an extra layer: English has only one "you" command — "Come here!" works for everyone. Italian has separate forms for *tu* (a friend) and *Lei* (a stranger, your boss, your doctor) — and the *Lei* form is the congiuntivo. This is a new social register to learn.

---

## Part 1: The big idea — the five-row table

In Italian, the imperative exists for five persons: *tu, Lei, noi, voi, Loro*. (For *io* and *lui/lei* it would be illogical — you can't command yourself or a third party directly.)

| Person | To whom | Where the form comes from |
|--------|---------|---------------------------|
| **tu** | a friend, a child | special form (see Part 2) |
| **Lei** | formal "you" (one person) | **congiuntivo presente, lui/lei** |
| **noi** | "let's…" | same as present indicative noi |
| **voi** | you (several) | same as present indicative voi |
| **Loro** | formal "you" (several) — rare in everyday speech | **congiuntivo presente, loro** |

### Base table — three regular verbs

| Person | parlare | credere | dormire |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| tu | <say>parla!</say> | <say>credi!</say> | <say>dormi!</say> |
| Lei | <say>parli!</say> | <say>creda!</say> | <say>dorma!</say> |
| noi | <say>parliamo!</say> | <say>crediamo!</say> | <say>dormiamo!</say> |
| voi | <say>parlate!</say> | <say>credete!</say> | <say>dormite!</say> |
| Loro | <say>parlino!</say> | <say>credano!</say> | <say>dormano!</say> |

> **Notice:** the *Lei* and *Loro* rows are exactly the **congiuntivo presente** in the *lui/lei* and *loro* forms. You already know them from L31.

### -IRE with -ISC- (the *finire, capire* type)

| Person | finire |
|--------|--------|
| tu | <say>finisci!</say> |
| Lei | <say>finisca!</say> *(= cong.)* |
| noi | <say>finiamo!</say> |
| voi | <say>finite!</say> |
| Loro | <say>finiscano!</say> *(= cong.)* |

## Part 2: The *tu* form — peculiarities

For the vast majority of regular verbs, the *tu* imperative coincides with the present tense *tu* — **but with one important catch for -ARE**:

| Verb type | tu (present) | tu (imperative) |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| -ARE: parlare | <say>parli</say> | <say>parla!</say> *(ending -a, not -i!)* |
| -ARE: lavorare | <say>lavori</say> | <say>lavora!</say> |
| -ERE: prendere | <say>prendi</say> | <say>prendi!</say> *(same)* |
| -IRE: dormire | <say>dormi</say> | <say>dormi!</say> *(same)* |
| -IRE: finire | <say>finisci</say> | <say>finisci!</say> *(same)* |

> **Main point:** for an **-ARE** verb, the *tu* imperative is **-a**, not **-i**: <say>parla!, mangia!, ascolta!, guarda!, aspetta!</say> This is the only case where the *tu* imperative form differs from the present.

### Short imperative forms for *tu* (tu only!)

Seven high-frequency verbs have **short** *tu* imperative forms:

| Verb | tu | Long form |
|------|-----|-----------|
| andare | <say>va'!</say> (or <say>vai!</say>) | — |
| dare | <say>da'!</say> (or <say>dai!</say>) | — |
| fare | <say>fa'!</say> (or <say>fai!</say>) | — |
| stare | <say>sta'!</say> (or <say>stai!</say>) | — |
| dire | <say>di'!</say> | — |
| essere | <say>sii!</say> | — |
| avere | <say>abbi!</say> | — |

These short forms (with the apostrophe) are **mandatory** when attaching pronouns: *dimmi!, fammi vedere!, dammelo!* — see Part 5.

## Part 3: The headline Italian feature — the formal imperative IS congiuntivo

This is the **heart** of the lesson. In Italian, when addressing someone formally (Lei), commands are built as the congiuntivo presente in the *lui/lei* form:

| English (formal "you") | Italian (Lei) | Where the form comes from |
|------------------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| Speak! | <say>Parli!</say> | cong. presente *lui parli* |
| Listen! | <say>Ascolti!</say> | cong. presente *lui ascolti* |
| Tell me! | <say>Mi dica!</say> | cong. presente *lui dica* |
| Go! | <say>Vada!</say> | cong. presente *lui vada* |
| Come! | <say>Venga!</say> | cong. presente *lui venga* |
| Do it! | <say>Faccia!</say> | cong. presente *lui faccia* |
| Excuse me! | <say>Mi scusi!</say> | cong. presente *lui scusi* |
| Wait! | <say>Aspetti!</say> | cong. presente *lui aspetti* |
| Wait a moment. | <say>Aspetti un momento.</say> | same |
| Stay well! | <say>Sia in salute!</say> | cong. *lui sia* |

> **The logic:** *Mi dica!* literally means "(may he) tell me" — i.e. "(I wish) that you tell me". That's congiuntivo with the sense "let…", turned into a command.
> This is the **most frequent** use of congiuntivo in live speech — every trip to a shop, a café, a doctor's office uses the formal imperative.

### Master table — five persons across six verbs

| | parlare | venire | fare | andare | dire | essere |
|---|---------|--------|------|--------|------|--------|
| tu | <say>parla!</say> | <say>vieni!</say> | <say>fa'!</say> | <say>va'!</say> | <say>di'!</say> | <say>sii!</say> |
| Lei | <say>parli!</say> | <say>venga!</say> | <say>faccia!</say> | <say>vada!</say> | <say>dica!</say> | <say>sia!</say> |
| noi | <say>parliamo!</say> | <say>veniamo!</say> | <say>facciamo!</say> | <say>andiamo!</say> | <say>diciamo!</say> | <say>siamo!</say> |
| voi | <say>parlate!</say> | <say>venite!</say> | <say>fate!</say> | <say>andate!</say> | <say>dite!</say> | <say>siate!</say> |
| Loro | <say>parlino!</say> | <say>vengano!</say> | <say>facciano!</say> | <say>vadano!</say> | <say>dicano!</say> | <say>siano!</say> |

## Part 4: The negative imperative — a special trap for *tu*

The Italian oddity: **the negative imperative for *tu* is the infinitive**, not the imperative form.

| Person | Affirmative | Negative |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| tu | <say>parla!</say> | <say>non parlare!</say> *(infinitive!)* |
| Lei | <say>parli!</say> | <say>non parli!</say> |
| noi | <say>parliamo!</say> | <say>non parliamo!</say> |
| voi | <say>parlate!</say> | <say>non parlate!</say> |
| Loro | <say>parlino!</say> | <say>non parlino!</say> |

| English | Italian |
|---------|---------|
| Don't speak! | <say>Non parlare!</say> |
| Don't do it! | <say>Non fare!</say> |
| Don't come! | <say>Non venire!</say> |
| Don't go away! | <say>Non andare!</say> |
| Don't eat! | <say>Non mangiare!</say> |

> **Remember:** *non parla!* does NOT exist. Only *non parlare!*
> For all other persons (*Lei, noi, voi, Loro*) — just add *non* before the affirmative form.

## Part 5: Pronouns with the imperative — the main muscle work

This is the part where Italian gives you its brightest phrases. Direct, indirect, and reflexive pronouns **attach to the end** of the affirmative imperative for *tu, noi, voi*. With the *Lei* form — the opposite: they go **before** the verb.

### Attachment rule

| Form | Where pronouns go |
|------|-------------------|
| tu | **attach to the end**: <say>Dimmi!</say> "Tell me!" |
| Lei | **before the verb**: <say>Mi dica!</say> "Tell me!" |
| noi | **attach to the end**: <say>Diamogli!</say> "Let's give him!" |
| voi | **attach to the end**: <say>Ditegli!</say> "Tell him!" |
| Loro | **before the verb**: <say>Mi dicano!</say> (rare in everyday speech) |

### Consonant doubling — short *tu* forms

The seven short forms (Part 2), when a pronoun is attached, **double** the first consonant of the pronoun:

| Verb + pronoun | Result | Translation |
|----------------|--------|-------------|
| di' + mi | <say>**dimmi!**</say> | tell me! |
| da' + mi | <say>**dammi!**</say> | give me! |
| fa' + mi | <say>**fammi**</say> + vedere | show me |
| va' + ci | <say>**vacci!**</say> | go there! |
| sta' + ci | <say>**stacci!**</say> | stay there! |
| da' + lo | <say>**dallo!**</say> | give it! |
| fa' + lo | <say>**fallo!**</say> | do it! |

> Exception — the pronoun *gli* does **not** double: <say>digli!, dagli!</say>

### Double pronouns — combinations

| Verb + pronouns | Result | Translation |
|-----------------|--------|-------------|
| di' + me + lo | <say>**dimmelo!**</say> | tell me it! |
| da' + me + lo | <say>**dammelo!**</say> | give me it! |
| fa' + me + lo | <say>**fammelo**</say> + vedere | show me it |
| porta + me + lo | <say>**portamelo!**</say> | bring me it! |

> **Double-pronoun rule:** indirect + direct = *me lo, te lo, glielo, ce lo, ve lo, glielo* (with *gli/le → glie-*, as in L17). All written as one word with the verb.

### With the Lei form — the opposite, before the verb

| Italian | English |
|---------|---------|
| <say>Mi dica!</say> | Tell me! |
| <say>Me lo dica!</say> | Tell me it! |
| <say>Glielo dia!</say> | Give it to him! |
| <say>Mi scusi!</say> | Excuse me! |
| <say>Si accomodi!</say> | Come in! (lit. "make yourself comfortable") |

### Negative imperative with pronouns

In *tu* (the infinitive form), pronouns can go **either** before **or** after:

| English | Italian |
|---------|---------|
| Don't tell me! | <say>Non dirmi!</say> *(or:* <say>Non mi dire!</say>*)* |
| Don't do it! | <say>Non farlo!</say> *(or:* <say>Non lo fare!</say>*)* |
| Don't give it to him! | <say>Non darglielo!</say> *(or:* <say>Non glielo dare!</say>*)* |

With *Lei* — only before:

> <say>Non mi dica!</say> "Don't tell me!" — never *non dica mi*.

```

GENERAL TABLE:
  Person  | Where the form comes from
  --------|---------------------------
  tu      | special (for -ARE: -a, not -i!)
  Lei     | congiuntivo presente lui/lei
  noi     | same as present indicative noi
  voi     | same as present indicative voi
  Loro    | congiuntivo presente loro

PARLARE:  parla! / parli! / parliamo! / parlate! / parlino!
CREDERE:  credi! / creda! / crediamo! / credete! / credano!
DORMIRE:  dormi! / dorma! / dormiamo! / dormite! / dormano!
FINIRE:   finisci! / finisca! / finiamo! / finite! / finiscano!

THE SEVEN SHORT TU FORMS:
  va' (vai), da' (dai), fa' (fai), sta' (stai), di', sii, abbi

NEGATION:
  tu  → NO "non parla!". Right: NON + INFINITIVE
        non parlare!, non venire!, non fare!, non dire!
  all other persons: just non + the affirmative form
        non parli!, non parliamo!, non parlate!, non parlino!

LEI-FORM = CONGIUNTIVO:
  This is half of practical Italian.
  Mi dica! = "tell me" = cong. lui dica
  Mi scusi! = "excuse me" = cong. lui scusi
  Vada! Venga! Faccia! Si accomodi! Stia tranquillo!

PRONOUNS — WHERE TO STICK THEM:
  tu, noi, voi   →  ATTACH TO THE END
                    Dimmi! Diamogli! Ditemelo!
  Lei, Loro      →  BEFORE THE VERB
                    Mi dica! Glielo dia! Me lo dica!

SHORT FORM + PRONOUN = CONSONANT DOUBLING:
  di' + mi      = DIMMI!
  da' + lo      = DALLO!
  fa' + mi      = FAMMI vedere!
  va' + ci      = VACCI!
  sta' + ci     = STACCI!
  da' + me + lo = DAMMELO!
  di' + me + lo = DIMMELO!

  Exception: gli does NOT double.
    di' + gli     = DIGLI! (one g, no doubling)
    da' + gli + lo = DAGLIELO!

NEGATION + PRONOUN:
  Tu (infinitive form): pronoun can go either before or after.
    Non dirmi! / Non mi dire!
    Non farlo! / Non lo fare!
  Lei: only before.
    Non mi dica! Non lo faccia!

  L32–L34 → when to use congiuntivo in subordinate clauses
  L35 → when to use congiuntivo as an imperative

CLASSIC POLITE FORMULAS:
  Scusi! / Mi scusi!         — Excuse me! / I'm sorry!
  Senta!                     — Excuse me! (getting attention)
  Mi dica!                   — Yes? / Tell me!
  Prego, si accomodi.        — Please, come in.
  Mi faccia un caffè.        — Make me a coffee.
  Aspetti un momento.        — Wait a moment.
  Stia tranquillo.           — Don't worry.
  Mi creda!                  — Believe me!
```
