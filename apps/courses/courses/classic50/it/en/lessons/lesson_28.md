# Lesson 28: Compound future and compound conditional
**Vocabulary: missed chances, sequence, reproach (~28 items)**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — get two formulas and three uses (10 minutes).
2. **Pronounce** both paradigms out loud — *avere* and *essere* in the future plus participle, then in the conditional plus participle.
3. **Produce** — learn to *hear* the difference between "I'd go (but still might)" and "I would have gone (but didn't)".

> Lesson 21 gave you the past participle, L25 the future, L26 the simple conditional. Today — compound tenses that combine the future or the conditional **with** the participle. The logic is the same as in L27: an auxiliary in the right tense plus a participle.

---

## Part 1: Two compound tenses — *futuro composto* and *condizionale composto*

You already saw that *avere*/*essere* + participle gives compound past (L21–22), and *avere*/*essere* in the imperfect + participle gives *trapassato prossimo* (L27). Today — two more combinations:

| Tense | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Futuro composto** (compound future, *futuro anteriore*) | future of *avere/essere* + participle | <say>avrò mangiato, sarò andato</say> |
| **Condizionale composto** (compound conditional) | conditional of *avere/essere* + participle | <say>avrei mangiato, sarei andato</say> |

> **Rule:** **only the tense of the auxiliary changes.** Everything else — *avere*/*essere* choice, agreement with *essere* — is identical to L22 and L27.

> **English mirror:** futuro composto = "I **will have eaten**". Condizionale composto = "I **would have eaten**". Same "auxiliary + participle" shape, same job, two more pieces of furniture in the same room you already know.

---

## Part 2: Futuro composto — formula and use

### Formula

| Person | *mangiare* (with avere) | *andare* (with essere) |
|---|---|---|
| io | <say>avrò mangiato</say> | <say>sarò andato/a</say> |
| tu | <say>avrai mangiato</say> | <say>sarai andato/a</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>avrà mangiato</say> | <say>sarà andato/a</say> |
| noi | <say>avremo mangiato</say> | <say>saremo andati/e</say> |
| voi | <say>avrete mangiato</say> | <say>sarete andati/e</say> |
| loro | <say>avranno mangiato</say> | <say>saranno andati/e</say> |

### Use 1: "will have already finished by a moment in the future"

> <say>Quando arriverai, **avrò già preparato** la cena.</say> — When you arrive, I'll have already prepared dinner.
> <say>Tra un anno, **avrò finito** l'università.</say> — In a year, I'll have finished university.

This is the mirror of trapassato prossimo (L27), but pointed at the future. English does exactly the same with "will have".

### Use 2: a guess about the past

A very common spoken use — "they must have…":

> <say>**Sarà già arrivato** a casa.</say> — He must have already arrived home.
> <say>**Avranno mangiato** prima di venire.</say> — They probably ate before coming.
> <say>Dove sono i bambini? — **Saranno usciti** a giocare.</say> — Where are the kids? — They probably went out to play.

> **Compare with the simple future from L25:** *Saranno le otto* "It must be eight o'clock" (guess about the present). *Saranno usciti* "They must have gone out" (guess about the past).

## Part 3: Condizionale composto — formula

| Person | *mangiare* (with avere) | *andare* (with essere) |
|---|---|---|
| io | <say>avrei mangiato</say> | <say>sarei andato/a</say> |
| tu | <say>avresti mangiato</say> | <say>saresti andato/a</say> |
| lui/lei | <say>avrebbe mangiato</say> | <say>sarebbe andato/a</say> |
| noi | <say>avremmo mangiato</say> | <say>saremmo andati/e</say> |
| voi | <say>avreste mangiato</say> | <say>sareste andati/e</say> |
| loro | <say>avrebbero mangiato</say> | <say>sarebbero andati/e</say> |

> **Double "mm"** in the *noi* form is mandatory: *saremmo*, *avremmo*. Same trap as L26: *saremo* (future) vs. *saremmo* (conditional).

## Part 4: Condizionale composto — three central uses

### Use 1: missed opportunity ("would've done, but didn't")

This is the **main** use and one of the most important contrasts with the simple conditional.

> <say>**Sarei venuto**, ma non potevo.</say> — I would have come, but I couldn't. (didn't come)
> <say>**Avrei comprato** quel libro, ma costava troppo.</say> — I would have bought that book, but it cost too much. (didn't buy)
> <say>**Sarebbe stato** un bel viaggio.</say> — It would have been a nice trip. (but it didn't happen)

> **Contrast with L26:** *Andrei al mare* — "I'd go to the sea (now or later, if I could)" — action **still possible**. *Sarei andato al mare* — "I would have gone to the sea (back then, but didn't)" — missed.

> **English shortcut:** "would have V-en" → *condizionale composto*. Plain "would V" → *condizionale semplice*. Carry that over and you'll be right almost always.

### Use 2: reproach, regret ("you should have…")

With *dovere, potere, volere* in the compound conditional — a missed obligation.

> <say>**Avresti dovuto** chiamarmi.</say> — You should have called me. (but you didn't)
> <say>**Avrei potuto** aiutarti, ma non sapevo.</say> — I could have helped you, but I didn't know.
> <say>**Avrei voluto** vederlo.</say> — I would have liked to see him. (but didn't)

> **This is the most frequent case.** Simple conditional *dovresti* — "you should" (advice for the future). Compound *avresti dovuto* — "you should have" (reproach about the past). Italian ears split these instantly — and English speakers actually have the same distinction built in ("you should" vs "you should have").

### Use 3: future-in-the-past — the Italian peculiarity

This is the **most unusual** point for English speakers. In English, "future-in-the-past" is expressed with a simple "would". In **Italian** — with the **compound** conditional.

> English: *He said he **would come** tomorrow.*
> Italian: <say>Disse che **sarebbe venuto** il giorno dopo.</say>

When the main verb is in the past (*ha detto, disse, ha promesso, ha pensato*), and you're reporting something **future relative to that past moment** — you need **condizionale composto**.

| Direct speech | Reported (main verb in the past) |
|---|---|
| <say>«Verrò domani».</say> | <say>Ha detto che sarebbe venuto il giorno dopo.</say> |
| <say>«Comprerò una casa».</say> | <say>Mi ha promesso che avrebbe comprato una casa.</say> |
| <say>«Andrò a Roma».</say> | <say>Sapevo che sarebbe andata a Roma.</say> |
| <say>«Finiremo presto».</say> | <say>Pensavano che avrebbero finito presto.</say> |

> **Hammer rule:** if the main verb is in the past, and you're talking about an action that is **future from that past perspective**, in Italian it's **condizionale composto**, not simple conditional.

> **English mirror, with a twist:** in English you'd just say "would come" — simple. In Italian, that exact spot wants *sarebbe venuto* — compound. Same scenario, different machinery. Watch for the "he said he WOULD…" shape in your head and translate it to *sarebbe + participle*.

> **Why?** Simple conditional in modern Italian is occupied by "hypothesis" (L26). To free up the slot for "future-in-the-past", Italian grabbed a separate form — the compound conditional. Convenient for Italian, surprising for everyone else.

## Part 5: Comparing the two conditionals on one situation

> A mother asks her son: "Why didn't you go to the wedding?"

| Answer | Form | What the son means |
|---|---|---|
| <say>Sarei andato volentieri.</say> | condizionale composto | I would have gone (but didn't) |
| <say>Andrei volentieri, ma sono malato.</say> | condizionale semplice | I'd go (now, if I could) |

And:

> A father after an argument: "You should have spoken to me differently."

| Form | Translation |
|---|---|
| <say>Dovresti parlarmi diversamente.</say> | "You should speak to me differently." (advice) |
| <say>Avresti dovuto parlarmi diversamente.</say> | "You should have spoken to me differently." (reproach) |

---

## Part 6: All the compound tenses in one picture

You now have **five** compound tenses with *avere/essere* + participle in the system. They differ **only by the tense of the auxiliary**:

| Auxiliary tense | Tense | Example |
|---|---|---|
| present | passato prossimo (L21–22) | <say>ho mangiato, sono andato</say> |
| imperfect | trapassato prossimo (L27) | <say>avevo mangiato, ero andato</say> |
| future | futuro composto | <say>avrò mangiato, sarò andato</say> |
| conditional | condizionale composto | <say>avrei mangiato, sarei andato</say> |
| (subjunctive — L37) | | |

> **One template, five tenses.** This is the investment that pays Italian back enormously: the *avere/essere* choice and the participle form — learned once, working everywhere.

## Language scales

### Scale 1 — futuro composto with *avere*

```
Domani a quest'ora avrò finito.
Domani a quest'ora avrai finito.
Domani a quest'ora avrà finito.
Domani a quest'ora avremo finito.
Domani a quest'ora avrete finito.
Domani a quest'ora avranno finito.
```

```

Avrebbero dovuto studiare di più.
```

## Exercises

### Exercise 1. Put into the futuro composto

1. Quando arriverai, (io / finire) di lavorare.
2. Tra un'ora, (loro / partire) di sicuro.
3. (Lei / mangiare) prima di venire.
4. Entro lunedì, (noi / spedire) il pacco.
5. Sarà già le dieci, (loro / chiudere) il negozio.

1. *avrò finito*
2. *saranno partiti*

### Exercise 2. Put into the condizionale composto

2. (Tu / dovere) telefonarmi.
3. (Noi / partire) prima, ma non potevamo.
4. (Lei / piacere) quel libro.
5. (Loro / arrivare) in tempo, se non fosse stato per il traffico.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

5. *Sarebbero arrivati in tempo, se non fosse stato per il traffico.*

</details>

### Exercise 3. Direct speech → reported speech

Turn into reported speech. Main verb in the past, so "future-in-the-past" = *condizionale composto*.

1. <say>Marco ha detto: «Verrò domani».</say>
2. <say>Anna ha promesso: «Ti aiuterò».</say>
3. <say>I miei genitori dissero: «Compreremo una casa».</say>
4. <say>Il professore disse: «L'esame finirà alle dodici».</say>
5. <say>Mi ha assicurato: «Sarò puntuale».</say>

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Marco ha detto che sarebbe venuto il giorno dopo.*
2. *Anna ha promesso che mi avrebbe aiutato.*
3. *I miei genitori dissero che avrebbero comprato una casa.*
4. *Il professore disse che l'esame sarebbe finito alle dodici.*
5. *Mi ha assicurato che sarebbe stato puntuale.*

</details>

### Exercise 4. Simple or compound conditional?

1. I'd go to the beach if it were sunny (right now).
2. I would have gone to the beach yesterday, but it was raining.
3. You should rest more.
4. You should have rested more (yesterday).
5. We'd buy this house, but it's too expensive.
6. We would have bought that house, but it cost too much.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Andrei in spiaggia se ci fosse il sole.* — semplice, hypothesis about the present.
2. *Sarei andato in spiaggia ieri, ma pioveva.* — composto, missed opportunity.
3. *Dovresti riposarti di più.* — semplice, advice.
4. *Avresti dovuto riposarti di più.* — composto, reproach.
5. *Compreremmo questa casa, ma è troppo cara.* — semplice, hypothesis about the present.
6. *Avremmo comprato quella casa, ma costava troppo.* — composto, missed.

</details>

### Exercise 5. Translate into Italian

1. I would have come yesterday, but I couldn't.
2. You should have called me.
3. He said he would arrive at seven.
4. By Friday I'll have already finished the project.
5. Where's Anna? — She must have gone home.
6. I would have liked that film.
7. They promised they would help.
8. If only I had known!

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

</details>

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
TWO COMPOUND TENSES — FUTURO COMPOSTO AND CONDIZIONALE COMPOSTO
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FORMULA:
  futuro composto       = avere/essere in the FUTURE      + participle
  condizionale composto = avere/essere in the CONDITIONAL + participle

  (avere/essere choice and essere agreement — as in L22)

WITH AVERE — no agreement:
  avrò mangiato, avrei mangiato

WITH ESSERE — with agreement:
  sarò andato/a, sarei andato/a
  saremo andati/e, saremmo andati/e   (NB: double mm!)

FUTURO COMPOSTO — TWO USES:
  1) "will have already finished by some future moment"
     Quando arriverai, avrò già preparato la cena.
     Tra un anno avrò finito l'università.
     = English "will have V-en"

  2) Guess about the PAST ("they must have…")
     Sarà già arrivato.
     Avranno mangiato fuori.

CONDIZIONALE COMPOSTO — THREE USES:
  1) Missed opportunity ("would have done, but didn't")
     Sarei venuto, ma non potevo.
     Avrei comprato quel libro, ma costava troppo.
     = English "would have V-en"

  2) Reproach / regret ("should have, back then")
     Avresti dovuto chiamarmi.
     Avrei potuto aiutarti, ma non sapevo.
     = English "should have", "could have"

  3) FUTURE IN THE PAST — the Italian peculiarity!
     In English — just "would" (simple).
     In Italian — COMPOUND conditional.
     Disse che sarebbe venuto.   = "He said he would come"
     Promise che mi avrebbe aiutato.

SIMPLE vs. COMPOUND CONDITIONAL:
  semplice  — hypothesis about present/future
              Vorrei un caffè. Andrei al mare.
              Dovresti riposarti. (advice)
  composto  — missed past
              Sarei andato. Avrei dovuto.
              Avresti dovuto chiamarmi. (reproach)

FIVE COMPOUND TENSES — ONE TEMPLATE:
  present   → ho mangiato       (passato prossimo, L21)
  imperfect → avevo mangiato    (trapassato, L27)
  future    → avrò mangiato     (futuro composto, L28)
  cond.     → avrei mangiato    (cond. composto, L28)
  + subjunctive (L37)

  Only the tense of the auxiliary changes.

SPELLING TRAP:
  saremo  (future, one m)
  saremmo (conditional, TWO m's)

ENGLISH SHORTCUT:
  "will have V-en"   → futuro composto
  "would V"          → condizionale semplice
  "would have V-en"  → condizionale composto
  "He said he WOULD" → condizionale composto (sarebbe venuto)
```
