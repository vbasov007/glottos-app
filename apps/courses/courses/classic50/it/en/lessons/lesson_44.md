# Lesson 44: Idioms, set phrases, figurative language
**Vocabulary: idioms, figurative extensions of workhorse verbs, proverbs (~42 items)**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand how an idiom works and why you can't "assemble" it from its words (10 minutes).
2. **Recognize ten times more than you produce** — the cardinal rule. C1 doesn't mean "speak in idioms"; C1 means **understand them** in speech and text.
3. **Learn in context** — phrase + the situation in which to say it.
4. **Don't overload your text with idioms** — 1–2 per paragraph maximum. More than that and you sound like a parody.

> **The cardinal property of an idiom:** the meaning of the **whole** is not derived from the meanings of the **parts**. *«In bocca al lupo»* — no wolves. *«Tirare il pacco»* — no parcels. These are **fixed, frozen** expressions. You learn them as a **single word**.

> **C1 warning:** foreigners who've collected a stash of idioms often **overuse** them — drop them in the wrong place, choose ones too colloquial for a business context, or mangle them slightly. A native spots it instantly. Better to **recognize** idioms freely and **use** them sparingly and accurately.

> **False-friend warning for English speakers:** Italian idioms with body parts and food are culturally distinct from English ones. *«In bocca al lupo!»* means "good luck!" — NOT "in the mouth of the wolf" (which is what it says word-for-word). *«Fare la scarpetta»* means "wipe up the sauce with bread" — NOT anything about shoes. Treat each idiom as a black-box word, just like *table* or *house*.

## Part 1: What an idiom is

> **Idiom** (espressione idiomatica) — a fixed expression whose meaning **doesn't add up** from the meanings of its component words.

| Idiom | Word-for-word | What it actually means |
|-------|---------------|------------------------|
| <say>in bocca al lupo</say> | in the mouth of the wolf | good luck (good wishes) |
| <say>essere al verde</say> | to be in the green | to be broke, out of money |
| <say>avere le mani in pasta</say> | to have one's hands in the dough | to be in the know, mixed up in something |
| <say>tirare il pacco</say> | to pull the parcel | to stand someone up, no-show |
| <say>tagliare la corda</say> | to cut the rope | to bolt, sneak off |
| <say>fare il finto tonto</say> | to play the fake idiot | to play dumb |
| <say>non avere peli sulla lingua</say> | to have no hairs on one's tongue | to speak one's mind, pull no punches |

If you know only the parts, you'll **never** get to the meaning. And vice-versa — even a native doesn't read the parts literally. *«In bocca al lupo»* — there's no mouth, no wolf. There's **one chunk of text** meaning "good luck!"

### Idiom vs collocation

| Collocation | Idiom |
|-------------|-------|
| Words keep their meanings; they just "go together" | Meaning of the whole is **decoupled** from the parts |
| *fare una domanda* — "do" + "question" = "ask a question" (makes sense) | *fare il finto tonto* — "do" + "fake idiot" (makes no literal sense) |
| Slight variation OK (*una bella domanda*) | No variation possible (you can't say *«fare il finto stupido»*) |

> Compare English: "take a shower" (collocation — *take* keeps its idea of "obtain/perform") vs. "kick the bucket" (idiom — nothing to do with kicking or buckets). Same split.

## Part 2: The "do" family — figurative *fare*

One of the most productive idiom factories in Italian. *Fare* in dozens of expressions doesn't mean "do" but something figurative.

| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| <say>fare il pieno</say> | fill up the tank |
| <say>fare colazione / pranzo / cena</say> | have breakfast / lunch / dinner |
| <say>fare la spesa</say> | do the grocery shopping |
| <say>fare un giro</say> | go for a walk/drive |
| <say>fare le ore piccole</say> | stay up till the small hours |
| <say>fare lo gnorri</say> | play dumb (rare/regional) |
| <say>fare orecchie da mercante</say> | turn a deaf ear |
| <say>fare il finto tonto</say> | play dumb |
| <say>fare due passi</say> | go for a quick stroll |
| <say>fare in tempo</say> | be in time, make it |
| <say>fare di tutto</say> | do everything possible |
| <say>fare il bagno</say> | take a bath / go for a swim |

> *Fare* is **obligatory** in these idioms. Swap it for another verb and the expression breaks.

## Part 3: The "put" family — figurative *mettere*

| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| <say>mettere in dubbio</say> | call into question |
| <say>mettere in chiaro</say> | make clear, set straight |
| <say>mettere alle strette</say> | back into a corner |
| <say>mettere su famiglia</say> | start a family |
| <say>mettere radici</say> | put down roots, settle |
| <say>mettersi nei panni di</say> | put oneself in someone's shoes |
| <say>mettere i puntini sulle i</say> | dot the i's, set things straight |
| <say>mettere il dito nella piaga</say> | rub salt in the wound |
| <say>mettersi le mani nei capelli</say> | tear one's hair out (in dismay) |

Notice that many of these are reflexive (*mettersi*), because the speaker is **doing something to themselves** or to their own life.

> **English parallels:** "put down roots", "put oneself in someone's shoes", "dot the i's", "back into a corner" — almost word-for-word identical. Italian and English share a lot of European-Latin idiomatic substrate here.

## Part 4: The "take" family — figurative *prendere*

| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| <say>prendere in giro</say> | tease, pull someone's leg |
| <say>prendere il sole</say> | sunbathe |
| <say>prendere fiato</say> | catch one's breath |
| <say>prendere posizione</say> | take a position (opinion) |

## Part 6: Other idiom categories

### A. With body parts

| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| <say>avere la testa fra le nuvole</say> | have one's head in the clouds |
| <say>essere giù di morale</say> | be down in the dumps |
| <say>avere le mani bucate</say> | be a spender (lit. "have holes in one's hands") |
| <say>essere in gamba</say> | be sharp, on the ball |
| <say>tirare le cuoia</say> | kick the bucket (vulgar) |
| <say>non avere peli sulla lingua</say> | pull no punches |
| <say>parlare a vanvera</say> | talk nonsense, ramble |
| <say>avere il pollice verde</say> | have a green thumb |

### B. With food and the kitchen — an Italian specialty!

| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| <say>essere come il cacio sui maccheroni</say> | be just what was needed (lit. "cheese on macaroni") |
| <say>non è tutto rose e fiori</say> | it's not all roses (= sunshine and roses) |
| <say>rendere pan per focaccia</say> | give tit for tat (lit. "bread for focaccia") |
| <say>finire a tarallucci e vino</say> | end amicably, no hard feelings |
| <say>fare polpette di qualcuno</say> | make mincemeat of someone |
| <say>essere una pizza</say> | be a bore (of a film, a person) |
| <say>fare la scarpetta</say> | wipe up the sauce with bread |

> **There are an unusual number of food idioms in Italian** — Italians genuinely think through food. This is a cultural layer foreigners need to acquire deliberately. English doesn't have nearly as many food-based idioms (you have "piece of cake", "spill the beans", "in a pickle" — but not the dense culinary metaphor system Italian has).

### C. Money, luck, misfortune

| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| <say>essere al verde</say> | be broke |
| <say>costare un occhio della testa</say> | cost an arm and a leg |
| <say>buttare i soldi dalla finestra</say> | throw money out the window |
| <say>fare i conti senza l'oste</say> | count without the host (= count chickens before they hatch) |
| <say>essere fortunato come un cane in chiesa</say> | be very unlucky (lit. "lucky as a dog in church") |
| <say>tirare la cinghia</say> | tighten one's belt |

### D. Luck and exclamations

| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| <say>in bocca al lupo!</say> | good luck! (reply: *crepi!*) |
| <say>che pizza!</say> | what a bore! |
| <say>magari!</say> | if only! / maybe! |
| <say>boh!</say> | dunno, beats me |
| <say>peccato!</say> | what a shame! |
| <say>che casino!</say> | what a mess! |

> **Big false-friend warning:** *«In bocca al lupo»* literally is "in the mouth of the wolf", but it means "good luck!" — used before exams, performances, big events. The reply is *«Crepi!»* (lit. "may it die!"). Never translate it literally; never reply *«Grazie»* (that's bad luck). Just learn the formula whole.

## Part 7: Proverbs — "frozen wisdom"

> **Proverb** (proverbio) — an idiom expressing a **generalized truth**. Recognize them all, use them with great caution.

| Proverb | Literal | English meaning |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| <say>Chi dorme non piglia pesci.</say> | He who sleeps catches no fish | The early bird catches the worm. |
| <say>Meglio tardi che mai.</say> | Better late than never | (same in English) |
| <say>Tutte le strade portano a Roma.</say> | All roads lead to Rome | (same in English) |
| <say>L'abito non fa il monaco.</say> | The habit doesn't make the monk | Don't judge a book by its cover. |
| <say>Chi va piano va sano e va lontano.</say> | He who goes slowly goes safely and goes far | Slow and steady wins the race. |
| <say>Il lupo perde il pelo ma non il vizio.</say> | The wolf loses its fur but not its vice | A leopard can't change its spots. |
| <say>Paese che vai, usanza che trovi.</say> | The country you go to, the custom you find | When in Rome, do as the Romans. |
| <say>A caval donato non si guarda in bocca.</say> | One doesn't look a gift horse in the mouth | Don't look a gift horse in the mouth. |

> Notice: many Italian proverbs **map directly** onto English proverbs, because they share a common European (often Latin) source. This is a huge advantage for English speakers — you recognize the meaning instantly.

## Part 8: The "recognize ≫ produce" rule

> **C1 goal for idioms:** **recognize** every idiom in this lesson (and hundreds more) in speech and text. **Produce** maybe a dozen of the most neutral and safe ones.

Why the asymmetry?

- **Idioms are culturally loaded.** *«Fare la scarpetta»* is opaque to someone who didn't grow up at an Italian table.
- **The register of an idiom is often colloquial.** *«Tirare il pacco»* in a client email is wrong.
- **A slightly mangled idiom sounds awful.** *«In bocca al lupo!»* — yes; *«In bocca al cane!»* — the native winces.
- **Too many idioms in a row = caricature.** The text becomes a parody.

### Safe to use actively

| Idiom | When appropriate |
|-------|------------------|
| <say>in bocca al lupo!</say> | wishing luck before an exam, event |
| <say>magari!</say> | "if only!" — response to something desired |
| <say>boh!</say> | in informal speech, "dunno" |
| <say>che peccato!</say> | "what a pity!" — sympathy response |
| <say>dare un'occhiata</say> | "have a look" — neutral, universal |
| <say>dare una mano</say> | "give a hand" — universal |
| <say>prendere una decisione</say> | "make a decision" — universal |
| <say>fare due passi</say> | "go for a stroll" — universal |

### Use with caution

- Regional idioms (Southern ≠ Northern)
- Idioms with a vulgar edge (*tirare le cuoia*, *fare polpette di qualcuno*)
- Idioms with emotional colouring you might not dose right

## Part 9: Language matrix — recognizing idioms in dialogue

| Context | Phrase | Meaning |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Before an exam | <say>In bocca al lupo!</say> | Good luck! |
| Reply to "good luck" | <say>Crepi (il lupo)!</say> | Thanks! |
| Out of money | <say>Sono al verde.</say> | I'm broke. |
| Offering help | <say>Ti do una mano?</say> | Can I give you a hand? |
| Missed a meeting | <say>Mi ha tirato il pacco.</say> | He stood me up. |
| Pretending not to get it | <say>Fai il finto tonto?</say> | Are you playing dumb? |
| Wanting a look | <say>Dammi un'occhiata.</say> | Let me have a look. |
| Out of focus | <say>Ho la testa fra le nuvole.</say> | My head's in the clouds. |
| Very expensive | <say>Costa un occhio della testa!</say> | It costs an arm and a leg! |
| Decided | <say>Ho preso una decisione.</say> | I've made a decision. |

Run aloud three times. Notice the expressions built on **basic verbs** *fare, mettere, prendere, dare* — these are the **most productive class** of Italian idioms.
