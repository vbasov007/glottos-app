# Lesson 22: Passato prossimo with essere. Participle agreement
**Vocabulary: verbs of motion and change of state, reflexives in the past**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read Part 1** — there are **two** auxiliary verbs, and the choice isn't intuitive. L21 showed "avere by default"; this lesson is the **exceptions**.
2. **Memorize the family of essere verbs** — about 20 of them. Seven motion pairs (andare/venire, entrare/uscire, salire/scendere, arrivare/partire) form the skeleton.
3. **Lock in the essere agreement rule** — the participle agrees **with the subject** in gender and number: *lei è andata*, *noi siamo arrivati*, *le ragazze sono partite*.
4. **Separately, learn the second agreement** — with the **direct-object pronoun** under avere: *l'ho vista*, *le ho viste*, *li ho visti*. This is a callback to L13.

> This is the most load-bearing lesson in Block 3. Lock in which verbs love essere, plus both agreement rules, and passato prossimo is yours forever.

---

## Part 1: Two auxiliaries — why

Italian, like French, has **two verbs** that mean "to have done": **avere** (as in L21) and **essere** (to be). The choice isn't up to you — each verb has its **own** auxiliary, fixed by the dictionary.

> **avere** — most verbs (transitive, plus many intransitive ones).
> **essere** — a short list: verbs of motion, change of state, and all reflexives.

Why? Historically (from Latin), **essere** went with verbs whose subject **changes themselves** — arrives, leaves, is born, dies, becomes. *Avere* went with action directed outward at something ("I ate the pizza"). The logic isn't strict today, but it's a useful mnemonic.

The key practical rule:

> If a verb takes **essere**, the participle **agrees with the subject** in gender and number (just like an adjective: -o / -a / -i / -e).

Compare:

| with avere (participle never changes) | with essere (participle changes) |
|----------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| <say>Marco ha mangiato.</say> | <say>Marco è andato.</say> |
| <say>Maria ha mangiato.</say> | <say>Maria è andat**a**.</say> |
| <say>I ragazzi hanno mangiato.</say> | <say>I ragazzi sono andat**i**.</say> |
| <say>Le ragazze hanno mangiato.</say> | <say>Le ragazze sono andat**e**.</say> |

> **English-speaker reality check:** English uses ONE auxiliary, "have", for absolutely everything: *I have gone, she has gone, they have gone* — the participle never changes. Italian asks you for two new moves at once: (1) pick the right auxiliary, (2) inflect the participle for gender/number. **This is the single biggest "Italian feels foreign" moment in Block 3.** Don't fight it — just memorize the essere list and watch the endings.

## Part 2: The form — essere + past participle

| Person | essere | + participle (with agreement!) |
|--------|--------|--------------------------------|
| io (m.) | **sono** | andat**o** |
| io (f.) | **sono** | andat**a** |
| tu (m.) | **sei** | andat**o** |
| tu (f.) | **sei** | andat**a** |
| lui | **è** | andat**o** |
| lei | **è** | andat**a** |
| noi (all m. / mixed) | **siamo** | andat**i** |
| noi (all f.) | **siamo** | andat**e** |
| voi (m. / mixed) | **siete** | andat**i** |
| voi (all f.) | **siete** | andat**e** |
| loro (m. / mixed) | **sono** | andat**i** |
| loro (all f.) | **sono** | andat**e** |

> **Mixed-group rule:** at least one male in the group → participle ends in **-i** (masculine wins, the Romance-language norm). All-female group → **-e**.

Negation: **non** before essere.
<say>Non sono andato a scuola.</say> — I didn't go to school.
<say>Non siamo arrivati in tempo.</say> — We didn't arrive on time.

Questions: just intonation.
<say>Sei andata al cinema?</say> — Did you (f.) go to the cinema?

## Part 3: Which verbs take essere

Memorize them in **families**. Seven motion pairs form the backbone.

### Motion pairs

| Outward / one direction | Back / the other direction |
|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| <say>andare</say> — to go (away) → **andato** | <say>venire</say> — to come → **venuto** |
| <say>entrare</say> — to go in → **entrato** | <say>uscire</say> — to go out → **uscito** |
| <say>salire</say> — to go up → **salito** | <say>scendere</say> — to go down → **sceso** |
| <say>arrivare</say> — to arrive → **arrivato** | <say>partire</say> — to leave → **partito** |
| <say>tornare</say> — to return → **tornato** | (paired with partire by sense) |

### Change of state (including birth/death)

| Verb | Participle | English |
|------|------------|---------|
| <say>nascere</say> | **nato** | to be born |
| <say>morire</say> | **morto** | to die |
| <say>crescere</say> | **cresciuto** | to grow, grow up |
| <say>diventare</say> | **diventato** | to become |
| <say>cambiare</say>* | **cambiato** | to change |

> *<say>cambiare</say> is two-faced: "to change something" (avere) vs "to change/be changed" (essere). *Ho cambiato lavoro* (I changed jobs — avere), *Sono cambiato* (I've changed — essere).

### Verbs of staying / remaining

| Verb | Participle | English |
|------|------------|---------|
| <say>essere</say> | **stato** | to be |
| <say>stare</say> | **stato** | to be, to stay, to feel |
| <say>restare</say> | **restato** | to stay, to remain |
| <say>rimanere</say> | **rimasto** | to remain |

> **Fun fact:** *essere* and *stare* share one participle — **stato**. <say>Sono stato a Roma.</say> — I have been to Rome (passato prossimo of essere). <say>Sono stato bene.</say> — I felt good (passato prossimo of stare).

### Other high-frequency essere verbs

| Verb | Participle | English |
|------|------------|---------|
| <say>piacere</say> | **piaciuto** | to be pleasing (= "like") |
| <say>succedere</say> | **successo** | to happen |
| <say>bastare</say> | **bastato** | to be enough |
| <say>sembrare</say> | **sembrato** | to seem |
| <say>costare</say> | **costato** | to cost |

> **"Mi è piaciuto / Mi sono piaciuti":** *piacere* is interesting — agreement runs with **what is liked**, not with you. <say>Mi è piaciuto il film.</say> (film — m. sg.) <say>Mi sono piaciuti i film.</say> (films — m. pl.) <say>Mi è piaciuta la pizza.</say> (pizza — f. sg.)

### All reflexives

**All** reflexive verbs (with *si* — see L16) take essere in passato prossimo, and the participle agrees with the subject.

| Verb | Example with agreement |
|------|------------------------|
| <say>alzarsi</say> | <say>Maria si è alzat**a** alle sette.</say> |
| <say>lavarsi</say> | <say>I bambini si sono lavat**i** le mani.</say> |
| <say>vestirsi</say> | <say>Le donne si sono vestit**e** bene.</say> |
| <say>svegliarsi</say> | <say>Mi sono svegliat**o** tardi.</say> (m.) / <say>svegliat**a**</say> (f.) |
| <say>divertirsi</say> | <say>Ci siamo divertit**i** alla festa.</say> |
| <say>annoiarsi</say> | <say>Mi sono annoiat**o** al concerto.</say> |

> **Lock in:** even if the non-reflexive form of the verb takes avere (*lavare* "to wash" — *Ho lavato la macchina* — avere), the **reflexive version** (*lavarsi* "to wash oneself" — *Mi sono lavato* — **essere**).

## Part 4: The second agreement rule — with a direct-object pronoun (under avere)

This is a callback to L13 (direct-object pronouns lo/la/li/le) and one of Italian's main "gotchas".

> **Rule:** when a direct-object pronoun **lo / la / li / le** sits in front of **avere**, the participle **agrees with that pronoun** in gender and number.

Compare:

| Without the pronoun (no agreement) | With the pronoun (agreement) |
|------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| <say>Ho visto Marco.</say> | <say>L'ho vist**o**.</say> (lo + visto) |
| <say>Ho visto Maria.</say> | <say>L'ho vist**a**.</say> (la + vista) |
| <say>Ho visto i ragazzi.</say> | <say>L**i** ho vist**i**.</say> |
| <say>Ho visto le ragazze.</say> | <say>L**e** ho vist**e**.</say> |

> **Note:** *lo* and *la* contract to **l'** before *ho/hai/ha/avete/hanno* (apostrophe). *Li* and *le* do **not** contract (different consonants, not the vowel *o/a*).

### Full paradigm with lo/la/li/le

| Pronoun | Example | Participle |
|---------|---------|------------|
| lo (it, m. sg.) | <say>Il libro? L'ho letto.</say> | letto |
| la (it, f. sg.) | <say>La lettera? L'ho letta.</say> | letta |
| li (them, m. pl.) | <say>I libri? Li ho letti.</say> | letti |
| le (them, f. pl.) | <say>Le lettere? Le ho lette.</say> | lette |

### With mi, ti, ci, vi — agreement is optional

With *mi, ti, ci, vi* (1st, 2nd person) agreement is **allowed but not required**.

<say>Ci ha visto.</say> or <say>Ci ha visti.</say> — both correct.

But with *lo/la/li/le* — **mandatory**.

## Part 5: Contested / double-faced cases

### One verb — two auxiliaries, two meanings

A handful of verbs run with both *avere* and *essere* — with different meanings.

| Avere (transitive) | Essere (intransitive) |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| <say>Ho cambiato lavoro.</say> — I changed jobs. | <say>Sono cambiato.</say> — I've changed. |
| <say>Ho cominciato il libro.</say> — I started the book. | <say>Il film è cominciato.</say> — The film started. |
| <say>Ho finito i compiti.</say> — I finished the homework. | <say>Il film è finito.</say> — The film is over. |
| <say>Ho passato un'ora qui.</say> — I spent an hour here. | <say>Un'ora è passata.</say> — An hour has passed. |
| <say>Ho salito le scale.</say> — I climbed the stairs (there's a "what" — the stairs). | <say>Sono salito al primo piano.</say> — I went up to the first floor (no "what"). |

> **Test question:** is there a "what? / whom?" (direct object)? **Yes → avere**. **No → essere** (for those verbs that can ever take essere).

### Modal verbs (potere, dovere, volere) — follow the main verb

Modals take the auxiliary **of the verb they govern**.

<say>Ho voluto mangiare.</say> — I wanted to eat. (*mangiare* with avere → *voluto* with avere)
<say>Sono dovuto andare.</say> — I had to go. (*andare* with essere → *dovuto* with essere)
<say>Ha potuto leggere.</say> — She managed to read. (*leggere* with avere → *potuto* with avere)
<say>È potuta venire.</say> — She managed to come. (*venire* with essere → *potuta* with essere)

> In informal speech many Italians flatten this and use avere everywhere — but the "correct" norm is to follow the main verb.

## Language scales

### Scale 1 — andare with agreement

> <say>Io sono andato al cinema.</say> (m.) / <say>Io sono andata al cinema.</say> (f.)
> <say>Tu sei andato al cinema.</say> / <say>Tu sei andata al cinema.</say>
> <say>Lui è andato al cinema.</say>
> <say>Lei è andata al cinema.</say>

> <say>Mi sono alzato presto.</say> (m.) / <say>alzata</say> (f.)
> <say>Ti sei alzato presto.</say> / <say>alzata</say>

> <say>Vi siete alzati presto.</say> / <say>alzate</say>
> <say>Si sono alzati presto.</say> / <say>alzate</say>

### Scale 3 — agreement with the direct-object pronoun

> <say>Il libro? L'ho letto.</say>
> <say>La lettera? L'ho letta.</say>
> <say>I libri? Li ho letti.</say>
> <say>Le lettere? Le ho lette.</say>

  Ho visto Maria.        → L'ho vista.       (la → -a)
  Ho visto i ragazzi.    → Li ho visti.      (li → -i)
  Ho visto le ragazze.   → Le ho viste.      (le → -e)

  With mi/ti/ci/vi the agreement is optional.
  With lo/la/li/le it's MANDATORY.

TWO-FACED VERBS (avere vs essere):
  Ho cominciato il libro.    (avere — there's a "what")
  Il film è cominciato.      (essere — no "what")
  Ho cambiato lavoro.        (avere)
  Sono cambiato.             (essere)

TEST QUESTION:
  Motion / change of state?   → essere
  Reflexive (with si)?        → essere
  Piacere, succedere, restare? → essere
  Otherwise                   → avere

KEY FOR ENGLISH SPEAKERS:
  English has ONE auxiliary ("have") and NO participle agreement.
  Italian asks for TWO new moves:
    1. pick the right auxiliary (avere vs essere)
    2. inflect the participle (-o/-a/-i/-e) with the subject (essere)
       or with the direct-object pronoun lo/la/li/le (avere)
  This is the most "un-English" feature of Block 3 — drill it.
