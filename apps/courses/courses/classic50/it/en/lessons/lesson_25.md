# Lesson 25: Futuro semplice. Future and conjecture
**Vocabulary: future markers, plans, predictions, probability**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read Part 1** — the Italian future is built on the same skeleton as Spanish (and yet the endings are simpler). The big news: where English says "I will do" or "I'm going to do", Italians often use the **plain present**.
2. **Memorize the regular endings** — they're the same for all three conjugations: *-ò, -ai, -à, -emo, -ete, -anno*.
3. **Learn the 9 irregular stems** — sarò, avrò, farò, andrò, verrò, potrò, dovrò, saprò, vorrò. These **stems come back** in the conditional (L26), so they're load-bearing.
4. **Pay special attention to conjecture:** *Saranno le otto* — "It must be 8". This is a **uniquely Italian use** of the future that English doesn't share.

> This is the **last** lesson in Block 3. After it, all the basic tenses of Italian are in your hands: present, two pasts and a future. From Lesson 26 onwards, the system branches into conditional and subjunctive.

## Part 1: What futuro semplice is

**Futuro semplice** — simple future. It's used for:

1. **Predictions** — what will happen.
2. **Distant plans** — next year, in ten years.
3. **Promises and decisions** — "I'll do it".
4. **Conjecture / probability in the present** — "it must be such-and-such".

> **Big warning up front:** for **near-term plans** and **confident intentions**, Italians more often use the **plain present** or *stare per + infinitive*:
> <say>Domani vado al cinema.</say> — Tomorrow I'm going to the cinema (planned, confident).
> <say>Sto per uscire.</say> — I'm about to head out (right now).
> Using *futuro semplice* in these contexts sounds **more formal, more distant, or less certain**.

Compare:

| Plain present (near, confident) | Futuro semplice (distant / predicted / promised) |
|---------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| <say>Domani lavoro a Milano.</say> | <say>L'anno prossimo lavorerò a Milano.</say> |
| <say>Stasera mangio a casa.</say> | <say>Domani penso a quello che mangerò.</say> |
| <say>Vengo subito.</say> | <say>Un giorno verrò a trovarti.</say> |

> **English-speaker note:** in English you'd often translate both columns with "will" or "going to". Italian distinguishes: confident-and-soon goes in the present; predicted, promised, distant, or hypothetical goes in futuro. Don't reach for futuro every time you hear English "will" — let the present do the job for tomorrow.

## Part 2: The form — regular endings

Unlike the other tenses, the future has **the same endings for all three conjugations** (with one small twist for -are).

### -are and -ere conjugations — endings identical (important!)

Verbs in **-are** change **a → e** in the stem in the future: *parlare → parler-* (just like -ere verbs). Then everyone shares one set of endings.

| Person | Ending |
|--------|--------|
| io | **-ò** |
| tu | **-ai** |
| lui / lei | **-à** |
| noi | **-emo** |
| voi | **-ete** |
| loro | **-anno** |

| parlare (-are) | credere (-ere) | dormire (-ire) |
|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| parler**ò** | creder**ò** | dormir**ò** |
| parler**ai** | creder**ai** | dormir**ai** |
| parler**à** | creder**à** | dormir**à** |
| parler**emo** | creder**emo** | dormir**emo** |
| parler**ete** | creder**ete** | dormir**ete** |
| parler**anno** | creder**anno** | dormir**anno** |

> **Key mnemonic:** the future endings — **"ò, ai, à, emo, ete, anno"** — are basically the forms of **avere** ("ho, hai, ha, abbiamo, avete, hanno") with the leading **h** chopped off! That's no accident: futuro evolved historically from "infinitive + avere".

### Accents

> All io and lui/lei forms carry a **written accent**: **parler**ò**, parler**à**. Don't drop it — it distinguishes *parlerà* (he/she will speak) from *parlera* (a non-form).

### Spelling tweaks

- **-care, -gare** → add an **h** to keep the hard sound: cercare → **cercherò**, pagare → **pagherò**.
- **-ciare, -giare** → drop the **i** before e: cominciare → **comincerò**, mangiare → **mangerò**.

## Part 3: Nine irregular stems — load-bearing

These stems come back **letter-for-letter** in the conditional (L26). Memorize them now — you'll save two lessons of pain.

| Infinitive | Future stem | io form | English |
|------------|-------------|---------|---------|
| <say>essere</say> | sar- | **sarò** | I will be |
| <say>avere</say> | avr- | **avrò** | I will have |
| <say>fare</say> | far- | **farò** | I will do |
| <say>andare</say> | andr- | **andrò** | I will go |
| <say>venire</say> | verr- | **verrò** | I will come |
| <say>potere</say> | potr- | **potrò** | I will be able |
| <say>dovere</say> | dovr- | **dovrò** | I will have to |
| <say>sapere</say> | sapr- | **saprò** | I will know |
| <say>volere</say> | vorr- | **vorrò** | I will want |

### Full paradigms — most frequent

#### essere → sarò

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| io | **sarò** |
| tu | **sarai** |
| lui / lei | **sarà** |
| noi | **saremo** |
| voi | **sarete** |
| loro | **saranno** |

#### avere → avrò

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| io | **avrò** |
| tu | **avrai** |
| lui / lei | **avrà** |
| noi | **avremo** |
| voi | **avrete** |
| loro | **avranno** |

#### andare → andrò

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| io | **andrò** |
| tu | **andrai** |
| lui / lei | **andrà** |
| noi | **andremo** |
| voi | **andrete** |
| loro | **andranno** |

> **Memory hack:** essere and avere are the most frequent — drill them solid. The other seven show the pattern **"drop the vowel from the infinitive"**: andare → andr-, dovere → dovr-, potere → potr-, sapere → sapr-, avere → avr-. *Venire* and *volere* are special — **double r** (verr-, vorr-).

### A few more irregulars (secondary)

| Infinitive | Stem | English |
|------------|------|---------|
| <say>vedere</say> | vedr- | to see |
| <say>rimanere</say> | rimarr- | to stay |
| <say>tenere</say> | terr- | to hold |
| <say>vivere</say> | vivr- | to live |
| <say>bere</say> | berr- | to drink |

## Part 4: Uses

### Use 1: Prediction

<say>Domani **pioverà**.</say> — Tomorrow it'll rain.
<say>Nei prossimi anni il clima **cambierà**.</say> — In the coming years the climate will change.
<say>L'anno prossimo i prezzi **aumenteranno**.</say> — Next year prices will go up.

### Use 2: Distant plans

<say>Tra dieci anni **vivrò** in Italia.</say> — In ten years I'll be living in Italy.
<say>Quando avrò trent'anni, **comprerò** una casa.</say> — When I'm 30, I'll buy a house.
<say>Dopo la laurea **cercherò** lavoro all'estero.</say> — After graduation I'll look for a job abroad.

### Use 3: Promise and decision

<say>**Ti chiamerò** stasera.</say> — I'll call you tonight.
<say>**Non lo dirò** a nessuno.</say> — I won't tell anyone.
<say>**Ti aiuterò**, promesso.</say> — I'll help you, I promise.

### Use 4: Conjecture / probability in the present

This is a **uniquely Italian** use that English doesn't share directly. When an Italian uses the future tense **about right now** — they're expressing **a guess**.

<say>**Saranno** le otto.</say> — It must be eight o'clock.
<say>Maria non risponde. **Sarà** occupata.</say> — Maria isn't answering. She must be busy.
<say>Quanti anni avrà? **Avrà** quarant'anni.</say> — How old is she? She must be about 40.
<say>Marco non c'è. **Sarà** ancora a casa.</say> — Marco's not here. He must still be at home.

> **English-speaker mapping:** *Sarà…* / *Saranno…* = English "It must be" / "probably" / "I bet". If you could say "must be" or "probably" in English, the Italian almost always uses futuro.
>
> Notice the framing flip: English uses the **modal "must"** ("it must be 8") to express inference. Italian uses **the future tense** ("Saranno le otto" — literally "they will be eight"). Same idea, completely different grammatical machinery. Don't translate word-for-word; learn the Italian habit.

### Use 5: Concessive "maybe yes, but…"

<say>**Sarà** ricco, ma non è felice.</say> — He may be rich, but he isn't happy.
<say>**Avrà** ragione, ma non lo ascolto.</say> — He may be right, but I'm not listening to him.

> A literary use, but it shows up in spoken Italian too.

### Use 6: After se / quando / appena in future contexts

When the main clause is in futuro, the subordinate clause with *quando / appena* is **also** in futuro (this is different from English and Spanish).

<say>**Quando arriverò** a Roma, **ti chiamerò**.</say> — When I arrive in Rome, I'll call you.
<say>**Se piove** domani, **non usciremo**.</say> — If it rains tomorrow, we won't go out. (with *se* — usually present)
<say>**Se pioverà** sabato, andremo al cinema.</say> — If it rains on Saturday, we'll go to the cinema. (futuro is acceptable too)
<say>**Appena vedrò** Marco, **gli dirò** tutto.</say> — As soon as I see Marco, I'll tell him everything.

> **English compare:** English forbids "will" in the if/when clause — "When I **will arrive** in Rome" is wrong; you say "When I arrive". **Italian is the opposite** — with *quando/appena* + future meaning, BOTH halves go in futuro. Don't import the English rule. With *se* — usually the present in the condition (this is a "real" condition, see L42).

## Part 5: Future markers

These words signal the future. Use futuro semplice — or, for the near future, the plain present.

| Italian | English |
|---------|---------|
| <say>domani</say> | tomorrow |
| <say>dopodomani</say> | the day after tomorrow |
| <say>la prossima settimana</say> | next week |
| <say>il mese prossimo</say> | next month |
| <say>l'anno prossimo</say> | next year |
| <say>fra / tra un'ora</say> | in an hour |
| <say>fra dieci giorni</say> | in ten days |
| <say>fra poco</say> | soon |
| <say>presto</say> | soon, early |
| <say>più tardi</say> | later |
| <say>fra cinque anni</say> | in five years |
| <say>in futuro</say> | in the future |
| <say>un giorno</say> | one day (someday) |
| <say>quando avrò X anni</say> | when I'm X years old |
| <say>dopo</say> | after |
| <say>nel duemilatrenta</say> | in 2030 |

> **Note:** *fra* and *tra* are the same word ("in X time"); choose by euphony. *Fra dieci anni* / *Tra dieci anni* — both normal.

## Language scales

### Scale 1 — parlare (regular -are)

> <say>Domani io parlerò con Marco.</say>
> <say>Domani tu parlerai con Marco.</say>
> <say>Domani lui parlerà con Marco.</say>
> <say>Domani noi parleremo con Marco.</say>
> <say>Domani voi parlerete con Marco.</say>
> <say>Domani loro parleranno con Marco.</say>

### Scale 2 — essere (irregular)

> <say>L'anno prossimo io sarò in Italia.</say>
> <say>L'anno prossimo tu sarai in Italia.</say>
> <say>L'anno prossimo lui sarà in Italia.</say>
> <say>L'anno prossimo noi saremo in Italia.</say>
> <say>L'anno prossimo voi sarete in Italia.</say>
> <say>L'anno prossimo loro saranno in Italia.</say>

### Scale 3 — conjecture (sarà)

> <say>Chi è? Sarà Marco.</say>
> <say>Quanti anni ha? Avrà trent'anni.</say>
> <say>Dove sono? Saranno a casa.</say>
> <say>Che ora è? Saranno le dieci.</say>
> <say>Perché non risponde? Sarà occupata.</say>

### Scale 4 — quando + futuro

> <say>Quando arriverò a Roma, ti chiamerò.</say>
> <say>Quando arriverai a Roma, mi chiamerai.</say>
> <say>Quando arriverà a Roma, ci chiamerà.</say>
> <say>Quando arriveremo a Roma, ti chiameremo.</say>
> <say>Quando arriverete a Roma, mi chiamerete.</say>
> <say>Quando arriveranno a Roma, ci chiameranno.</say>

```
  Sarà occupata.          — She must be busy.
  English-bridge: "must be" / "probably" → Italian futuro.
  (English uses a modal verb; Italian uses the future tense.
   Different framing, same idea — don't translate word-for-word.)

WITH QUANDO / APPENA:
  Quando + futuro, futuro:  Quando arriverò, ti chiamerò.
  Appena + futuro, futuro:  Appena lo vedrò, glielo dirò.
  (Opposite of English — English forbids "will" in the when-clause!)
  With SE — usually the present in the condition: Se piove, restiamo.
```

> **Next up:** Lesson 26 — **condizionale**. Same nine irregular stems, different endings: *sarei, avrei, farei, andrei*. It's the "would" form — politeness, hypothesis, soft advice, dreams. Most useful tense for everyday polite speech: *vorrei un caffè* — "I'd like a coffee".
