# Lesson 9: Negation, questions and connectors
**Vocabulary: question words, negative words, conjunctions**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — three topics: negation with *non*, question words, sentence connectors.
2. **Train the scales** — five or six questions in a row, until the question words come out without delay.
3. **Notice the big thing:** in Italian, **double negation is the rule, not an error**. <say>Non vedo niente</say> = "I don't see anything" / "I see nothing" — and it's grammatically correct.
4. **Don't muddle** *perché* in a question with *perché* meaning "because" — same word, two roles.

> Grammatically, questions are the easiest part of Italian: stick the question word in front, the rest goes as usual. Your job here is to learn the **set of question words** and put them into your active speech.

---

## Part 1: Negation — *non* before the verb

You already saw the main rule in L7:

> **The word <say>non</say> goes directly before the conjugated verb.**

| Statement | Negation |
|---|---|
| <say>Parlo italiano.</say> | <say>Non parlo italiano.</say> |
| <say>Anna lavora qui.</say> | <say>Anna non lavora qui.</say> |
| <say>Capiamo tutto.</say> | <say>Non capiamo tutto.</say> |
| <say>Sono italiano.</say> | <say>Non sono italiano.</say> |
| <say>Ha una macchina.</say> | <say>Non ha una macchina.</say> |

> **The English-speaker's gift, restated:** English needs *do/does/don't/doesn't* in present-tense negation: "I don't speak", "she doesn't work". Italian doesn't. Just *non* + verb. <say>**Non parlo. Non lavora.**</say> That's it. You can throw the *do/does* machinery away.

> **Important:** *non* goes **before** the verb, not before the pronoun. <say>Io non parlo</say> — correct; **not** <say>non io parlo</say>.

---

## Part 2: Double negation — it's the norm!

In Italian (and Spanish, French, Russian — but unlike standard English) you **can and should** combine *non* with another negative word in the same clause:

| Structure | Example | Translation |
|---|---|---|
| *non* … *niente* | <say>Non vedo niente.</say> | I don't see anything. |
| *non* … *nessuno* | <say>Non conosco nessuno.</say> | I don't know anyone. |
| *non* … *mai* | <say>Non lavoro mai il sabato.</say> | I never work on Saturdays. |
| *non* … *più* | <say>Non fumo più.</say> | I don't smoke any more. |
| *non* … *ancora* | <say>Non ho ancora finito.</say> | I haven't finished yet. |
| *non* … *neanche* | <say>Non parla neanche inglese.</say> | He doesn't even speak English. |

> **The shift for English speakers:** in standard English, "I don't know nothing" is a double negative — frowned on in school. In Italian, "*non conosco nessuno*" (literally "I don't know nobody") is the only correct form. Drop the *non* and you've broken the sentence: ~~*vedo niente*~~ is wrong. It must be <say>**non vedo niente**</say>.

### When the negative word stands **before** the verb — *non* drops

If *niente, nessuno, mai* are placed **at the start of the clause**, before the verb, you drop *non*:

| Equivalent | With *non* | Without *non* |
|---|---|---|
| | <say>Non viene nessuno.</say> | <say>Nessuno viene.</say> |
| | <say>Non succede mai niente.</say> | <say>Niente succede mai.</say> |
| | <say>Non lavoro mai il sabato.</say> | <say>Mai lavoro il sabato.</say> (literary) |

> **The logic:** one negative word has to land somewhere. Before the verb — *non* is redundant. After the verb — *non* is required.

## Part 3: Asking a question — intonation or a question word

### Yes/no question — just intonation

Unlike English, **word order doesn't change**:

| Statement | Question |
|---|---|
| <say>Parli italiano.</say> | <say>Parli italiano?</say> |
| <say>Marco lavora a Roma.</say> | <say>Marco lavora a Roma?</say> |
| <say>Anna è italiana.</say> | <say>Anna è italiana?</say> |

In speech — voice up at the end. In writing — a question mark.

> **Massive simplification for English speakers.** Compare:
>
> | English (auxiliary-do machinery) | Italian (intonation only) |
> |---|---|
> | Do you speak Italian? | <say>Parli italiano?</say> |
> | Does Anna work in Rome? | <say>Anna lavora a Roma?</say> |
> | Don't you like coffee? | <say>Non ti piace il caffè?</say> |
>
> No "do", no inversion, no auxiliary. The whole apparatus of English present-tense questions just doesn't exist here. Big win.

### Alternative order: subject to the back

You can (but don't have to) push the subject to the end — it emphasises the subject:

- <say>Lavora a Roma Marco?</say> = <say>Marco lavora a Roma?</say>
- <say>È italiana Anna?</say> = <say>Anna è italiana?</say>

Both are correct. In neutral speech the normal order is more common.

### Answers — *sì* and *no*

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| <say>Parli italiano?</say> | <say>Sì.</say> / <say>Sì, parlo italiano.</say> / <say>No, non parlo italiano.</say> |
| <say>Anna è qui?</say> | <say>Sì, è qui.</say> / <say>No, non c'è.</say> |

> **Notice:** <say>sì</say> with the accent — that's "yes". Without the accent <say>si</say> is a completely different word (reflexive pronoun, L16).

## Part 4: Question words

| Word | English | Example |
|---|---|---|
| <say>chi</say> | who | <say>Chi parla?</say> — Who's speaking? |
| <say>che cosa</say> / <say>cosa</say> / <say>che</say> | what | <say>Cosa fai?</say> — What are you doing? |
| <say>dove</say> | where | <say>Dove abiti?</say> — Where do you live? |
| <say>quando</say> | when | <say>Quando arrivi?</say> — When are you arriving? |
| <say>perché</say> | why | <say>Perché non vieni?</say> — Why aren't you coming? |
| <say>come</say> | how | <say>Come stai?</say> — How are you? |
| <say>quanto</say> | how much (with mass nouns) | <say>Quanto costa?</say> — How much does it cost? |
| <say>quale</say> | which | <say>Quale libro vuoi?</say> — Which book do you want? |

> **Cognate gold:** *chi, come, quando, quanto, quale* — all from the Latin **qu-** family, just like English *who, when, what, which, how*. Same family tree.

### Three ways to say "what": *che, cosa, che cosa*

All three mean the same — "what":

- <say>Che fai?</say>
- <say>Cosa fai?</say>
- <say>Che cosa fai?</say>

You'll meet all three. *Cosa* is the commonest in modern spoken Italian. *Che cosa* is neutral and always correct. *Che* is shorter, slightly colloquial.

### *Quanto* — agrees in number/gender

When *quanto* sits before a noun, it acts like an adjective and agrees with it:

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| <say>quanto</say> (m.sg.) | <say>Quanto pane?</say> — How much bread? |
| <say>quanta</say> (f.sg.) | <say>Quanta acqua?</say> — How much water? |
| <say>quanti</say> (m.pl.) | <say>Quanti libri?</say> — How many books? |
| <say>quante</say> (f.pl.) | <say>Quante persone?</say> — How many people? |

On its own — "how much does it cost?" — <say>quanto costa?</say> — no agreement, because there's no noun.

### *Quale* — agrees in number

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| <say>quale</say> (sg.) | <say>Quale libro?</say> / <say>Quale macchina?</say> |
| <say>quali</say> (pl.) | <say>Quali libri?</say> / <say>Quali ragazze?</say> |

In the singular, one form covers m. and f. (pattern II of L6 adjectives).

### *Perché* — does double duty

The word <say>perché</say> works **both** as a question ("why?") **and** as the answer ("because"):

- <say>Perché non vieni?</say> — Why aren't you coming?
- <say>Perché sono stanco.</say> — Because I'm tired.

> **English splits these into "why" and "because" — different words. Italian uses one word for both.** Same trick in French (*pourquoi / parce que*) — but Italian is more economical. Don't be surprised.

### Question with a preposition — preposition goes **before** the question word

Unlike English ("where do you come **from**?", "who are you talking **to**?"), Italian puts the preposition at the very start:

- <say>Da dove vieni?</say> — Where are you from? (literally "From where do you come?")
- <say>A che ora?</say> — At what time?
- <say>Con chi parli?</say> — Who are you talking to? (literally "With whom do you speak?")
- <say>Di chi è questo libro?</say> — Whose book is this? (literally "Of whom is this book?")
- <say>Per quanto tempo?</say> — For how long?

> **Massive rule for English speakers:** **never leave the preposition at the end of an Italian sentence.** "Who are you talking to?" → <say>Con chi parli?</say>, not <say>parli a chi?</say>. This is "preposition stranding" — English does it freely, Italian forbids it. (Old-fashioned English teachers actually agree with Italian here: "the position to which one preposes" — same logic.)

## Part 5: Connectors — joins between clauses

| Word | English | Example |
|---|---|---|
| <say>e</say> | and | <say>Marco e Anna parlano italiano.</say> |
| <say>ma</say> | but | <say>Parlo italiano ma non perfettamente.</say> |
| <say>o</say> / <say>oppure</say> | or | <say>Prendi un caffè o un tè?</say> |
| <say>anche</say> | also, too | <say>Parlo anche francese.</say> |
| <say>perché</say> | because | <say>Non vengo perché sono stanco.</say> |
| <say>quando</say> | when (in a statement) | <say>Quando arrivo, ti chiamo.</say> |
| <say>se</say> | if | <say>Se piove, restiamo a casa.</say> |
| <say>quindi</say> | so, therefore | <say>Sono stanco, quindi vado a letto.</say> |
| <say>poi</say> | then, afterwards | <say>Prima mangio, poi studio.</say> |
| <say>però</say> | however, but | <say>Mi piace, però costa troppo.</say> |

> **Notice:** *quando* and *perché* are the same words as in questions. In a question they sit at the top with a ?; in a statement they work as connectors.

### *Anche* — position

*Anche* normally sits **directly before** the word it refers to:

- <say>Anche io parlo italiano.</say> — I speak Italian too. (= I'm not the only one)
- <say>Io parlo anche italiano.</say> — I also speak Italian (among other languages).
- <say>Parlo italiano anche con Anna.</say> — I speak Italian with Anna too.

> **Compare English "also/too":** English uses position too, but more loosely. Italian is stricter: *anche* almost always attaches to the next word.

### *E* before a vowel — *ed*

If the next word starts with **e**, Italians often write *ed* (for euphony):

- <say>Marco ed Elena</say> (rather than *Marco e Elena*)

It's standard but not strict. *Marco e Elena* also turns up.

## Language scales

### Scale 1 — ten questions in a row

> <say>Chi sei? Come ti chiami? Da dove vieni? Dove abiti? Cosa fai? Quanti anni hai? Quando arrivi? Perché studi italiano? Con chi vivi? A che ora finisci?</say>

Target: 15 seconds. All ten without a pause.

### Scale 2 — negations, double negations

> <say>Non parlo francese. Non capisco niente. Non conosco nessuno qui. Non lavoro mai il sabato. Non bevo più caffè. Non ho ancora finito. Non parla neanche inglese.</say>

### Scale 3 — connectors

> <say>Parlo italiano e inglese.</say>
> <say>Parlo italiano ma non perfettamente.</say>
> <say>Prendi un caffè o un tè?</say>
> <say>Anche io parlo italiano.</say>
> <say>Non vengo perché sono stanco.</say>
> <say>Se piove, resto a casa.</say>
> <say>Prima studio, poi mangio.</say>

## Part 6: Exercises

### Exercise 1. Make the sentence negative

1. Parlo italiano.
2. Anna lavora a Roma.
3. Capiamo tutto.
4. Sono italiano.
5. I bambini dormono.
6. Bevi vino?

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *Non parlo italiano.*
2. *Anna non lavora a Roma.*
3. *Non capiamo tutto.* (or stronger: *Non capiamo niente.*)
4. *Non sono italiano.*
5. *I bambini non dormono.*
6. *Non bevi vino?*

</details>

### Exercise 2. Double negation — translate

1. I don't see anything.
2. I don't know anyone here.
3. Anna never smokes.
4. I don't drink coffee any more.
5. I haven't understood yet.
6. He doesn't even speak English.
7. We eat neither meat nor fish.

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *Non vedo niente.* (or: *Non vedo nulla.*)
2. *Non conosco nessuno qui.*
3. *Anna non fuma mai.*
4. *Non bevo più caffè.*
5. *Non ho ancora capito.* (or: *Non capisco ancora.*)
6. *Non parla neanche inglese.* (or: *…neppure inglese / nemmeno inglese*)
7. *Non mangiamo né carne né pesce.*

Note that all of these need *non* before the verb, even though English wouldn't.

</details>

### Exercise 3. Insert the question word

1. ___ ti chiami? — Mi chiamo Marco.
2. ___ abiti? — Abito a Roma.
3. ___ vieni? — Vengo dalla Russia.
4. ___ fai? — Faccio il medico.
5. ___ arrivi? — Arrivo domani.
6. ___ non vieni? — Perché sono stanco.
7. ___ stai? — Sto bene.
8. ___ costa? — Dieci euro.
9. ___ anni hai? — Ho trent'anni.
10. ___ parli? — Con Anna.

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *Come*
2. *Dove*
3. *Da dove*
4. *Cosa* (or: *Che cosa / Che*)
5. *Quando*
6. *Perché*
7. *Come*
8. *Quanto*
9. *Quanti* (pl., agreeing with *anni*)
10. *Con chi*

</details>

### Exercise 4. Translate — question with a preposition

1. Who do you live with?
2. What are you (pl.) talking about?
3. Where are you (pl.) from?
4. At what time are you coming?
5. Whose book is this?
6. Who is this present for?
7. Until what time?

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *Con chi vivi?*
2. *Di che cosa parlate?* (or: *Di cosa parlate?*)
3. *Da dove venite?*
4. *A che ora vieni?* (or: *A che ora arrivi?*)
5. *Di chi è questo libro?*
6. *Per chi è questo regalo?*
7. *Fino a che ora?*

The main point: preposition **at the beginning**, never at the end. English "who … with?" becomes Italian "with whom …?".

</details>

### Exercise 5. Join the clauses with a connector

1. Parlo italiano. Parlo inglese. (and)
2. Parlo italiano. Non parlo francese. (but)
3. Prendi un caffè? Prendi un tè? (or)
4. Anche io parlo italiano. (too)
5. Non vengo. Sono stanco. (because)
6. Piove. Resto a casa. (if)
7. Sono stanco. Vado a letto. (therefore)

<details><summary>Key</summary>

1. *Parlo italiano e inglese.*
2. *Parlo italiano ma non parlo francese.* (or: *…però non parlo francese*)
3. *Prendi un caffè o un tè?*
4. *Anche io parlo italiano.*
5. *Non vengo perché sono stanco.*
6. *Se piove, resto a casa.*
7. *Sono stanco, quindi vado a letto.* (or: *…allora vado a letto*)

</details>

### Exercise 6. Read aloud (full run) — dialogue

> <say>— Ciao! Come ti chiami?
> — Mi chiamo Anna. E tu?
> — Io sono Marco. Da dove vieni?
> — Vengo dalla Russia. Sono di Mosca.
> — Ah, bene! Parli bene italiano!
> — Grazie, ma non perfettamente. Studio l'italiano da due anni.
> — Cosa fai nella vita?
> — Faccio la dottoressa. E tu?
> — Io faccio l'insegnante. Lavoro in una scuola.
> — Interessante. A che ora finisci di solito?
> — Finisco alle tre. Perché?
> — Perché vorrei un caffè. Vieni con me?
> — Volentieri! Andiamo!</say>

Run it 3 times. On the second pass, try playing both roles from memory.
