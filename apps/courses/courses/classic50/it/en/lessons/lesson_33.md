# Lesson 33: Congiuntivo after doubt, opinion, and in relative clauses
**Vocabulary: doubt, opinion, probability; searching with an indefinite antecedent**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** the rule: congiuntivo is the mood of **non-categorical** statements. Doubting, denying an opinion, weighing probability, looking for "anyone who fits" → congiuntivo.
2. **Say out loud** the pair *credo che venga / non credo che venga* — until the flip feels automatic.
3. **Drill the matrix** "do you think X will happen?" and "I have an X / I'm looking for an X" — these are the two most common contexts for today's rule.

> Remember the main thing: **congiuntivo lives in the zone of uncertainty.** As soon as the speaker stops claiming a fact and instead doubts, denies an opinion, or searches for any suitable object — the verb after *che* goes into congiuntivo.

---

## Part 1: The third trigger — doubt, opinion, probability

We already know the triggers from L32:
- **Will and desire** (*voglio che venga*).
- **Necessity** (*è necessario che venga*).
- **Emotion** (*sono contento che venga*).

Today — the **third trigger**: when the main clause expresses **doubt, opinion, or supposition**, the subordinate clause with *che* takes congiuntivo.

| What the main clause expresses | Mode in the subordinate |
|--------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **fact, assertion** (*so che, è vero che, è certo che*) | indicativo |
| **doubt, opinion, probability** (*dubito che, non penso che, può darsi che, credo che*) | **congiuntivo** |

The logic is simple: indicativo = "this is so". Congiuntivo = "maybe, maybe not / I'm not asserting it".

> **English speakers, take note:** English makes none of these switches. "I think he's coming" and "I don't think he's coming" both use the plain indicative. Italian flips the verb form. The decision is purely about what the main clause asserts — fact, or non-fact.

---

## Part 2: Verbs of doubt

| Italian | English | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **dubitare che** | to doubt that | <say>Dubito che venga oggi.</say> — I doubt he'll come today. |
| **non essere sicuro/a che** | to not be sure that | <say>Non sono sicuro che sia vero.</say> — I'm not sure it's true. |
| **non essere certo/a che** | to not be certain that | <say>Non sono certo che abbiano ragione.</say> — I'm not certain they're right. |
| **negare che** | to deny that | <say>Nega che sia colpa sua.</say> — He denies that it's his fault. |

> **Remember:** *dubitare che* **always** requires congiuntivo. Even when the doubt is weak — the mode stays the same.

### What about *non dubito che*?

If you remove the doubt (*non dubito che* — "I don't doubt that"), it becomes a **fact assertion** → indicativo:

> <say>Non dubito che viene domani.</say> — I don't doubt that he's coming tomorrow.
> <say>Dubito che venga domani.</say> — I doubt he's coming tomorrow.

---

## Part 3: The main flip — credo che / non credo che

This is **the most important** rule of the lesson. Italian's distinctive feature: verbs of opinion (*credere, pensare, ritenere, supporre, immaginare, sembrare, parere*) **always** require congiuntivo — both in affirmative and in negative. In this respect Italian goes further than English, and even further than Spanish (where the affirmative *creo que* triggers indicative).

| Italian | English |
|---------|---------|
| <say>Credo che venga.</say> | I think he'll come. |
| <say>Non credo che venga.</say> | I don't think he'll come. |
| <say>Penso che abbia ragione.</say> | I think he's right. |
| <say>Non penso che abbia ragione.</say> | I don't think he's right. |
| <say>Mi sembra che stia bene.</say> | It seems to me he's fine. |
| <say>Non mi sembra che stia bene.</say> | It doesn't seem to me he's fine. |
| <say>Suppongo che sappia tutto.</say> | I suppose he knows everything. |
| <say>Immagino che siano stanchi.</say> | I imagine they're tired. |

> **Main Italian rule:** after *credere che, pensare che, ritenere che, supporre che, immaginare che, sembrare che, parere che* — **always congiuntivo**, regardless of whether the verb is affirmative or negative.
> For English speakers, this is the biggest mental shift of L33: even a calm "I think he's right" pulls subjunctive. Think of it as: Italian uses the indicative only for **claimed facts**. "I think" is a claim about your own opinion, not about the world — so the world-clause stays in the non-asserting mood.

### Contrast: verbs of knowledge and perception → indicativo

| Italian | English | Mode |
|---------|---------|------|
| <say>So che viene.</say> | I know he's coming. | indicativo |
| <say>Vedo che lavori molto.</say> | I see that you work a lot. | indicativo |
| <say>Sono sicuro che ha ragione.</say> | I'm sure he's right. | indicativo |
| <say>È vero che vive qui.</say> | It's true he lives here. | indicativo |
| <say>È certo che parte.</say> | It's certain he's leaving. | indicativo |
| <say>È evidente che mente.</say> | It's evident he's lying. | indicativo |

> **Test:** *I know / see / am sure of a fact* → indicativo. *I think / it seems to me / I suppose / I doubt / I deny* → congiuntivo.

## Part 4: Impersonal expressions of probability

After these, **always** congiuntivo.

| Italian | English | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **è possibile che** | it's possible that | <say>È possibile che arrivi tardi.</say> |
| **è probabile che** | it's likely that | <say>È probabile che piova.</say> |
| **è impossibile che** | it's impossible that | <say>È impossibile che lo sappia.</say> |
| **è improbabile che** | it's unlikely that | <say>È improbabile che vengano.</say> |
| **può darsi che** | maybe (it could be that) | <say>Può darsi che abbia ragione.</say> |
| **non è detto che** | it's not certain that | <say>Non è detto che venga.</say> |
| **pare che** | it seems that | <say>Pare che ci sia uno sciopero.</say> |
| **sembra che** | it seems that | <say>Sembra che la situazione migliori.</say> |

> **Two key frozen phrases:** *può darsi che* (≈ "maybe") and *non è detto che* (≈ "not necessarily") — both **always** with congiuntivo.

### Adverbs of probability

| Italian | English | Note |
|---------|---------|------|
| forse | maybe | usually with indicativo |
| magari | maybe; if only | with congiuntivo: <say>Magari venisse!</say> "if only he'd come!" |
| probabilmente | probably | with indicativo |
| chissà | who knows | <say>Chissà se viene.</say> |

> **Subtle point:** *forse* and *probabilmente* in Italian usually take **indicativo** — this is a confident supposition. <say>Forse viene domani.</say> — Maybe he's coming tomorrow.
> But *può darsi, è possibile, è probabile, sembra, pare* — **only** congiuntivo.

## Part 6: Certainty scale

Arrange these phrases from "definitely yes" to "definitely no" — notice where the mode switches:

| Certainty | Construction | Mode |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| 100% yes | <say>So che viene.</say> | indicativo |
| 95% | <say>Sono sicuro che viene.</say> | indicativo |
| 80% | <say>Forse viene.</say> | indicativo |
| 60% | <say>Credo che venga.</say> / <say>Penso che venga.</say> | **congiuntivo** |
| 50/50 | <say>Può darsi che venga.</say> / <say>Sembra che venga.</say> | **congiuntivo** |
| 40% | <say>È possibile che venga.</say> | **congiuntivo** |
| 25% | <say>Dubito che venga.</say> / <say>Non credo che venga.</say> | **congiuntivo** |
| 10% | <say>È improbabile che venga.</say> | **congiuntivo** |
| 0% | <say>È impossibile che venga.</say> / <say>Nego che venga.</say> | **congiuntivo** |

> **The mode boundary** in Italian sits **higher** than in Spanish: already at *credo / penso* (60%) the congiuntivo kicks in. Memorize this feature.

## Part 7: The fourth trigger — relative clauses with an indefinite antecedent

Remember the relative pronouns from L29 (*che, cui, dove*)? Today they come back with a new rule.

> **The key question:** does the thing or person the relative clause describes actually exist (and do I know it)?
> - Yes, it exists, I know it → **indicativo**.
> - No, I'm looking, hoping to find one, or it doesn't exist → **congiuntivo**.

### Basic contrast pair

| Antecedent | Example | Translation | Why |
|------------|---------|-------------|-----|
| **known** | <say>Conosco una ragazza che parla russo.</say> | I know a girl who speaks Russian. | indicativo — a specific person I know |
| **sought** | <say>Cerco una ragazza che parli russo.</say> | I'm looking for a girl who speaks Russian. | congiuntivo — maybe none exists |
| **have** | <say>Ho un appartamento che ha tre stanze.</say> | I have an apartment with three rooms. | indicativo |
| **looking** | <say>Cerco un appartamento che abbia tre stanze.</say> | I'm looking for an apartment with three rooms. | congiuntivo |
| **known** | <say>Conosco un libro che spiega tutto.</say> | I know a book that explains everything. | indicativo |
| **sought** | <say>Cerco un libro che sia interessante.</say> | I'm looking for a book that's interesting. | congiuntivo |

> **The main idea:** the main verb can be the same one (*cercare, volere, avere bisogno di, conoscere, esserci*). It doesn't decide. What decides is **whether I know the specific object, or am looking for any suitable one**.
> English doesn't make this distinction at all — "I'm looking for a book that's interesting" and "I have a book that's interesting" both use the plain present. Italian shifts mode.

### Three situations that require congiuntivo in a relative clause

**Situation A: Looking for / needing.**

| Italian | English |
|---------|---------|
| <say>Cerco un libro che spieghi bene questo.</say> | I'm looking for a book that explains this well. |
| <say>Abbiamo bisogno di una persona che parli cinese.</say> | We need a person who speaks Chinese. |
| <say>Voglio una macchina che consumi poco.</say> | I want a car that uses little fuel. |
| <say>Conosci qualcuno che possa aiutarmi?</say> | Do you know anyone who could help me? |

**Situation B: Doesn't exist (negated antecedent).**

| Italian | English |
|---------|---------|
| <say>Non c'è nessuno che sappia la risposta.</say> | There's no one who knows the answer. |
| <say>Non conosco nessuno che viva a Bologna.</say> | I don't know anyone who lives in Bologna. |
| <say>Non esiste nulla che faccia miracoli.</say> | Nothing exists that works miracles. |

**Situation C: Hypothetical / whoever fits.**

| Italian | English |
|---------|---------|
| <say>Chiunque abbia interesse può venire.</say> | Whoever's interested can come. |
| <say>Il primo studente che arrivi aprirà la porta.</say> | The first student to arrive will open the door. |
| <say>Qualunque cosa tu dica, io la faccio.</say> | Whatever you say, I'll do it. |
