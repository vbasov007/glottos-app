# Italian course (50 lessons)
**Glottos Matrix — structured course for English speakers**

> Target level — a rich B2 with one foot in C1. Italian's grammar is close in shape to Spanish's, and 50 lessons cover the full system. What separates B2 from C1 isn't more grammar — it's lexis, register, idiomatics.

> Principle: 5% understand the rule, 95% train the mouth. Every topic runs through a question-answer matrix: io → tu → lui/lei → noi → voi → loro.

## BLOCK 1: A1 FOUNDATIONS (Lessons 1–10) → Test "Apprendista" (Apprentice)

*Goal: read confidently, build the present tense, start handling articles.*

| # | Grammar | Vocabulary |
|---|---------|------------|
| 1 | Read Italian aloud — almost as it's spelled. Hard vs soft c/g, double consonants, the rolled r | Greetings: ciao, buongiorno, grazie, prego, scusa; numbers 0–20 |
| 2 | Tell masculine from feminine nouns; build the plural | Everyday objects: house, food, animals; numbers 20–100 |
| 3 | Meet the articles (English doesn't have masculine/feminine ones) and their prepositional contractions | Places in town, days of the week, months |
| 4 | Verb *essere*: "I'm a teacher", "I'm from Boston". The subject pronoun is usually dropped — the ending already shows the person | Nationalities, professions, state adjectives |
| 5 | Verb *avere* and the "I HAVE hunger / thirst / cold / X years" idioms; *c'è / ci sono* (there is / there are) | Personal things, body parts, age, physical state |
| 6 | Describe with adjectives — agreement in gender and number, usually after the noun (unlike English) | Colour, size, quality, character |
| 7 | Conjugate regular -ARE verbs (parlare, lavorare, mangiare) — 80% of Italian verbs | Daily-action verbs |
| 8 | Conjugate -ERE and -IRE verbs; the key irregulars fare, andare, venire, stare, dare, dire | Verbs across the three conjugations + irregulars in context |
| 9 | Negate and ask questions (by intonation and with question words) | Question words, connectors e, ma, o, perché, anche |
| 10 | Connected text in the present — no new grammar, consolidation | Connectors, review of Block 1 topics |

**→ TEST "APPRENDISTA" (Apprentice): introduce yourself, ask directions, order at a bar, A1**

## How to work with each lesson

1. **Theory** — watch the video / read the rule (5 minutes)
2. **Translation** — translate phrases from English to Italian, check against the key
3. **Language scales** — run the new rule through every person (io → tu → lui → noi → voi → loro)
4. **Matrix** — drill through question-answer
5. **Mirror principle** — the answer is already in the question; don't build from scratch

## Memory hacks for English speakers

- **Articles aren't a small problem.** English has only "the" / "a/an" / nothing. Italian articles flex for gender and the noun's first sound, and they're never omitted where English would skip "the". The article thread runs through the whole course — L3, L7, L12, L29 — never "learned once".
- **Pronunciation is a gift.** Italian is almost phonetic: learn the rules in L1 and you can read any word. After English, where spelling is a treasure hunt, this feels supernatural.
- **Gender shows on the noun.** -o → masculine, -a → feminine, -e → either. Coming from English (no gender), the discipline is new but the cue is right there in the spelling.
- **The copula is mandatory.** "I a teacher" — wrong. *Sono insegnante* — right. English drops the verb in some phrases ("nice to meet you"); Italian almost never.
- **Two auxiliaries in the past.** *Avere* by default, *essere* for motion, change of state, and reflexives. With *essere* the participle agrees with the subject: *lei è andata, noi siamo arrivati* — a step English never asks for.
- **The subjunctive is alive.** Unlike English (where the subjunctive is half-fossilised: "if I were"), and unlike colloquial Spanish (which sometimes drops it), Italian uses it actively in everyday speech. It's a working B2 tool, not a museum piece.
- **Pronouns are a system, not a list.** Direct, indirect, *ci*, *ne*, combinations — L13–17 build them step by step. Front-loaded clitic placement (before the verb, attached to infinitives) feels foreign at first.
- **Cognates are your friend.** A huge Latinate stratum overlaps with English: *informazione, ristorante, ospedale, università, programma, problema*. Free vocabulary at the start. Watch out for false friends — *libreria* is "bookshop", not "library".
- **Passato remoto — read it, don't speak it.** In novels and southern speech it's everywhere; in standard northern speech it's gone. Learn to recognise (L42), not produce.
