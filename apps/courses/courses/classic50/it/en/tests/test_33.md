# Test for Lesson 33: Congiuntivo after doubt, opinion, and in relative clauses

**How do you say it in Italian?**
Translate the phrases into Italian. Pay special attention to the Italian peculiarity: verbs of opinion (*credere, pensare, sembrare*) pull congiuntivo both in affirmative and in negative. And — to the pair "known antecedent → indicativo / sought antecedent → congiuntivo".

---

1. I think he'll come.
2. I don't think he'll come.
3. I doubt this is true.
4. Maybe it'll rain. *(può darsi)*
5. It seems to be raining. *(sembra che)*
6. Maybe it'll rain. *(forse — regular indicativo!)*
7. I think you have time.
8. I don't think you have time.
9. I'm sure they're at home.
10. I'm not sure they're at home.
11. It seems to me she's right.
12. It doesn't seem to me she's right.
13. Maybe you're right.
14. There'll probably be traffic.
15. It's impossible he knows this.
16. It's unlikely they'll arrive on time.
17. I suppose they already know.
18. I don't think they already know.
19. It's not true that he lives here.
20. He denies that it's his fault.
21. I have a friend who lives in Rome.
22. I'm looking for a friend who lives in Rome.
23. Do you know anyone who speaks Russian?
24. There's no one here who speaks Russian.
25. I need an apartment that has two balconies.
26. I have an apartment that has two balconies.
27. Anyone who's interested can come.
28. I'm looking for a book that's interesting.
29. I don't know anyone who works on Sundays.
30. I doubt he's right, but maybe you're right.
