# Spanish Course Curriculum (50 units)
**Glottos Matrix — structural language-learning course**

> Principle: 5% understanding the rule, 95% training your mouth. Every topic gets drilled through the matrix: yo → tú → él/ella → nosotros → vosotros → ellos.

---

## BLOCK 1: A1 FOUNDATIONS (Lessons 1–10) → Test "Aprendiz / Aprendiza" (Apprentice)

*Goal: build the spine of the language — pronouns, present tense, basic agreement.*

| # | Grammar | Vocabulary |
|---|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Read Spanish aloud and put stress on the right syllable | Greetings and politeness: hola, buenos días, gracias, por favor, adiós, perdón |
| 2 | Tell masculine from feminine nouns; form plurals | Everyday objects: home, food, animals |
| 3 | Use el/la/un/una and the contractions al/del | Places around town, days of the week, months |
| 4 | The verb ser: "I am a teacher", "I'm from Spain" | Nationalities and countries, professions, family members |
| 5 | The verb estar: "I'm at home", "I'm tired" | Prepositions of place, feelings and physical states, rooms of a house |
| 6 | Describe things with adjectives that match in gender and number | Descriptive adjectives: colors, size, character |
| 7 | Conjugate regular verbs in the present (-AR, -ER, -IR) | Everyday-action verbs: hablar, comer, vivir, trabajar, estudiar, escribir, leer, comprar |
| 8 | Say "no" and ask wh-questions | qué, quién, cuándo, dónde, cómo, cuánto, por qué; nada, nadie, nunca |
| 9 | Tell ser vs estar apart — Spanish's signature contrast | Two-meaning adjectives, weather, time |
| 10 | hay ("there is/are") vs está/están ("is/are located") | mucho, poco, bastante, demasiado, algún; furniture and home setup, ordinal numbers 1–10 |

**→ TEST "APRENDIZ / APRENDIZA" (Apprentice): you can introduce yourself, order at a café, and find your way around town, level pre-A1 → A1**

## BLOCK 3: B1 PAST, FUTURE, COMMANDS (Lessons 21–30) → Test "Caballero / Dama" (Knight)

*Goal: tell stories, talk about plans, give commands.*

| # | Grammar | Vocabulary |
|---|---------|-----------|
| 21 | Simple past: "yesterday I bought, did" (pretérito indefinido) | Past markers: ayer, anoche, la semana pasada, hace dos años |
| 22 | Irregular verbs in the simple past: tuve, hice, fui | Irregular preterites, grouped by stem pattern |
| 23 | Habits and backdrop in the past — imperfecto ("I used to go") | Childhood, habits, adverbs of frequency: siempre, a menudo, normalmente |

| 25 | "I've already done it" — pretérito perfecto (he hablado) | ya, todavía, alguna vez, nunca; life experiences |
| 26 | "I had been there before" — pluscuamperfecto (había hablado) | Sequence in the past: antes, ya, cuando llegué |
| 27 | Future: "I'll go / I'm going to go" (ir a + inf, iré) | Future markers, plans, predictions |
| 28 | Conditional: "I'd want", "I'd do that" (condicional) | Polite formulas, hypothetical situations, advice |
| 29 | Commands and requests: "come!", "bring!" (affirmative imperative) | Instructions, recipes, directions, classroom commands |
| 30 | Prohibitions: "don't do that!" (negative imperative) | Prohibitions, rules, safety, signs |

## How to work with each unit

1. **Theory** — watch the video / read the rule (5 minutes)
2. **Translation** — translate phrases from English to Spanish, check against the key
3. **Language scales** — run the new rule through all persons (yo → tú → él → nosotros → vosotros → ellos)
4. **Matrix** — drill through Q&A in three scenarios: you (sg.) → you (pl.) → third persons
5. **Mirror principle** — the answer is already encoded in the question, don't build from scratch

## Memory hacks for English speakers

- **Gender**: 80% predictable from ending (-o → masc, -a → fem, -ción → fem, -dad → fem). Keep a short list of the exceptions: *el problema*, *el día*, *el mapa*, *la mano*, *la foto*, *la moto*. English has no gender, so assign one mentally from day one — say *la mesa*, not *the table*.
- **Stress + the tilde**: agudas (last syllable — needs a tilde if the word ends in vowel/n/s), llanas (second-to-last — needs a tilde if it does NOT end in vowel/n/s), esdrújulas (third-from-last — ALWAYS tilde). Spelling tells you exactly where the stress falls.
- **Ser vs estar**: ser = permanent (identity, origin, profession), estar = temporary / location / result. *Soy alta* (I am tall — always) vs *Estoy cansada* (I'm tired — right now).
- **Past tenses**: indefinido = "and that was it" (one action, a beginning or end), imperfecto = "..." (it lasted, was background, was a habit). *Comí* = "I ate" (one meal). *Comía* = "I used to eat / I was eating".
- **Subjunctive**: remember **WEIRDO** — **W**ishes, **E**motions, **I**mpersonal expressions, **R**ecommendations, **D**oubt/denial, **O**jalá. If your main clause is one of these, the verb in the *que* clause needs subjunctive.
- **Cognates**: Spanish gives English speakers a huge free vocabulary head start — *información*, *restaurante*, *universidad*, *imposible*, *natural*, *posible*, *especial*. Watch out for "false friends" like *embarazada* (pregnant, not embarrassed) and *éxito* (success, not exit).
- **Word order**: Spanish is more flexible than English. Adjectives usually come after the noun (*casa grande*, not *grande casa*); object pronouns come before the conjugated verb (*lo veo* — "I see it"); subject pronouns are usually dropped because the verb ending tells you who.
