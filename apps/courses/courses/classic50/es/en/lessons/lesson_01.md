# Lesson 1: Reading Spanish. The alphabet. Stress. Numbers 0–20
**Vocabulary: Alphabet, pronunciation, greetings**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — slowly, consciously, analyzing every sound
3. **Speed up** — repeat until the phrases fly out on their own

> Knowing the rule = 5%. Training your mouth = 95%.
> Good news: Spanish is **phonetic**. The way it's written is the way it's pronounced. No surprises.

---

## Part 1: The golden rule of Spanish reading

> **Spanish reads exactly the way it's written.** No silent letters trying to ambush you, no "hidden" sounds. Learn 15 rules and you'll read ANY Spanish word correctly.

Spanish is the most reading-friendly language in the Romance family. If you see *<say>casa</say>*, it's "KAH-sah" — no other interpretation possible. Compare that with English, where "though, through, tough, thought" all use the same letters and sound completely different. Spanish doesn't do that to you.

---

## Part 2: Vowels — five clean sounds

| Letter | Sound | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **a** | "ah" (always pure, like *father*) | *<say>casa</say>* — "KAH-sah" |
| **e** | "eh" (always open, like *bed*) | *<say>mesa</say>* — "MEH-sah" |
| **i** | "ee" (short, like *see*) | *<say>libro</say>* — "LEE-bro" |
| **o** | "oh" (always pure — NOT the schwa English does in unstressed syllables) | *<say>mono</say>* — "MOH-no" |
| **u** | "oo" (like *boot*) | *<say>uno</say>* — "OO-no" |

> **The single biggest trap for English speakers:** Spanish vowels **don't reduce**. English turns unstressed vowels into "uh" (the schwa) — *banana* sounds like "buh-NAN-uh". Spanish *<say>banana</say>* is "bah-NAH-nah" — every vowel stays loud and clear. If you pronounce *<say>casa</say>* as "KAH-suh", a Spanish speaker will hear it as a different word. Loud and equal — every vowel, every time.

### Diphthongs — two vowels in a row

They don't merge into one sound; they stay distinct but flow quickly together:

| Diphthong | Sound | Example |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **ai / ay** | "eye" | *<say>baile</say>* — "BUY-leh" |
| **ei / ey** | "ay" (like *day*) | *<say>seis</say>* — "SAYS" |
| **oi / oy** | "oy" (like *boy*) | *<say>hoy</say>* — "OY" |
| **au** | "ow" (like *cow*) | *<say>causa</say>* — "COW-sah" |
| **eu** | "eh-oo" (no perfect English match) | *<say>Europa</say>* — "eh-oo-ROH-pah" |
| **ia / ie / io** | "yah / yeh / yoh" | *<say>piano</say>* — "PYAH-no" |
| **ua / ue / uo** | "wah / weh / woh" | *<say>agua</say>* — "AH-gwah", *<say>bueno</say>* — "BWEH-no" |

## Part 3: Consonants — ten rules cover 90%

| Letter / combo | How to read | Example |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **h** | **ALWAYS silent** | *<say>hola</say>* — "OH-lah", *<say>hotel</say>* — "oh-TEL" |
| **j** | harsh "h" (think *loch* in Scottish, or German *Bach*) | *<say>jefe</say>* — "HEH-feh", *<say>José</say>* — "ho-SEH" |
| **g + e/i** | same harsh "h" (= j) | *<say>gente</say>* — "HEN-teh", *<say>gigante</say>* — "hee-GAHN-teh" |
| **g + a/o/u** | hard "g" (like *go*) | *<say>gato</say>* — "GAH-toh", *<say>grande</say>* — "GRAHN-deh" |
| **gu + e/i** | hard "g" (the u is silent) | *<say>guerra</say>* — "GEH-rrah", *<say>guitarra</say>* — "gee-TAH-rrah" |
| **gü + e/i** | "gw" (the ü makes u sound) | *<say>pingüino</say>* — "peen-GWEE-no" |
| **c + e/i** | in Spain = "th" (like *think*); in Latin America = "s" | *<say>cinco</say>* — "THEEN-ko" / "SEEN-ko" |
| **c + a/o/u** | "k" | *<say>casa</say>* — "KAH-sah", *<say>cuna</say>* — "KOO-nah" |
| **z** | in Spain = "th"; in Latin America = "s" | *<say>zapato</say>* — "thah-PAH-toh" / "sah-PAH-toh" |
| **qu + e/i** | "k" (the u is silent) | *<say>queso</say>* — "KEH-soh", *<say>quince</say>* — "KEEN-theh" / "KEEN-seh" |
| **ñ** | "ny" (like *canyon*) | *<say>España</say>* — "es-PAH-nyah", *<say>niño</say>* — "NEE-nyoh" |
| **ll** | in Spain = "y" (like *yes*); in Argentina = "zh/sh" | *<say>llamar</say>* — "yah-MAR" / "zhah-MAR" |
| **r** at start / **rr** | rolled "r-r-r" (you'll have to practice this — flick the tongue against the alveolar ridge) | *<say>rosa</say>* — "RROH-sah", *<say>perro</say>* — "PEH-rroh" |
| **r** in middle / end | single flap — closer to American "tt" in *butter* | *<say>pero</say>* — "PEH-roh", *<say>para</say>* — "PAH-rah" |
| **v** | pronounced as **"b"** (there is no English-style "v" sound in Spanish!) | *<say>vino</say>* — "BEE-no", *<say>vaca</say>* — "BAH-kah" |
| **ch** | "ch" (like English *church*) | *<say>chico</say>* — "CHEE-ko", *<say>coche</say>* — "KO-cheh" |
| **y** | "y" at start, "ee" at the end of a word | *<say>yo</say>* — "YOH", *<say>hoy</say>* — "OY" |

> **Trap #1:** *<say>Hola</say>* reads "**O**H-lah" — not "HO-lah". The h is silent. Forget it exists.
> **Trap #2:** *<say>Vino</say>* (wine) and *bino* sound **identical** to a Spanish ear. Even native speakers occasionally mix them up in writing.
> **Trap #3:** *<say>Que</say>* is "**KEH**", not "kweh" or "kway". After q, the u is always silent.
> **Trap #4:** *<say>Perro</say>* (dog) ≠ *<say>pero</say>* (but). Different word — the only difference is whether you roll the r. Worth practicing until your tongue cooperates.

## Part 4: Stress — three types of words

In Spanish, **every word** belongs to one of three categories. You can tell where the stress falls in **3 seconds** from a simple rule.

### Type 1: Aguda — stress on the LAST syllable

**Rule:** if the word ends in a **consonant** (except *n* or *s*), stress is on the last syllable — **no tilde needed**.

| Word | Pronunciation | Ending |
|------|---------------|--------|
| <say>ciudad</say> | "syoo-DAHD" | -d |
| <say>profesor</say> | "pro-feh-SOR" | -r |
| <say>feliz</say> | "feh-LEETH" | -z |
| <say>reloj</say> | "rreh-LOH" (the j is harsh "h") | -j |

If an *aguda* ends in a **vowel / -n / -s** — a **tilde IS required**:

| Word | Pronunciation | Why the tilde |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| <say>café</say> | "kah-FEH" | ends in a vowel, stress on the last syllable |
| <say>sofá</say> | "soh-FAH" | ends in a vowel |
| <say>canción</say> | "kahn-THYOHN" | ends in -n |
| <say>autobús</say> | "ow-toh-BOOS" | ends in -s |

### Type 2: Llana / Grave — stress on the SECOND-TO-LAST syllable

**Rule:** if the word ends in a **vowel / -n / -s** — stress on the second-to-last syllable, **no tilde**. This is the **most common** type in Spanish.

| Word | Pronunciation | Ending |
|------|---------------|--------|
| <say>casa</say> | "KAH-sah" | -a |
| <say>libro</say> | "LEE-bro" | -o |
| <say>examen</say> | "ek-SAH-men" | -n |
| <say>crisis</say> | "KREE-sees" | -s |
| <say>comen</say> | "KOH-men" | -n |

If a *llana* ends in a **consonant (other than n/s)** — a **tilde IS required**:

| Word | Pronunciation | Why the tilde |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| <say>árbol</say> | "AR-bol" | ends in -l, but stress on second-to-last |
| <say>azúcar</say> | "ah-THOO-kar" | ends in -r, but stress on second-to-last |
| <say>fácil</say> | "FAH-theel" | ends in -l |

### Type 3: Esdrújula — stress on the THIRD-FROM-LAST syllable

**Rule: ALWAYS takes a tilde.** No exceptions.

| Word | Pronunciation |
|------|---------------|
| <say>música</say> | "MOO-see-kah" |
| <say>miércoles</say> | "MYEHR-koh-less" |
| <say>América</say> | "ah-MEH-ree-kah" |
| <say>matemáticas</say> | "mah-teh-MAH-tee-kahs" |
| <say>teléfono</say> | "teh-LEH-foh-no" |

> **Memorize it this way:** see a tilde → stress is right there. No tilde → apply the ending rule: vowel/n/s → second-to-last; consonant → last.

## Part 5: Numbers 0–20

### Numbers 0–10 — memorize cold

| 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| <say>cero</say> | <say>uno</say> | <say>dos</say> | <say>tres</say> | <say>cuatro</say> | <say>cinco</say> |

| 6 | 7 | 8 | 9 | 10 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| <say>seis</say> | <say>siete</say> | <say>ocho</say> | <say>nueve</say> | <say>diez</say> |

**Pronunciation:** *<say>cero</say>* "THEH-ro" (Spain) / "SEH-ro" (LatAm), *<say>uno</say>* "OO-no", *<say>cinco</say>* "THEEN-ko", *<say>seis</say>* "SAYS", *<say>nueve</say>* "NWEH-beh" (v=b!), *<say>diez</say>* "DYETH" / "DYES".

### Numbers 11–15 (their own system)

| 11 | 12 | 13 | 14 | 15 |
|----|----|----|----|----|
| <say>once</say> | <say>doce</say> | <say>trece</say> | <say>catorce</say> | <say>quince</say> |

**Pronunciation:** *<say>once</say>* "OHN-theh", *<say>quince</say>* "KEEN-theh" (qu = "k", u silent!).

### Numbers 16–19 (compound: dieci- + digit)

| 16 | 17 | 18 | 19 |
|----|----|----|----|
| <say>dieciséis</say> | <say>diecisiete</say> | <say>dieciocho</say> | <say>diecinueve</say> |

> **Notice the tilde on *<say>dieciséis</say>*!** It became *aguda* (stress on the last syllable), ends in -s — so the tilde is required.

### Number 20

**<say>veinte</say>** — "BAYN-teh" (v=b, ei = "ay").

## Part 6: Vocabulary — Greetings and basic politeness

| Spanish | English | Note |
|---------|---------|------|
| <say>Hola</say> | Hi / Hello | universal, any time of day |
| <say>Buenos días</say> | Good morning | until ~1 p.m. |
| <say>Buenas tardes</say> | Good afternoon / Good evening | ~1 p.m. – sunset |
| <say>Buenas noches</say> | Good night | after sunset + before bed |
| <say>Adiós</say> | Bye / Goodbye | universal |
| <say>Hasta luego</say> | See you later | literally "until later" |
| <say>Hasta pronto</say> | See you soon | |
| <say>Hasta mañana</say> | See you tomorrow | |
| <say>Gracias</say> | Thank you | |
| <say>Muchas gracias</say> | Thank you very much | |
| <say>Por favor</say> | Please | when making a request |
| <say>De nada</say> | You're welcome | response to *<say>gracias</say>* |
| <say>Sí</say> | Yes | |
| <say>No</say> | No | |
| <say>Perdón</say> | Sorry / Excuse me | universal |
| <say>Disculpe</say> | Excuse me (formal) | before asking a stranger something |
| <say>¿Cómo está usted?</say> | How are you? | formal |
| <say>¿Cómo estás?</say> | How are you? | informal |
| <say>Bien, gracias</say> | Fine, thanks | |
| <say>Muy bien</say> | Very well | |
| <say>Mal</say> | Bad | |
| <say>Regular</say> / <say>Más o menos</say> | So-so | |
| <say>¿Cómo se llama usted?</say> | What's your name? | formal |
| <say>¿Cómo te llamas?</say> | What's your name? | informal |
| <say>Me llamo…</say> | My name is… (lit. "I call myself…") | |
| <say>Mucho gusto</say> | Nice to meet you | |
| <say>Encantado</say> / <say>Encantada</say> | Pleased to meet you (m. / f.) | use the form that matches YOUR gender |

> **The tú vs usted thing:** Spanish has two "you"s. *<say>Tú</say>* is informal (friends, family, kids, anyone you'd be on first-name terms with). *<say>Usted</say>* is formal (strangers, older people, business contacts, anyone you'd address as Mr./Mrs.). It's like old English thou/you — except Spanish kept the distinction alive. When in doubt with an adult, start with *<say>usted</say>*. They'll switch you to *<say>tú</say>* if appropriate.

7. *fácil* — **llana** (ends in -l → tilde required)
8. *matemáticas* — **esdrújula** (tilde — mandatory)
9. *ciudad* — **aguda** (-d)

### Exercise 4. Translate the phrases

Translate from English to Spanish:

1. Hi! What's your name?
2. My name is Marco. Nice to meet you.
3. Good evening! How are you? (formal)
4. Fine, thanks. And you? (formal)
5. Sorry, goodbye!

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

### Exercise 5. Inverted punctuation marks

Spanish is the **only** language that opens questions and exclamations with **upside-down** marks (¿ and ¡). They're flags telling the reader "heads up — question/exclamation coming".

Translate and add the marks:

1. What's your name? (informal)
2. Hi!
3. How are you? (formal)
4. Nice to meet you!

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *¿Cómo te llamas?*
2. *¡Hola!*
3. *¿Cómo está usted?*
4. *¡Mucho gusto!*

Remember: the **opening** ¿ or ¡ marks the **start** of the question or exclamation, the **closing** ? or ! marks the end. People often skip the opening mark in texting and social media, but in proper writing it's required.

</details>

### Exercise 6. Dialog — read both voices out loud
