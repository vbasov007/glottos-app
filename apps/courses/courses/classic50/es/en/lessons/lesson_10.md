# Lesson 10: Hay vs está/están. Impersonal haber. Quantifiers
**Vocabulary: furniture, home setup, ordinal numbers 1–10, mucho/poco/bastante/demasiado/algún**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the difference between *<say>hay</say>* (existence/availability) and *<say>está/están</say>* (location of something specific) (5 minutes)
2. **Say it out loud** — every pair in contrast: "there is a table" vs "the table is in the kitchen"
3. **Speed up** — until the choice between *hay* and *está* becomes automatic

> This is the closing lesson of Block A1. After this the spine of the language is assembled: *<say>ser</say>*, *<say>estar</say>*, *<say>hay</say>*, present tense, agreement, negation, questions.

---

## Part 1: The main rule — hay vs está/están

English has one phrase — "there is / there are" — for two completely different jobs. Spanish splits them in two:

> **<say>Hay</say>** = "there is / there are" (something **exists**, you're announcing it as new — no specific identity).
> **<say>Está</say> / <say>están</say>** = "is / are" (something **specific** is located somewhere — you already know which one).

| Hay | Está / Están |
|-----|--------------|
| **existence**, **availability** | **location** of something known |
| with **<say>un</say> / <say>una</say> / <say>unos</say> / <say>unas</say>**, numbers, quantifiers | with **<say>el</say> / <say>la</say> / <say>los</say> / <say>las</say>**, names, possessives |
| introduces something **new** | talks about something already identified |
| **always one form** — *<say>hay</say>* (sg. and pl.!) | agrees: *<say>está</say>* (sg.) / *<say>están</say>* (pl.) |

| Hay (new, impersonal) | Está / Están (specific) |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| *<say>Hay un libro en la mesa.</say>* — There is a book on the table. | *<say>El libro está en la mesa.</say>* — The book is on the table. |
| *<say>Hay tres sillas.</say>* — There are three chairs. | *<say>Las sillas están en la cocina.</say>* — The chairs are in the kitchen. |
| *<say>¿Hay una farmacia cerca?</say>* — Is there a pharmacy nearby? | *<say>¿Dónde está la farmacia?</say>* — Where is the pharmacy? |
| *<say>Hay mucha gente.</say>* — There are a lot of people. | *<say>María está aquí.</say>* — María is here. |

> **The decision test:** can you slot in "the / my / Juan" before the noun? → *<say>está</say> / <say>están</say>*. Are you slotting in "a / two / a lot of"? → *<say>hay</say>*.

## Part 2: Hay — impersonal, never changes

> **<say>Hay</say>** is the third-person-singular form of the verb *<say>haber</say>* used impersonally. **It never agrees** with anything — not number, not gender. One form, always.

| English | Spanish |
|---------|---------|
| There is a table. | *<say>Hay una mesa.</say>* |
| There **are** tables. | *<say>Hay mesas.</say>* (NOT ~~*han mesas*~~) |
| There are **many** tables. | *<say>Hay muchas mesas.</say>* |
| There **aren't** any tables. | *<say>No hay mesas.</say>* |
| Is there **anything**? | *<say>¿Hay algo?</say>* |
| There's **nothing**. | *<say>No hay nada.</say>* |

> **Trap #1:** *<say>Hay</say>* is **one word**, not *ha + y*. Pronounced "**eye**" — exactly like English "eye".
> **Trap #2:** Never say ~~*hayn*~~ or ~~*hayan*~~ for the plural. *<say>Hay</say>* is impersonal — it stays *<say>hay</say>* no matter how many things there are.
> **Trap #3:** ~~*Hay el libro*~~ is **forbidden**. *<say>Hay</say>* cannot be followed by a definite article. If you want to say "the book is on the table", you need *<say>está</say>*: *<say>el libro está en la mesa</say>*.

The reason for Trap #3: *<say>hay</say>* introduces something new and unspecified into the conversation. The moment a noun is "the X" (= you and I both know which X), it's no longer new — so it's no longer *<say>hay</say>* territory. Switch to *<say>está</say>*.

## Part 3: Quantifiers — how much, how many?

Quantifiers behave like adjectives in Spanish: most of them agree with their noun in gender and number. A handful are invariable.

### Agreeing: mucho, poco, demasiado, todo, otro

| Form | Example |
|------|---------|
| *<say>mucho</say> / <say>mucha</say> / <say>muchos</say> / <say>muchas</say>* | *<say>Hay mucha agua.</say>* — There's a lot of water. / *<say>muchos libros</say>* — a lot of books. |
| *<say>poco</say> / <say>poca</say> / <say>pocos</say> / <say>pocas</say>* | *<say>Hay poca gente.</say>* — There are few people. / *<say>pocos coches</say>* — few cars. |
| *<say>demasiado</say> / <say>demasiada</say> / <say>demasiados</say> / <say>demasiadas</say>* | *<say>Hay demasiado ruido.</say>* — There's too much noise. |
| *<say>todo</say> / <say>toda</say> / <say>todos</say> / <say>todas</say>* | *<say>todos los días</say>* — every day / all the days. |
| *<say>otro</say> / <say>otra</say> / <say>otros</say> / <say>otras</say>* | *<say>otra silla</say>* — another chair. **No article!** Not ~~*una otra silla*~~. |

> **English speaker note:** English uses "much" for uncountables (*much water*) and "many" for countables (*many books*). Spanish doesn't make that distinction — *<say>mucho</say>* covers both. You only have to pick the right gender/number ending.

### Invariable in gender: bastante, suficiente, más, menos

| Form | Example |
|------|---------|
| *<say>bastante</say> / <say>bastantes</say>* (number only) | *<say>Hay bastante comida.</say>* — There's enough food. / *<say>bastantes problemas</say>* — enough problems. |
| *<say>suficiente</say> / <say>suficientes</say>* (number only) | *<say>Hay suficiente tiempo.</say>* — There's enough time. |
| *<say>más</say>* (invariable) | *<say>Hay más sillas aquí.</say>* — There are more chairs here. |
| *<say>menos</say>* (invariable) | *<say>Hay menos gente hoy.</say>* — There are fewer people today. |

### Algún / ningún — "some" / "any / no"

Before a **masculine singular** noun, *<say>alguno</say>* and *<say>ninguno</say>* drop the final *-o* — same shortening trick as *<say>uno</say> → <say>un</say>*, *<say>bueno</say> → <say>buen</say>*, *<say>primero</say> → <say>primer</say>*. This is called **apocopation** (from L6).

| Full form | Apocopated (before m.sg.) | Example |
|-----------|----------------------------|---------|
| *<say>alguno</say>* | **<say>alguno → algún</say>** | *<say>algún día</say>* — some day / one day |
| *<say>ninguno</say>* | **<say>ninguno → ningún</say>** | *<say>ningún problema</say>* — no problem (at all) |
| *<say>alguna</say> / <say>algunos</say> / <say>algunas</say>* | unchanged | *<say>alguna idea</say>* — any/some idea |
| *<say>ninguna</say>* | unchanged | *<say>ninguna duda</say>* — no doubt |

> *<say>Ningún</say> / <say>ninguna</say>* are almost always **singular**: "no books" in Spanish is literally "not a single book". *<say>No hay ningún libro</say>* = there isn't a single book.

## Part 4: Hay + quantifier — the workhorse pattern

This is the base structure for describing any space, any room, any situation.

| Spanish | English |
|---------|---------|
| *<say>En el salón hay un sofá y dos sillones.</say>* | In the living room there's a sofa and two armchairs. |
| *<say>Hay muchos libros en la estantería.</say>* | There are a lot of books on the shelf. |
| *<say>No hay nadie en la oficina.</say>* | There's nobody in the office. |
| *<say>¿Hay algún restaurante por aquí?</say>* | Is there any restaurant around here? |
| *<say>Hay bastante espacio.</say>* | There's enough space. |
| *<say>Hay demasiada gente.</say>* | There are too many people. |
| *<say>No hay ningún problema.</say>* | There's no problem (at all). |

> **Notice:** *<say>no hay nadie</say>*, *<say>no hay nada</say>*, *<say>no hay ningún</say>*. Spanish requires the **double negative** (from L8): *<say>no</say>* before the verb AND the negative word after. English drops the first *no* ("there's nothing"); Spanish keeps both.

## Part 5: Hay vs estar — common situations side by side

| Situation | Hay (existence) | Está / están (specific location) |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Asking if something exists | *<say>¿Hay una farmacia cerca?</say>* | — |
| Asking where a specific thing is | — | *<say>¿Dónde está la farmacia?</say>* |
| Listing furniture in a room | *<say>Hay una mesa, dos sillas y un sofá.</say>* | — |
| Saying where our sofa stands | — | *<say>El sofá está al lado de la ventana.</say>* |
| How many people in the hall | *<say>Hay mucha gente.</say>* | — |
| Where María is | — | *<say>María está en la cocina.</say>* |
| Weather (from L9) | *<say>Hay sol. Hay viento. Hay niebla.</say>* | — |
| State / condition | — | *<say>Está nublado. Estoy bien.</say>* |

> **Weather note:** some weather expressions use *<say>hay</say>* (*<say>hay sol, hay viento, hay nubes, hay niebla, hay tormenta</say>*) and others use *<say>está</say>* (*<say>está nublado, está despejado, está soleado</say>*). The split is simple: *<say>hay</say>* + noun (sun, wind, fog), *<say>está</say>* + adjective/participle (cloudy, clear, sunny).

## Part 6: Ordinal numbers 1–10

These work as adjectives: they agree in gender and number with the noun. They usually sit **before** the noun (unlike most Spanish adjectives).

| # | Masculine | Feminine |
|---|-----------|----------|
| 1st | <say>primero</say> (→ **<say>primer</say>** before m.sg.) | <say>primera</say> |
| 2nd | <say>segundo</say> | <say>segunda</say> |
| 3rd | <say>tercero</say> (→ **<say>tercer</say>** before m.sg.) | <say>tercera</say> |
| 4th | <say>cuarto</say> | <say>cuarta</say> |
| 5th | <say>quinto</say> | <say>quinta</say> |
| 6th | <say>sexto</say> | <say>sexta</say> |
| 7th | <say>séptimo</say> | <say>séptima</say> |
| 8th | <say>octavo</say> | <say>octava</say> |
| 9th | <say>noveno</say> | <say>novena</say> |
| 10th | <say>décimo</say> | <say>décima</say> |

> **Apocopation trap:** *<say>primero</say>* and *<say>tercero</say>* drop the *-o* before a masculine singular noun (just like *<say>uno</say>*, *<say>alguno</say>*, *<say>bueno</say>*, *<say>malo</say>* — pattern from L6):
> *<say>el primer día</say>* (the first day), but *<say>la primera vez</say>* (the first time);
> *<say>el tercer piso</say>* (the third floor), but *<say>la tercera puerta</say>* (the third door).
>
> Only *<say>primero</say>* and *<say>tercero</say>* apocopate. *<say>Segundo</say>, <say>cuarto</say>, <say>quinto</say>*... stay as they are: *<say>el segundo día</say>*, *<say>el cuarto piso</say>*.

| Example | Translation |
|---------|-------------|
| *<say>Vivo en el primer piso.</say>* | I live on the first floor. |
| *<say>Es mi tercera clase de español.</say>* | This is my third Spanish class. |
| *<say>La quinta puerta a la derecha.</say>* | The fifth door on the right. |
| *<say>Es la segunda vez.</say>* | This is the second time. |

> **Beyond 10, ordinals go away in spoken Spanish.** From 11 onwards, native speakers usually switch to **cardinal numbers**: *<say>el siglo XXI</say>* is read *<say>el siglo veintiuno</say>* ("century twenty-one"), not *<say>vigésimo primero</say>*. *<say>El piso 15</say>* = "the 15th floor", said as *<say>el piso quince</say>*. The "proper" ordinals (*<say>undécimo</say>, <say>duodécimo</say>, <say>vigésimo</say>*...) exist but feel bookish or legal — don't worry about them at this stage.

> **English speaker note:** the Spanish "first floor" (*<say>primer piso</say>*) is what Americans call the **second** floor — the ground floor (*<say>planta baja</say>*) doesn't count as "first" in Spain or most of Latin America. So *<say>vivo en el tercer piso</say>* often means three flights up from the street.

## Part 8: Language matrix — hay vs está

Run each pair out loud until the choice is automatic.

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| <say>¿Hay un sofá en el salón?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Sí, hay un sofá.</say> |
| <say>¿Dónde está el sofá?</say> (answer: by the window) | <say>El sofá está al lado de la ventana.</say> |
| <say>¿Hay libros en la estantería?</say> (answer: yes, many) | <say>Sí, hay muchos libros.</say> |
| <say>¿Dónde están mis libros?</say> (answer: on the shelf) | <say>Tus libros están en la estantería.</say> |
| <say>¿Hay alguien en casa?</say> (answer: no, nobody) | <say>No, no hay nadie.</say> |
| <say>¿Dónde está María?</say> (answer: in the kitchen) | <say>María está en la cocina.</say> |
| <say>¿Hay algún problema?</say> (answer: no problem) | <say>No, no hay ningún problema.</say> |
| <say>¿Cuántas sillas hay?</say> (answer: four) | <say>Hay cuatro sillas.</say> |
| <say>¿Hay bastante comida?</say> (answer: yes) | <say>Sí, hay bastante.</say> |
| <say>¿Está abierta la tienda?</say> (answer: no, closed) | <say>No, está cerrada.</say> |
