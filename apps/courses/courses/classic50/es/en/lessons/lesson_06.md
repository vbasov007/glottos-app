# Lesson 6: Adjectives — agreement, position, apocopation
**Vocabulary: Descriptive adjectives — colors, size, character, appearance**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — get the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — drill noun + adjective pairs through all four forms (m.sg., f.sg., m.pl., f.pl.)
3. **Speed up** — repeat until agreement is automatic

> A Spanish adjective is the **shadow of the noun**. Wherever the noun goes, the adjective follows: same gender, same number.
> Bad news for English speakers: English adjectives don't change. *Red car, red house, red cars, red houses* — one form fits all. Good news: in Spanish you only get four forms (m.sg., f.sg., m.pl., f.pl.), and the pattern is rigidly logical.

---

## Part 1: The main rule — the adjective agrees with the noun

In English we say "red car", "red house", "red flowers" — the adjective never moves. In Spanish, **four forms**, and you have to pick the right one every single time.

| Form | Ending (Type 1: -o/-a) | Example |
|------|------------------------|---------|
| m. sg. | **-o** | <say>el coche **rojo**</say> — the red car |
| f. sg. | **-a** | <say>la casa **roja**</say> — the red house |
| m. pl. | **-os** | <say>los coches **rojos**</say> — the red cars |
| f. pl. | **-as** | <say>las casas **rojas**</say> — the red houses |

> **Remember Lesson 2:** *<say>el coche</say>* is masculine (despite ending in -e!), *<say>la casa</say>* is feminine. The adjective takes its gender from the **noun**, not from its own ending.

There's a second twist: Spanish adjectives normally come **after** the noun. English fixes them before: *the red car*. Spanish flips them: *<say>el coche rojo</say>* (literally "the car red"). That order is the default; we'll get to the exceptions in Part 3.

---

## Part 2: Three types of adjectives by ending

### Type A: ending -o (4 forms)

The most common type. Both gender and number change.

| | m. sg. | f. sg. | m. pl. | f. pl. |
|---|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| tall | <say>alto</say> | <say>alta</say> | <say>altos</say> | <say>altas</say> |
| red | <say>rojo</say> | <say>roja</say> | <say>rojos</say> | <say>rojas</say> |
| pretty | <say>bonito</say> | <say>bonita</say> | <say>bonitos</say> | <say>bonitas</say> |

### Type B: ending -e or a consonant (2 forms)

Gender **doesn't change**. Only number does.

| | sg. (m. = f.) | pl. (m. = f.) |
|---|---------------|----------------|
| big | <say>grande</say> | <say>grandes</say> |
| green | <say>verde</say> | <say>verdes</say> |
| easy | <say>fácil</say> | <say>fáciles</say> |
| young | <say>joven</say> | <say>jóvenes</say> (tilde added because the syllable count grew!) |
| intelligent | <say>inteligente</say> | <say>inteligentes</say> |

> *<say>un libro grande</say> / <say>una mesa grande</say>* — same form for both genders.
> Plural rule: consonant + **-es**, vowel + **-s**.

### Type C: nationalities and adjectives in certain consonants (4 forms — exception to Type B)

If a consonant-ending adjective denotes a **nationality** or ends in **-or / -ón / -án / -ín**, it **takes an -a** in the feminine.

| | m. sg. | f. sg. | m. pl. | f. pl. |
|---|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Spanish | <say>español</say> | <say>española</say> | <say>españoles</say> | <say>españolas</say> |
| French | <say>francés</say> | <say>francesa</say> | <say>franceses</say> | <say>francesas</say> |
| talkative | <say>hablador</say> | <say>habladora</say> | <say>habladores</say> | <say>habladoras</say> |
| hardworking | <say>trabajador</say> | <say>trabajadora</say> | <say>trabajadores</say> | <say>trabajadoras</say> |

> **Trap:** *<say>francés</say>* → *<say>francesa</say>* loses its tilde in the feminine. (The stress is now on the second-to-last syllable, and a *llana* ending in a vowel needs no tilde — see Lesson 1.)

## Part 3: Position — usually AFTER the noun

> **Default:** the adjective comes **after** the noun. This is the opposite of English.

| English | Spanish |
|---------|---------|
| a red car | <say>un coche **rojo**</say> |
| a tall man | <say>un hombre **alto**</say> |
| a Spanish wine | <say>un vino **español**</say> |
| an interesting book | <say>un libro **interesante**</say> |

Train your ear: in Spanish, the noun arrives first and tells you *what* you're talking about, the adjective tags on second and tells you *which kind*. English does the reverse.

### When the adjective goes BEFORE the noun

1. **Numerals and quantifiers:** *<say>dos libros</say>, <say>muchas casas</say>, <say>pocos amigos</say>.*
2. **Demonstratives and possessives:** *<say>este libro</say>, <say>mi coche</say>.* (Lessons 13–14.)
3. **A few short, common adjectives** — *<say>bueno</say>, <say>malo</say>, <say>grande</say>, <say>pequeño</say>* — sliding before the noun feels natural and slightly more literary or emotional.
4. **When you want a subjective / evaluative shade** instead of a neutral description (see Part 5).

---

## Part 4: Apocopation — short forms before a noun

A handful of adjectives **lose their final vowel** in front of a masculine singular noun. This shortening is called **apocopation**.

| Full form | Apocopated | When | Example |
|-----------|------------|------|---------|
| <say>bueno</say> | **<say>buen</say>** | before m.sg. | *<say>un buen amigo</say>* (a good friend), but *<say>una buena amiga</say>*, *<say>unos buenos amigos</say>* |
| <say>malo</say> | **<say>mal</say>** | before m.sg. | *<say>un mal día</say>* (a bad day), but *<say>una mala idea</say>* |
| <say>primero</say> | **<say>primer</say>** | before m.sg. | *<say>el primer día</say>*, but *<say>la primera vez</say>* |
| <say>tercero</say> | **<say>tercer</say>** | before m.sg. | *<say>el tercer piso</say>*, but *<say>la tercera vez</say>* |
| <say>uno</say> | **<say>un</say>** | before m.sg. | *<say>un libro</say>*, but *<say>una casa</say>* |
| <say>alguno</say> | **<say>algún</say>** | before m.sg. | *<say>algún día</say>*, but *<say>alguna idea</say>* |
| <say>ninguno</say> | **<say>ningún</say>** | before m.sg. | *<say>ningún problema</say>*, but *<say>ninguna razón</say>* |
| <say>grande</say> | **<say>gran</say>** | before **any** singular | *<say>un gran amigo</say>*, *<say>una gran idea</say>*, but *<say>unos grandes amigos</say>* |

> **Trap:** *<say>grande</say>* apocopates before **both** genders in the singular, but **not** in the plural.
> **Trap:** *<say>algún</say>*, *<say>ningún</say>* gain a tilde when they lose the -o (see Lesson 1: aguda ending in -n needs a tilde).

## Part 5: Adjectives whose meaning shifts with position

A small but high-impact class. **Before** the noun → figurative / subjective meaning. **After** the noun → literal / objective.

| Adjective | BEFORE (figurative) | AFTER (literal) |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------|
| **<say>gran(de)</say>** | great | big in size |
| | *<say>un gran hombre</say>* — a great man | *<say>un hombre grande</say>* — a big man (physically) |
| **<say>viejo</say>** | old = longtime | old = aged |
| | *<say>un viejo amigo</say>* — a longtime friend | *<say>un amigo viejo</say>* — an elderly friend |
| **<say>pobre</say>** | poor = unfortunate | poor = without money |
| | *<say>un pobre hombre</say>* — a poor (pitiful) man | *<say>un hombre pobre</say>* — a poor (penniless) man |
| **<say>nuevo</say>** | new = different one | new = just made |
| | *<say>un nuevo coche</say>* — a new (different) car for me | *<say>un coche nuevo</say>* — a brand-new car (off the lot) |
| **<say>mismo</say>** | the same | itself |
| | *<say>el mismo día</say>* — the same day | *<say>el día mismo</say>* — the day itself |

> **The hack:** before = **emotion**, after = **fact**.
> A *<say>gran amigo</say>* is your treasured friend. An *<say>amigo grande</say>* is the friend who plays rugby and could pick up a fridge.

## Part 6: An adjective with two nouns

When one adjective covers two nouns:

| Situation | Rule | Example |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Both masculine | m. pl. | *<say>un libro y un coche rojos</say>* |
| Both feminine | f. pl. | *<say>una casa y una mesa rojas</say>* |
| Mixed genders | **m. pl. wins** | *<say>un libro y una mesa rojos</say>* |

> Mixed-gender group → masculine wins. The same rule applies to *<say>nosotros</say> / <say>ellos</say>*.

## Part 8: Language scales — drill through all forms

Take a noun + adjective combo and run it through 4 forms. This is the single most important drill of the lesson.

### Scale 1: *<say>el chico alto</say>* (the tall boy)

| Form | Article + noun + adjective |
|------|----------------------------|
| m. sg. | <say>el chico **alto**</say> |
| f. sg. | <say>la chica **alta**</say> |
| m. pl. | <say>los chicos **altos**</say> |
| f. pl. | <say>las chicas **altas**</say> |

### Scale 2: *<say>un libro interesante</say>* (an interesting book — Type B)

| Form | Article + noun + adjective |
|------|----------------------------|
| m. sg. | <say>un libro **interesante**</say> |
| f. sg. | <say>una mesa **interesante**</say> |
| m. pl. | <say>unos libros **interesantes**</say> |
| f. pl. | <say>unas mesas **interesantes**</say> |

### Scale 3: *<say>un profesor español</say>* (a Spanish teacher — Type C)

| Form | Article + noun + adjective |
|------|----------------------------|
| m. sg. | <say>un profesor **español**</say> |
| f. sg. | <say>una profesora **española**</say> |
| m. pl. | <say>unos profesores **españoles**</say> |
| f. pl. | <say>unas profesoras **españolas**</say> |

### Scale 4: apocopation of *<say>bueno</say>*

| Position | Form |
|----------|------|
| before m.sg. | <say>un **buen** amigo</say> |
| before f.sg. | <say>una **buena** amiga</say> |
| before m.pl. | <say>unos **buenos** amigos</say> |
| before f.pl. | <say>unas **buenas** amigas</say> |
| after m.sg. | <say>un amigo **bueno**</say> |

> Only the masculine singular shortens.

## Part 9: Language matrix — describe people with ser and estar

Hook this lesson onto Lessons 4 (ser) and 5 (estar). **Remember the contrast:**
- **<say>Ser</say> + adjective** = permanent characteristic (he's intelligent, she's tall).
- **<say>Estar</say> + adjective** = temporary state (he's tired, she's happy).

### Matrix A: SER (characteristic)

| Subject | verb | adjective | Translation |
|---------|------|-----------|-------------|
| <say>Yo</say> | <say>soy</say> | <say>alto</say> / <say>alta</say> | I am tall (m./f.) |
| <say>Tú</say> | <say>eres</say> | <say>inteligente</say> | You are intelligent |
| <say>Él</say> | <say>es</say> | <say>trabajador</say> | He is hardworking |
| <say>Ella</say> | <say>es</say> | <say>trabajadora</say> | She is hardworking |
| <say>Nosotros</say> | <say>somos</say> | <say>jóvenes</say> | We are young |
| <say>Vosotras</say> | <say>sois</say> | <say>guapas</say> | You are good-looking (f.) |
| <say>Ellos</say> | <say>son</say> | <say>españoles</say> | They are Spanish |
| <say>Ellas</say> | <say>son</say> | <say>habladoras</say> | They are talkative |

### Matrix B: ESTAR (state)

| Subject | verb | adjective | Translation |
|---------|------|-----------|-------------|
| <say>Yo</say> | <say>estoy</say> | <say>cansado</say> / <say>cansada</say> | I am tired |
| <say>Tú</say> | <say>estás</say> | <say>contento</say> / <say>contenta</say> | You are happy |
| <say>Él</say> | <say>está</say> | <say>enfermo</say> | He is sick |
| <say>Nosotros</say> | <say>estamos</say> | <say>ocupados</say> | We are busy |
| <say>Vosotras</say> | <say>estáis</say> | <say>tristes</say> | You are sad |
| <say>Ellos</say> | <say>están</say> | <say>nerviosos</say> | They are nervous |

> **Drill:** take each line and turn it into a question: *<say>¿Eres alto? — Sí, soy alto.</say>* Cycle through all the persons.

### Exercise 7. Micro-dialogue

Read it aloud. Notice every agreement.

> — Es alta, delgada y muy simpática. Tiene los ojos verdes.

<details>
<summary>Masculine version</summary>

> — ¿Cómo es tu hermano?

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
ADJECTIVE = SHADOW OF THE NOUN.
Agrees in GENDER and NUMBER. English never does this — Spanish always does.

THREE TYPES BY ENDING:
  Type A:  -o / -a / -os / -as   (rojo, alto, bueno)        4 forms
  Type B:  -e or consonant       (grande, verde, joven)     2 forms (sg./pl.)
  Type C:  nationalities + -or   (español, trabajador)      4 forms (take -a!)

POSITION:
  Default — AFTER the noun:    un coche rojo, una casa grande
  BEFORE the noun:
    - numerals/quantifiers:    dos libros, muchas casas
    - evaluative/emotional:    un buen amigo, una gran idea
    - meaning-shifters (below)

APOCOPATION (drop the final vowel before m.sg.):
  bueno   → buen     (un buen día)
  malo    → mal      (un mal día)
  primero → primer   (el primer día)
  tercero → tercer   (el tercer piso)
  uno     → un       (un libro)
  alguno  → algún    (algún día)        + TILDE
  ninguno → ningún   (ningún problema)  + TILDE
  grande  → gran     (before ANY sg.!  un gran hombre / una gran idea)

MEANING BY POSITION (before = emotion, after = fact):
  gran hombre   = a great man         /  hombre grande = a big man
  viejo amigo   = a longtime friend   /  amigo viejo   = an elderly friend
  pobre hombre  = a poor (pitiful)    /  hombre pobre  = a penniless man
  nuevo coche   = a different car     /  coche nuevo   = a brand-new car

TWO NOUNS OF DIFFERENT GENDERS:
  Masculine wins: un libro y una mesa rojos.

SER vs ESTAR (Lessons 4–5 recap):
  ser + adj   = characteristic (es alto, es simpática)
  estar + adj = state          (está cansada, están contentos)

INVARIABLE COLORS:
  naranja, rosa — NEVER agree:  una camisa naranja, unas flores rosa.
```
