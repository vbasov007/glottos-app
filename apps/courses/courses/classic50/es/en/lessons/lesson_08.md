# Lesson 8: Negation and basic questions
**Vocabulary: Question words, negative pronouns, connectors**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes)
2. **Say it out loud** — each "question → answer" pair, both voices
3. **Run the matrix** through all persons; **speed up** until questions and negations fly out on their own

> Today you step out of statement-only mode. Without questions and negations you read the news, you don't hold a conversation. After today, you can actually **trade lines**.

---

## Part 1: Negation — one word, one slot

> **Rule #1:** drop *<say>no</say>* **directly in front of the verb** — and you're done. No auxiliaries, no inversions, no "do not".

| Statement | Negation |
|-----------|----------|
| <say>Hablo español.</say> | <say>**No** hablo español.</say> |
| <say>Comemos en casa.</say> | <say>**No** comemos en casa.</say> |
| <say>María trabaja aquí.</say> | <say>María **no** trabaja aquí.</say> |
| <say>Vivís en Madrid.</say> | <say>**No** vivís en Madrid.</say> |

Much simpler than English, which has to insert "do/does/don't/doesn't" from thin air: "I speak Spanish" → "I **do not** speak Spanish". Spanish just inserts one word.

> **Trap:** object pronouns (Lesson 15) squeeze in **between** *<say>no</say>* and the verb: *<say>No lo sé</say>* — "I don't know". Memorize **<say>No lo sé</say>** as a formula.

### The short "no" answer

Two *<say>no</say>*s in a row is **normal**: *— <say>¿Hablas inglés?</say> — <say>**No, no** hablo inglés.</say>* First *<say>no</say>* = the answer; second *<say>no</say>* = the verb-negation. Don't fuse them, don't drop either one.

---

## Part 2: Double negation — yes, required

> **Rule #2:** if a negative word (*<say>nada</say>, <say>nadie</say>, <say>nunca</say>, <say>ningún</say>*) sits **after** the verb, you MUST put *<say>no</say>* before the verb as well. If it sits **before** the verb, the *<say>no</say>* drops.

| Negative word | Meaning |
|---------------|---------|
| **<say>nada</say>** | nothing / anything |
| **<say>nadie</say>** | nobody / anybody |
| **<say>nunca</say>** / **<say>jamás</say>** | never / ever |
| **<say>ningún</say> / <say>ninguna</say>** | no / not any (one) |
| **<say>tampoco</say>** | neither / not… either |
| **<say>ni … ni</say>** | neither … nor |

### Two patterns — pick either

| After the verb (needs *<say>no</say>*) | Before the verb (no *<say>no</say>*) |
|------------------------------|----------------------------|
| <say>**No** como **nada**.</say> | <say>**Nada** como.</say> *(rare)* |
| <say>**No** habla **nadie**.</say> | <say>**Nadie** habla.</say> |
| <say>**No** trabajo **nunca** los domingos.</say> | <say>**Nunca** trabajo los domingos.</say> |
| <say>**No** tengo **ningún** problema.</say> | — |
| <say>**No** bebo **ni** café **ni** té.</say> | <say>**Ni** café **ni** té bebo.</say> *(literary)* |
| <say>Yo **tampoco**.</say> | — |

> **Trap #1 (the big one):** *<say>No como nada</say>* literally reads "I don't eat nothing". English forbids the double negative — "I don't eat nothing" supposedly means "I do eat something". **Spanish does the opposite: it REQUIRES the double negative**, and the negatives never cancel. *<say>No veo nada</say>* = "I don't see anything". Drop either *<say>no</say>* or *<say>nada</say>* and the sentence breaks.

> **Trap #2:** *<say>ningún</say>* before a masculine singular noun loses its -o: *<say>ningún libro</say>*, but *<say>ninguna casa</say>*. The plural is rare — *<say>ningún</say>* already means "not even one".

> **Trap #3:** *<say>también</say>* (also, positive) ↔ *<say>tampoco</say>* (also-not, negative). English has the same split (also/too vs neither/either): *<say>Hablo francés. — Yo también.</say>* / *<say>No hablo francés. — Yo tampoco.</say>*

## Part 3: Yes/no questions — intonation and inversion

> **Rule #3:** general questions (yes/no) can be made **three ways** — all three are correct.

| Method | Example | Comment |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Intonation only** | <say>¿Tú hablas español?</say> | Statement order, voice up at the end. Most common in speech. |
| **Subject inversion** | <say>¿Hablas tú español?</say> | Subject after the verb. Slightly formal / emphatic. |
| **Subject at the end** | <say>¿Habla español tu hermano?</say> | Long subjects get pushed to the tail. |

No "do" / "does" — English needs a dummy verb ("**Do** you speak Spanish?"); Spanish doesn't.

> Don't forget the **upside-down ¿** at the very start. Not decoration — it flags "question coming". Spanish is the only major language to mark questions and exclamations at both ends.

### Yes / no answer

| Question | Affirmative answer | Negative answer |
|----------|--------------------|-----------------|
| <say>¿Hablas español?</say> | <say>**Sí**, hablo español.</say> | <say>**No**, no hablo español.</say> |
| <say>¿Trabajáis aquí?</say> | <say>**Sí**, trabajamos aquí.</say> | <say>**No**, no trabajamos aquí.</say> |

Note the person shift: *<say>tú</say>* → answer with *<say>yo</say>*; *<say>vosotros</say>* → *<say>nosotros</say>*. The **mirror principle** — it runs through the whole course.

## Part 4: Question words — learn them as a set

> **Rule #4:** every Spanish question word **carries a written tilde** (even when in unstressed position the same syllable would have none). The tilde is the flag for "this is a question word".

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **<say>¿Qué?</say>** | What? Which? | <say>¿Qué comes?</say> / <say>¿Qué libro?</say> |
| **<say>¿Quién?</say> / <say>¿Quiénes?</say>** | Who? (sg. / pl.) | <say>¿Quién habla?</say> / <say>¿Quiénes son?</say> |
| **<say>¿Cuándo?</say>** | When? | <say>¿Cuándo llegas?</say> |
| **<say>¿Dónde?</say>** | Where? | <say>¿Dónde vives?</say> |
| **<say>¿Adónde?</say> / <say>¿A dónde?</say>** | Where to? | <say>¿Adónde vas?</say> |
| **<say>¿De dónde?</say>** | Where from? | <say>¿De dónde eres?</say> |
| **<say>¿Cómo?</say>** | How? What … like? | <say>¿Cómo estás?</say> / <say>¿Cómo es Marco?</say> |
| **<say>¿Cuánto?</say> / <say>¿Cuánta?</say> / <say>¿Cuántos?</say> / <say>¿Cuántas?</say>** | How much? / How many? *(agrees with the noun)* | <say>¿Cuántos años tienes?</say> / <say>¿Cuánta agua bebes?</say> |
| **<say>¿Cuál?</say> / <say>¿Cuáles?</say>** | Which? Which one (of)? | <say>¿Cuál es tu nombre?</say> |
| **<say>¿Por qué?</say>** | Why? | <say>¿Por qué no comes?</say> |
| **<say>Porque</say>** *(no tilde, one word)* | Because *(the answer)* | <say>Porque no tengo hambre.</say> |

### Cognate alert

The Romance *cu-/qu-* and the English *wh-* both come from the same Indo-European *kw-* sound: *<say>quién</say>* ≈ *who*, *<say>cuándo</say>* ≈ *when*, *<say>cómo</say>* ≈ *how*, *<say>cuánto</say>* ≈ *quantity*, *<say>cuál</say>* ≈ *which/quality*. Spot the pattern and the list is half-memorized.

### The big pair of traps

> **Trap ¿qué? vs ¿cuál?:**
> - *<say>¿Qué es esto?</say>* = "What is this (thing)?" — asking for a definition.
> - *<say>¿Cuál es tu nombre?</say>* = "What's your name?" — picking one out of a set.
> - In front of *<say>es</say>* + a noun, Spanish almost always uses **<say>¿cuál?</say>**, NOT *<say>¿qué?</say>*. So: *<say>¿Cuál es tu teléfono?</say>*, *<say>¿Cuál es la capital?</say>*. English uses "what" for both; Spanish doesn't.

> **Trap ¿por qué? vs porque (every learner mixes these up):**
>
> | Form | What it is | Example |
> |------|------------|---------|
> | **<say>¿por qué?</say>** | "why?" — **two words, tilde on qué** | <say>¿Por qué trabajas?</say> |
> | **<say>porque</say>** | "because" — **one word, no tilde, no space** | <say>Porque necesito dinero.</say> |
>
> English has the same split: "**why** do you work?" / "**because** I need money" — two completely different words. Spanish writes them with the same letters, which is exactly why learners conflate them.

### The agreeing *<say>cuánto</say>*

Unlike English's two separate phrases "how much / how many", Spanish has ONE word that **agrees in gender and number** — same four-way endings as *<say>bueno</say> / <say>buena</say> / <say>buenos</say> / <say>buenas</say>* from Lesson 6:

- *<say>¿**Cuánto** dinero tienes?</say>* (masc. sg., uncountable)
- *<say>¿**Cuánta** agua bebes?</say>* (fem. sg., uncountable)
- *<say>¿**Cuántos** años tienes?</say>* (masc. pl., countable)
- *<say>¿**Cuántas** hermanas tienes?</say>* (fem. pl., countable)

### Word order in a special question

**Question word → verb → (subject).** Examples: *<say>¿Qué comes (tú)?</say>*, *<say>¿Dónde vive María?</say>*, *<say>¿Cuándo trabajan ellos?</say>*, *<say>¿Por qué estudias español?</say>*

> **Rule #5:** in a Spanish question, the subject **almost never sits in front of the verb**. *<say>¿Dónde tú vives?</say>* sounds like an English calque. Correct: *<say>¿Dónde vives (tú)?</say>*.

## Part 5: Connectors

The minimum glue to link your phrases:

| Connector | Meaning | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **<say>y</say>** / **<say>e</say>** *(before i-/hi-)* | and | <say>Hablo español **y** francés.</say> / <say>España **e** Italia.</say> |
| **<say>o</say>** / **<say>u</say>** *(before o-/ho-)* | or | <say>¿Té **o** café?</say> / <say>siete **u** ocho</say> |
| **<say>pero</say>** | but (general contrast) | <say>Estudio mucho, **pero** no hablo bien.</say> |
| **<say>sino</say>** | but (after negation, direct replacement) | <say>No estudio inglés, **sino** español.</say> |
| **<say>también</say>** / **<say>tampoco</say>** | also / also-not | <say>Yo **también** trabajo.</say> / <say>Yo **tampoco**.</say> |
| **<say>porque</say>** | because | <say>No como **porque** no tengo hambre.</say> |

> **Rule #6 (y → e, o → u):** before the sound "ee" or "oh", the connector swaps form to avoid merging. About **sound**, not spelling — *<say>y</say>* stays before *<say>hierro</say>* ("yerro", starts with "y"), flips to *<say>e</say>* before *<say>Italia</say>*.

> **pero vs sino:** *<say>sino</say>* needs (a) a negative first clause AND (b) a direct contradiction. *<say>No es alto, sino bajo</say>* — "not tall, but (rather) short". *<say>Pero</say>* = everything else: *<say>No es alto, pero juega bien</say>* — "He's not tall, but he plays well". Contrasting detail → *<say>pero</say>*; replacement → *<say>sino</say>*.

## Part 6: Vocabulary recap

**Negatives + answer words:** *<say>nada</say>* (nothing/anything), *<say>nadie</say>* (nobody/anybody), *<say>nunca</say> / <say>jamás</say>* (never; *<say>jamás</say>* is more emphatic), *<say>ningún</say> / <say>ninguna</say>* (not any; *<say>ningún</say>* before a masc. sg. noun), *<say>tampoco</say>* (neither, also-not), *<say>ni … ni</say>* (neither … nor), *<say>sí</say>* (yes — tilde!), *<say>no</say>* (no / not).

**Question words** (Part 4): *<say>qué</say>, <say>quién(es)</say>, <say>cuándo</say>, <say>dónde</say>, <say>adónde</say>, <say>de dónde</say>, <say>cómo</say>, <say>cuánto/-a/-os/-as</say>, <say>cuál(es)</say>, <say>por qué</say>*.

**Connectors:** *<say>y</say> / <say>e</say>* (and), *<say>o</say> / <say>u</say>* (or), *<say>pero</say>* (but), *<say>sino</say>* (but, after negation), *<say>también</say> / <say>tampoco</say>*, *<say>porque</say>* (because).

> **Spelling trap:** *<say>sí</say>* (yes, tilde) vs *si* (if, no tilde). The tilde here isn't about stress — it's a **distinguishing tilde** for homographs. Same trick: *<say>tú</say>* vs *tu*, *<say>él</say>* vs *el*, *<say>sé</say>* vs *se*, *<say>qué</say>* vs *que*.

## Part 7: Language scales — negation through every person

Drill all six persons — *<say>hablar</say>* (single *<say>no</say>*) and *<say>comer</say> + <say>nunca</say>* (both schemas) — out loud until they fly:

| Person | *<say>no</say> + <say>hablar</say>* | Schema A: *<say>no … nunca</say>* | Schema B: *<say>nunca …</say>* |
|--------|---------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| <say>yo</say> | <say>no hablo inglés</say> | <say>No como nunca carne.</say> | <say>Nunca como carne.</say> |
| <say>tú</say> | <say>no hablas inglés</say> | <say>No comes nunca carne.</say> | <say>Nunca comes carne.</say> |
| <say>él</say> / <say>ella</say> / <say>usted</say> | <say>no habla inglés</say> | <say>No come nunca carne.</say> | <say>Nunca come carne.</say> |
| <say>nosotros</say> | <say>no hablamos inglés</say> | <say>No comemos nunca carne.</say> | <say>Nunca comemos carne.</say> |
| <say>vosotros</say> | <say>no habláis inglés</say> | <say>No coméis nunca carne.</say> | <say>Nunca coméis carne.</say> |
| <say>ellos</say> / <say>ellas</say> / <say>ustedes</say> | <say>no hablan inglés</say> | <say>No comen nunca carne.</say> | <say>Nunca comen carne.</say> |

## Part 8: Language matrix — question → answer

Mirror principle: the answer is already encoded in the question. Person shift: *<say>tú</say> → <say>yo</say>*, *<say>vosotros</say> → <say>nosotros</say>*.

| Question | Affirmative answer | Negative answer |
|----------|--------------------|-----------------|
| <say>¿Hablas español?</say> | <say>Sí, hablo español.</say> | <say>No, no hablo español.</say> |
| <say>¿Qué estudias?</say> | <say>Estudio medicina.</say> | <say>No estudio nada.</say> |
| <say>¿Cuándo trabajáis?</say> | <say>Trabajamos los lunes.</say> | <say>No trabajamos nunca.</say> |
| <say>¿Quién habla francés?</say> | <say>Marco habla francés.</say> | <say>Nadie habla francés.</say> |
| <say>¿Por qué no comes?</say> | <say>Porque no tengo hambre.</say> | — |
| <say>¿Cuántos hermanos tienes?</say> | <say>Tengo dos hermanos.</say> | <say>No tengo ningún hermano.</say> |
| <say>¿Cuál es tu nombre?</say> | <say>Mi nombre es Ana.</say> | — |
| <say>¿De dónde sois?</say> | <say>Somos de EE.UU.</say> | <say>No somos de aquí.</say> |

Run it **three times**: read, cover the right column, then cover both and work from memory.

## Part 9: Exercises

### Exercise 1. Put *no* in the right slot

Make each sentence negative:

1. Hablo italiano.
2. María vive en Barcelona.
3. Comemos pasta los domingos.
4. Los estudiantes trabajan mucho.
5. Pedro y Ana son médicos.
6. Estoy cansado.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *No hablo italiano.*
2. *María no vive en Barcelona.*
3. *No comemos pasta los domingos.*
4. *Los estudiantes no trabajan mucho.*
5. *Pedro y Ana no son médicos.*
6. *No estoy cansado.*

> *no* always sits **directly in front of the verb**, even when a subject precedes it.

</details>

### Exercise 2. Double negation

Translate — give **both versions** (with *no … X* and with *X … verb*) where both are natural:

1. I never work on Saturdays.
2. Nobody speaks Chinese.
3. I don't eat anything.
4. I don't have a single book. *(only one version)*
5. We don't work either.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *No trabajo nunca los sábados.* / *Nunca trabajo los sábados.*
2. *No habla nadie chino.* / *Nadie habla chino.* (the second is more natural)
3. *No como nada.* / *Nada como.* (the second is rare, literary)
4. *No tengo ningún libro.* (only normal word order)
5. *Nosotros tampoco trabajamos.* / *Tampoco trabajamos nosotros.*

> "I don't eat nothing" is ungrammatical in English; *No como nada* is the **only** way to say it in Spanish. Double negative = required, not banned.

</details>

### Exercise 3. Pick the question word

Insert *qué, quién, cuándo, dónde, cómo, cuánto(s)/cuánta(s), cuál, por qué*:

1. ¿___ vives? — Vivo en Madrid.
2. ¿___ es tu profesor? — Mi profesor es Pedro.
3. ¿___ años tienes? — Tengo veinte años.
4. ¿___ estudias español? — Porque me gusta.
5. ¿___ es tu número de teléfono? — Es 555-1234.
6. ¿___ eres? — Soy de Estados Unidos.
7. ¿___ agua bebes al día? — Dos litros.
8. ¿___ hermanas tienes? — Tengo tres.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *¿Dónde vives?*
2. *¿Quién es tu profesor?*
3. *¿Cuántos años tienes?* (*años* — masc. pl. → *cuántos*)
4. *¿Por qué estudias español?* — **Porque** me gusta. *(question: two words + tilde; answer: one word, no tilde)*
5. *¿Cuál es tu número de teléfono?* (before *es +* noun, almost always *cuál*, not *qué*)
6. *¿De dónde eres?*
7. *¿Cuánta agua bebes al día?* (*agua* — fem. sg. → *cuánta*)
8. *¿Cuántas hermanas tienes?* (*hermanas* — fem. pl. → *cuántas*)

</details>

### Exercise 4. Build the question to match the answer

The **bolded** part is what you're asking about:

1. Vivo en **París**.
2. Tengo **tres** hermanos.
3. Estudiamos español **porque** queremos vivir en España.
4. Soy **alta y morena**.
5. Comen pasta **los domingos**.
6. Soy **de Argentina**.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *¿Dónde vives?*
2. *¿Cuántos hermanos tienes?*
3. *¿Por qué estudiáis español?* — question = **¿por qué?**, answer uses **porque**.
4. *¿Cómo eres?* (description / character → *cómo*, NOT *qué*)
5. *¿Cuándo comen pasta?*
6. *¿De dónde eres?*

</details>

### Exercise 5. Translate from English

1. Who is that girl? — She is my sister. Her name is Laura.
2. Where do you work and what do you do? — I work at a school. I'm a teacher.
3. Why don't you speak English? — Because we're only studying Spanish.
4. I never drink coffee. — Me neither.
5. How many books do you have? — I don't have a single book at home.
6. What's your phone number? — I don't know, sorry.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *¿Quién es esa chica? — Es mi hermana. Se llama Laura.*
2. *¿Dónde trabajas y qué haces? — Trabajo en una escuela. Soy profesor.* (no article before the profession with *ser* — see Lesson 4)
3. *¿Por qué no habláis inglés? — Porque estudiamos solo español.* (question with tilde, answer without)
4. *No bebo nunca café. / Nunca bebo café. — Yo tampoco.*
5. *¿Cuántos libros tienes? — No tengo ningún libro en casa.* (*ningún* — apocopated before a masc. sg. noun)
6. *¿Cuál es tu número de teléfono? — No lo sé, perdón.* (*cuál* before *es* + noun; *no lo sé* is the standard formula)

</details>

### Dialog — read both voices out loud

> — ¡Hola! ¿Cómo te llamas?
> — Me llamo Daniel. ¿Y tú?
> — Yo soy Elena. ¿De dónde eres?
> — Soy de México. ¿Y tú? ¿Eres española?
> — No, no soy española. Soy estadounidense, pero vivo en Madrid.
> — ¿Por qué vives en Madrid?
> — Porque trabajo aquí. Soy médica.
> — ¡Qué interesante! ¿Qué haces los fines de semana?
> — Nada. Nunca tengo tiempo libre.
> — Yo tampoco.

Repeat 3 times. Then cover the text and reconstruct it from memory.

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
NEGATION:    no + verb    (always — no "do" needed)
  No hablo español.   María no trabaja aquí.

DOUBLE NEGATION (REQUIRED, never cancels):
  no + verb + nada/nadie/nunca/ningún
  OR  nada/nadie/nunca + verb   (no "no")

  No como nada.     =  Nada como. (rare)
  No habla nadie.   =  Nadie habla.
  No trabajo nunca. =  Nunca trabajo.

  No veo nada = "I don't see anything" (literally "I don't see nothing").
  Required in Spanish, ungrammatical in English. Negatives stack.

NEG. WORDS: nada / nadie / nunca / jamás / ningún(-a) / tampoco / ni…ni

YES/NO QUESTIONS (no "do"!):
  1. Intonation:        ¿Tú hablas español?
  2. Inversion:         ¿Hablas tú español?
  3. Subject to tail:   ¿Habla español tu hermano?

QUESTION WORDS (all take a tilde!):
  ¿Qué?  ¿Quién(es)?  ¿Cuándo?  ¿Dónde? / ¿Adónde? / ¿De dónde?
  ¿Cómo?  ¿Cuánto/-a/-os/-as? (AGREES!)  ¿Cuál(es)?  ¿Por qué?

WORD ORDER:   question word + verb + (subject)
  ¿Dónde vives (tú)?     NOT: ¿Dónde tú vives?

BIG TRAPS:
  ¿Qué es esto? vs ¿Cuál es tu nombre? → cuál, NOT qué, before es+noun
  ¿por qué? (2 words + tilde)  vs  porque (1 word, no tilde, no space)
  sí (yes) vs si (if)    tú vs tu    él vs el

INVERTED PUNCTUATION:  ¿…?  ¡…!   (Spanish flags both ends.)

CONNECTORS:
  y → e before "ee" (España e Italia)   o → u before "oh" (siete u ocho)
  pero (general contrast)  vs  sino (after negation, replacement)
  también ↔ tampoco        porque = because

MIRROR:  ¿Tú hablas? → Sí, (yo) hablo.   ¿Vosotros trabajáis? → (nosotros) trabajamos.
```
