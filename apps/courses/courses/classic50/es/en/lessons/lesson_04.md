# Lesson 4: Pronouns and the verb ser. Who you are, where you're from, what you do
**Vocabulary: Nationalities and countries, professions, family members**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Run the paradigm** — all six forms of *ser* out loud, until they bounce off your teeth
3. **Matrix** — question → answer across all persons. The answer is already inside the question

> This is the first real verb in the course. *<say>Ser</say>* is the foundation. It covers 30% of everything you'll say in Spanish in the first year. Drill it to reflex level **now**.

---

## Part 1: Subject pronouns — 12 forms (but you'll use half)

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | **<say>yo</say>** — I | **<say>nosotros</say> / <say>nosotras</say>** — we (m. / f.) |
| 2nd | **<say>tú</say>** — you (informal) | **<say>vosotros</say> / <say>vosotras</say>** — you all (m. / f., Spain only) |
| 3rd | **<say>él</say> / <say>ella</say>** — he / she | **<say>ellos</say> / <say>ellas</say>** — they (m. / f.) |
| 2nd formal | **<say>usted</say>** — you (formal) | **<say>ustedes</say>** — you all (formal in Spain; formal + informal in LatAm) |

> **Trap #1 — usted is grammatically 3rd person.** It means "you" (polite), but the verb conjugates as if you said "he/she". *<say>Usted es</say>* (✓), not *<say>usted eres</say>* (✗). It comes from old *<say>vuestra merced</say>* ("your grace") — a 3rd-person phrase used to address one person politely. The grammar froze that way.
> **Trap #2 — vosotros is Spain-only.** In Latin America (450+ million speakers) *<say>vosotros</say>* is **not used**: *<say>ustedes</say>* covers both formal and informal plural "you". In Spain *<say>vosotros</say>* is the everyday "you guys".
> **Trap #3 — Spanish drops subject pronouns.** The verb ending already tells you who. *<say>Soy estudiante</say>* = "I am a student" — no "I" needed. *<say>Eres alto</say>* = "you are tall" — no "you" needed. Adding *<say>yo</say> / <say>tú</say> / <say>él</say>* puts the pronoun **in spotlight**: contrast or emphasis.

### When the pronoun IS needed

- **Contrast:** *<say>Yo soy de Madrid, ella es de Barcelona.</say>* — I'm from Madrid, **she's** from Barcelona.
- **Emphasis:** *<say>¡Tú eres el jefe!</say>* — **You're** the boss!
- **After a preposition:** *<say>para mí</say>, <say>contigo</say>* (more on this later)
- **<say>Usted</say> / <say>ustedes</say>** — often kept for polite clarity, since *<say>es</say>* alone could mean he/she/you

> **The tú vs usted thing** — like old English *thou* vs *you*. *Thou* was the intimate one, *you* the polite one. English ended up dropping *thou* and keeping *you* for everyone. Spanish kept the distinction alive: *<say>tú</say>* for friends, family, kids, peers; *<say>usted</say>* for strangers, elders, your boss, anyone you'd address as Mr./Mrs. When in doubt with an adult, start with *<say>usted</say>*. They'll switch you to *<say>tú</say>* if appropriate.

## Part 2: The verb ser — the full paradigm

| Person | Form | Meaning |
|--------|------|---------|
| <say>yo</say> | **<say>soy</say>** | I am |
| <say>tú</say> | **<say>eres</say>** | you are |
| <say>él</say> / <say>ella</say> / <say>usted</say> | **<say>es</say>** | he / she is, you (formal) are |
| <say>nosotros</say> / <say>nosotras</say> | **<say>somos</say>** | we are |
| <say>vosotros</say> / <say>vosotras</say> | **<say>sois</say>** | you all are (Spain) |
| <say>ellos</say> / <say>ellas</say> / <say>ustedes</say> | **<say>son</say>** | they are, you all are |

> **Memorize it as a rhythm:** <say>soy</say> — <say>eres</say> — <say>es</say> — <say>somos</say> — <say>sois</say> — <say>son</say>. Say it ten times. Then ten more. This is the most frequent verb in Spanish — it has to live in your spinal cord, not in your head.

**Pronunciation:** *<say>soy</say>* "soy", *<say>eres</say>* "EH-res", *<say>es</say>* "ES", *<say>somos</say>* "SOH-mos", *<say>sois</say>* "soys", *<say>son</say>* "SOHN".

Notice — *<say>ser</say>* is wildly irregular. So is English "be" (I am, you are, he is, we were, been). The most-used verbs in any language refuse to be regular. Just memorize the six forms.

## Part 3: The five main uses of ser

> **Mnemonic — DOCTOR:** **D**escription, **O**ccupation, **C**haracteristic, **T**ime, **O**rigin, **R**elationship. Everything that describes "the essence" or "the permanent" → *<say>ser</say>*.

| Use | Example | Translation |
|-----|---------|-------------|
| **Identity** | *<say>Soy Marco.</say>* | I'm Marco. |
| **Origin** (with *<say>de</say>*) | *<say>Soy de Rusia.</say>* | I'm from Russia. |
| **Nationality** | *<say>Es española.</say>* | She's Spanish. |
| **Profession** (NO article!) | *<say>Es médico.</say>* | He's a doctor. |
| **Ownership** (with *<say>de</say>*) | *<say>El libro es de Ana.</say>* | The book is Ana's. |
| **Character / permanent trait** | *<say>Eres inteligente.</say>* | You're intelligent. |
| **Time / date** | *<say>Son las tres.</say>* | It's three o'clock. |
| **Material** (with *<say>de</say>*) | *<say>La mesa es de madera.</say>* | The table is (made of) wood. |

> **Trap #4 — profession takes NO article.** *<say>Soy profesor</say>* (✓), not *<say>soy un profesor</say>* (✗). English forces "a/an" (*I'm **a** teacher*), Spanish doesn't. The article returns only if there's an adjective: *<say>Soy un profesor excelente</say>* (✓) — "I'm an excellent teacher".
> **Trap #5 — don't mix up ser and estar.** *<say>Estar</say>* is for location and temporary states. That's Lesson 5. For now: anything that's **"who/what you are by your nature"** → *<say>ser</say>*. *<say>Soy alta</say>* = "I'm tall" (always). *<say>Estoy cansada</say>* = "I'm tired" (right now).

## Part 4: Origin and ownership — the *de* construction

**Formula:** `<say>ser</say> + <say>de</say> + (country / city / person / material)`

| Spanish | English |
|---------|---------|
| *<say>¿De dónde eres?</say>* | Where are you from? |
| *<say>Soy de Moscú.</say>* | I'm from Moscow. |
| *<say>Somos de España.</say>* | We're from Spain. |
| *<say>¿De quién es el coche?</say>* | Whose car is it? |
| *<say>Es de María.</say>* | It's María's. |
| *<say>Es de mi padre.</say>* | It's my father's. |

> **English uses "'s" for ownership (*Maria's car*). Spanish has no apostrophe-s.** Instead — flip the order: "the car of Maria" → *<say>el coche de María</say>*. Always *<say>de</say> + owner*.

### The de + el → del contraction

> **Recap from Lesson 3 (R1):** *<say>de</say> + <say>el</say> = <say>del</say>*. *<say>a</say> + <say>el</say> = <say>al</say>*. With *<say>la</say> / <say>los</say> / <say>las</say>* — no contraction.

- *<say>de</say> + <say>el profesor</say>* → **<say>del profesor</say>** — *<say>El libro es del profesor.</say>*
- *<say>de</say> + <say>el padre de Juan</say>* → **<say>del padre de Juan</say>** — *<say>Soy amigo del hermano de Ana.</say>*
- *<say>de</say> + <say>la profesora</say>* → *<say>de la profesora</say>* (unchanged)
- *<say>de</say> + <say>los chicos</say>* → *<say>de los chicos</say>* (unchanged)

## Part 5: Nationalities — they agree like adjectives

> **Recap from Lesson 2 (R2):** noun gender and number. Same rule applies to nationalities — they're **adjectives** (lowercase!) and they agree in gender and number with the person.

Pattern:

| Form | Example (Spain) | Example (Russia) | Example (France) |
|------|----------------|------------------|------------------|
| m. sg. | <say>español</say> | <say>ruso</say> | <say>francés</say> |
| f. sg. | <say>española</say> | <say>rusa</say> | <say>francesa</say> |
| m. pl. | <say>españoles</say> | <say>rusos</say> | <say>franceses</say> |
| f. pl. | <say>españolas</say> | <say>rusas</say> | <say>francesas</say> |

> **Notice:** *<say>español</say>*, *<say>francés</say>*, *<say>inglés</say>*, *<say>alemán</say>*, *<say>japonés</say>* — the masculine ends in a **consonant**, the feminine adds **-a** (and the tilde often disappears: *<say>francés</say> → <say>francesa</say>*. The stress stays put; the word now ends in a vowel, so it's a regular *llana* — no tilde needed).
> **Special case:** *<say>estadounidense</say>*, *<say>canadiense</say>*, *<say>belga</say>* — one form for both genders. Common with adjectives ending in *-e* or *-ista*.
> **Spelling rule:** countries → capitalized. Nationalities → lowercase. *<say>Soy de España. Soy español.</say>* English capitalizes both. Spanish only capitalizes the country.

### Family members

| Spanish | English | Spanish | English |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| <say>el padre</say> / <say>la madre</say> | father / mother | <say>el abuelo</say> / <say>la abuela</say> | grandfather / grandmother |

> **The masculine plural covers mixed groups:** *<say>los padres</say>* = "parents" (mom + dad); *<say>los hermanos</say>* = "siblings"; *<say>los hijos</say>* = "children"; *<say>los abuelos</say>* = "grandparents". One man in a group of nine hundred women, and the noun still goes masculine plural. That's how the language works.

## Language matrix: question → answer

> **Mirror principle:** the answer is already encoded in the question. Just change the pronoun and the verb ending.

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| *<say>¿Quién eres tú?</say>* | *<say>Yo soy Marco.</say>* |
| *<say>¿Quién es usted?</say>* | *<say>Yo soy el señor Pérez.</say>* |
| *<say>¿Quiénes son ellos?</say>* (answer: Ana's friends) | *<say>Son los amigos de Ana.</say>* |
| *<say>¿De dónde eres?</say>* | *<say>Soy de Rusia.</say>* |
| *<say>¿De dónde es ella?</say>* (answer: from Madrid) | *<say>Es de España, de Madrid.</say>* |
| *<say>¿De dónde son ustedes?</say>* | *<say>Somos de los Estados Unidos.</say>* |
| *<say>¿A qué te dedicas?</say>* | *<say>Soy ingeniero.</say>* |
| *<say>¿Qué es tu padre?</say>* (answer: a doctor) | *<say>Mi padre es médico.</say>* |
| *<say>¿Qué son ellos?</say>* (answer: journalists) | *<say>Son periodistas.</say>* |
| *<say>¿De quién es el libro?</say>* (answer: María's) | *<say>Es de María.</say>* |
| *<say>¿De quién es la casa?</say>* (answer: the teacher's) | *<say>Es del profesor.</say>* |
| *<say>¿De quién es esta foto?</say>* (answer: grandma's) | *<say>Es de la abuela.</say>* |

### Exercise 3. Article or no article? + the *del* contraction

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

2. *una* (adjective *excelente* → article returns)

4. *del*
5. *del*
6. *de la* (no contraction — *la*, not *el*)

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
PRONOUNS + SER

         Singular              Plural
1st   yo            soy    nosotros/as   somos
2nd   tú            eres   vosotros/as   sois
3rd   él/ella       es     ellos/ellas   son
2-form usted        es     ustedes       son   ← takes 3rd-person verb!

RHYTHM:  soy — eres — es — somos — sois — son

WHEN SER (DOCTOR):
  Description    — character, permanent quality
  Occupation     — profession (NO article!)
  Characteristic — nationality, color, size
  Time           — hour, date
  Origin         — where from (with de)
  Relationship   — who is who to whom

SPANISH DROPS SUBJECT PRONOUNS:
  Soy estudiante.   = I'm a student.   (no "yo" needed)
  Eres alto.        = You're tall.     (no "tú" needed)
  Use yo/tú/él only for contrast or emphasis.

TÚ vs USTED:
  tú     — informal (friends, family, peers; like old English "thou")
  usted  — formal (strangers, elders, business; takes 3rd-person verb)
  vosotros — only in Spain. LatAm uses ustedes for both formal + informal plural.

ORIGIN AND OWNERSHIP:
  ser + de + country/city          Soy de Rusia.
  ser + de + person                El libro es de Ana.
  ¿De dónde eres? — Soy de Moscú.
  ¿De quién es?   — Es de mi padre.

CONTRACTION:  de + el = del   (de la, de los, de las — unchanged)
              a + el  = al

NATIONALITY = ADJECTIVE (agrees in gender + number):
  m.    español   ruso     francés    alemán
  f.    española  rusa     francesa   alemana
  pl.   españoles rusos    franceses  alemanes

  Countries  → capitalized.   Nationalities → lowercase.

TRAPS:
  ✗ Soy un profesor.     ✓ Soy profesor.
  ✗ Usted eres...        ✓ Usted es...
  ✗ vosotros (LatAm)     ✓ ustedes (covers both "you" plurals)
  ✗ es de el padre       ✓ es del padre

FAMILY — masculine plural covers mixed groups:
  los padres   = parents (mom + dad)
  los hermanos = siblings
  los hijos    = children (sons + daughters)
  los abuelos  = grandparents

KEY QUESTIONS:
  ¿Quién eres? / ¿Cómo te llamas?  — who are you
  ¿De dónde eres?                  — where from
  ¿A qué te dedicas? / ¿Qué eres?  — what do you do
  ¿De quién es?                    — whose is it
```
