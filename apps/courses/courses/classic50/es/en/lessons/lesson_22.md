# Lesson 22: Pretérito indefinido — irregular verbs
**Vocabulary: irregular preterites, grouped by stem pattern (~25 verbs)**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — get the three big stem families (u-stem, i-stem, j-stem) and the four "wild" verbs (ser/ir, dar, ver). 5 minutes, no more.
2. **Run the tables out loud** — every form, with your mouth, not your eyes.
3. **Scale drill** — one verb, one pass: yo → tú → él → nosotros → vosotros → ellos. No pauses.
4. **Matrix Q→A** — answer "yesterday" questions to a partner (or to yourself).

> Knowing that the past of *tener* is *tuve* — that's 10%. Saying *tuve, tuviste, tuvo, tuvimos, tuvisteis, tuvieron* in 5 seconds — that's 90%. The good news: about 14 of the hardest verbs in Spanish all follow **one single set of endings**. Learn the endings once and you get the whole bundle for free.

---

## Part 1: The big idea — one set of endings for almost everyone

In Lesson 21 you got the regular preterite endings. The irregular preterites use a **different** set — but the same set is shared across nearly all irregular verbs.

| Person | Ending |
|--------|--------|
| yo | **-e** |
| tú | **-iste** |
| él / ella / usted | **-o** |
| nosotros | **-imos** |
| vosotros | **-isteis** |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes | **-ieron** (or **-eron** after a j-stem) |

> **The single biggest trap:** in 1st and 3rd person singular (yo, él), **NO tilde**!
> *tuve*, *tuvo* — never *tuvé*, never *tuvó*.
> *hice*, *hizo* — never *hicé*, never *hizó*.
> This is exactly what separates irregular preterites from the regular ones you saw in Lesson 21 (where *hablé* and *habló* DO carry tildes). The stress in irregular preterites falls on the stem, not the ending — so no tilde is needed.

If you learn the **stem** of each irregular verb, the rest of the paradigm comes free — the endings are always the same.

---

## Part 2: U-stems — the biggest family

Seven verbs replace their regular vowel with a **u**.

| Infinitive | Stem | English |
|------------|------|---------|
| tener | **tuv-** | to have |
| estar | **estuv-** | to be (location/state) |
| poder | **pud-** | to be able to |
| poner | **pus-** | to put |
| saber | **sup-** | to know (a fact) |
| haber | **hub-** | to have (aux.) / there was |
| andar | **anduv-** | to walk |

### Full paradigm (example: *tener*)

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| yo | **tuve** |
| tú | **tuviste** |
| él / ella / Ud. | **tuvo** |
| nosotros | **tuvimos** |
| vosotros | **tuvisteis** |
| ellos / ellas / Uds. | **tuvieron** |

Same machine for the other six: *anduve … anduvieron*, *estuve … estuvieron*, *pude … pudieron*, *puse … pusieron*, *supe … supieron*, *hube … hubieron*.

> **Meaning shifts to learn cold:**
> - *supe* (from *saber*) = **"I found out"** (the moment information arrived), not "I knew". *Lo supe ayer.* — "I found out yesterday."
> - *pude* (from *poder*) = **"I managed / I was able to"**. *No pude* = **"I couldn't / didn't manage to"**.
> - *tuve que + infinitive* = **"I had to"** (and I did it). *Tuve que ir al médico.* — "I had to go to the doctor."
> - *hubo* = **"there was / there were"** (one-time event in the past). *Hubo un accidente.* — "There was an accident."

## Part 3: I-stems — three verbs

The stem-vowel changes to **i**.

| Infinitive | Stem | English |
|------------|------|---------|
| querer | **quis-** | to want, to love |
| hacer | **hic- / hiz-** | to do, to make |
| venir | **vin-** | to come |

### Full paradigm (example: *hacer*)

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| yo | **hice** |
| tú | **hiciste** |
| él / ella / Ud. | **hizo** |
| nosotros | **hicimos** |
| vosotros | **hicisteis** |
| ellos / ellas / Uds. | **hicieron** |

> **Spelling trap:** in the 3rd person singular, it's **hizo** (with **z**, not c!). Why? Spanish spelling rules say c+o would be pronounced "ko". To keep the soft "s/th" sound before o, you must switch to z. Same logic as *empecé* (Lesson 21).

*Querer*: quise, quisiste, quiso, quisimos, quisisteis, quisieron.
*Venir*: vine, viniste, vino, vinimos, vinisteis, vinieron.

> **Meaning shifts:**
> - *quise + infinitive* = **"I tried to"**: *Quise abrir la puerta.* — "I tried to open the door (and may have failed)."
> - *no quise + infinitive* = **"I refused to"**: *No quise ir.* — "I refused to go."
> - *hice* + general past activity = "I did, I made". *Ayer hice la cena.* — "I made dinner yesterday."

---

## Part 4: J-stems — the "dij-" family

A few verbs have a stem ending in **j**. The unique feature: in **3rd person plural** the ending is not *-ieron* but **-eron** — the j swallows the i.

In our list this matters for one verb: **decir**. (Verbs in -ucir like *traducir, conducir, producir* and the verb *traer* also belong to this family — they all drop the i in *ellos*.)

| Infinitive | Stem | English |
|------------|------|---------|
| decir | **dij-** | to say, to tell |

### Full paradigm (example: *decir*)

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| yo | **dije** |
| tú | **dijiste** |
| él / ella / Ud. | **dijo** |
| nosotros | **dijimos** |
| vosotros | **dijisteis** |
| ellos / ellas / Uds. | **dijeron** ← no *i*! |

> **Trap:** **not** *dijieron*. The "ieron" rule disappears after a j-stem.

## Part 5: The four "wild" verbs — ser, ir, dar, ver

These four are short, super-frequent, and have to be memorized one paradigm at a time. The good news: they're tiny, you'll hear them constantly, and they cement themselves fast.

### Ser and Ir — identical forms!

Yes, "to be" and "to go" share **the exact same preterite paradigm**. Context tells you which one is meant: *Fui a Madrid* = "I went to Madrid"; *Fui profesor* = "I was a teacher". A Spanish speaker decodes this in 0.2 seconds — and so will you.

| Person | ser / ir |
|--------|----------|
| yo | **fui** |
| tú | **fuiste** |
| él / ella / Ud. | **fue** |
| nosotros | **fuimos** |
| vosotros | **fuisteis** |
| ellos / ellas / Uds. | **fueron** |

> **No tildes!** *fui, fue* — short one-syllable words. The stress is automatic, so no written accent. (Until 1999 students were taught to write *fuí, fué* with accents — that rule was abolished. Modern Spanish: never tilde them.)

### Dar — takes -ER/-IR endings

Yes, the infinitive is -AR, but the preterite of *dar* borrows the -ER/-IR endings — and drops all tildes (because the forms are one syllable each, or stressed correctly without them).

| Person | dar |
|--------|-----|
| yo | **di** |
| tú | **diste** |
| él / ella / Ud. | **dio** |
| nosotros | **dimos** |
| vosotros | **disteis** |
| ellos / ellas / Uds. | **dieron** |

### Ver — looks almost regular, but tildeless

| Person | ver |
|--------|-----|
| yo | **vi** |
| tú | **viste** |
| él / ella / Ud. | **vio** |
| nosotros | **vimos** |
| vosotros | **visteis** |
| ellos / ellas / Uds. | **vieron** |

> **Why no tildes on *vi, vio, di, dio, fui, fue*?** They're all **one-syllable words** — *vio* and *dio* read as a single syllable ("byoh", "dyoh"). Spanish orthography forbids tildes on monosyllables (except a few homographs like *sí/si*, *él/el*). In Lesson 21 the regular preterite gave you *habló, comió* with tildes, but those are two-syllable forms.

## Part 6: Vocabulary — the irregular preterites in one place

| Spanish (inf. / 1sg / 3sg) | English |

| tener / tuve / tuvo | to have |
| estar / estuve / estuvo | to be (loc./state) |
| andar / anduve / anduvo | to walk |
| poder / pude / pudo | to be able to |
| poner / puse / puso | to put |
| saber / supe / supo | to find out / know |
| haber / hube / hubo | there was / aux. |
| hacer / hice / **hizo** | to do, to make |
| querer / quise / quiso | to want, to try |
| venir / vine / vino | to come |
| decir / dije / dijo | to say, to tell |
| ser / fui / fue | to be |
| ir / fui / fue | to go |
| dar / di / dio | to give |
| ver / vi / vio | to see |

### Time markers (recap from Lesson 21)

*ayer* (yesterday), *anoche* (last night), *anteayer* (day before yesterday), *la semana pasada / el mes pasado / el año pasado* (last week/month/year), *hace dos años* (two years ago).

---

> English irregular pasts are a chaotic museum: *go → went*, *see → saw*, *put → put* — each one memorized one-off. Spanish gives you a much better deal: **one set of endings** covers ~14 verbs, the stems are grouped by family, and the "wild" group is just three paradigms. The price: **no tildes on yo and él**. That's the single most common learner mistake here — drill it into your fingers.

### Exercise 3. Find and fix the mistake

Each sentence has exactly one error in an irregular preterite form. Identify it and correct it.

1. Ayer yo *tené* mucho trabajo.
2. Mi amigo *hació* la tarea.
3. Ellos *dijieron* la verdad.
4. Yo *fuí* al cine ayer.
5. Ella *vió* la película anoche.
6. Yo *podé* hacerlo sin ayuda.
7. Ana *dió* el regalo a su hermano.
8. ¿Qué *hací* tú ayer?
9. Yo *sabí* la verdad anoche.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *tuve* (u-stem, no tilde — *tener* is irregular)
2. *hizo* (i-stem, with **z** in 3sg, no tilde)
3. *dijeron* (j-stem drops the *i* in 3pl)
4. *fui* (monosyllable — no tilde, modern spelling)
5. *vio* (monosyllable, no tilde)
6. *pude* (u-stem — irregular, no tilde)
7. *dio* (monosyllable, no tilde)
8. *hiciste* (the tú-form is irregular: *hiciste*, not *hací*)
9. *supe* (u-stem irregular — *saber* in the preterite means "I found out")

</details>

### Exercise 4. Translate from English to Spanish

1. I had a lot of work yesterday.
2. What did you do last weekend?
3. They came at eight and brought a gift.
4. I put the keys on the table.
5. María went to Madrid and saw the museum.
6. I couldn't come — I had to work.
7. The children wanted to play more.
8. Who told you that?
9. We were at the beach last week.
10. I found out the news this morning.
11. There was a party at Pedro's house.
12. He gave me a beautiful book.
13. They didn't want to come (= they refused).
14. We walked through the old city.

<summary>Key</summary>

1. *Ayer tuve mucho trabajo.*
2. *¿Qué hiciste el fin de semana pasado?*

4. *Puse las llaves en la mesa.*
5. *María fue a Madrid y vio el museo.* (both verbs irregular, no tildes)

12. *Me dio un libro precioso.* (*dio* — no tilde!)

### Exercise 5. Dialogue — read both voices out loud, then reconstruct from memory

> — ¿Qué tal el fin de semana?
> — Muy bien, gracias. El sábado fui a la montaña con Pedro.
> — ¿Y qué hicisteis allí?
> — Anduvimos mucho, vimos un lago precioso y comimos en un pueblo pequeño.
> — ¿A qué hora volvisteis?
> — Volvimos tarde. Llegué a casa a las once y me dormí enseguida.
> — ¿Pudiste descansar el domingo?
> — Sí, dormí hasta las diez. Después hice la compra y leí un libro.
> — ¿Y no tuviste tiempo de salir?
> — No quise. Estuve muy cansado.
> — ¡Te entiendo! Fue un fin de semana perfecto.

Count the irregular preterites in that short exchange: *fui, hicisteis, anduvimos, vimos, volvisteis, pude, tuviste, quise, estuve, fue*. Ten forms from this lesson — that's the real density you'll hear in any past-tense conversation. Repeat the dialogue 3 times, then cover the text and reconstruct it from memory.

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
PRETÉRITO INDEFINIDO — IRREGULAR VERBS

SHARED ENDINGS (almost all irregulars):
  yo -e   tú -iste   él -o   nos -imos   vos -isteis   ellos -ieron
  ▶ NO TILDES on yo and él!  tuve, tuvo (NOT tuvé, tuvó)

U-STEMS (insert u):
  tener tuv- | estar estuv- | poder pud- | poner pus-
  saber sup- | haber hub-  | andar anduv-

I-STEMS:
  querer quis- | hacer hic- (3sg = HIZO, z before o!) | venir vin-

J-STEMS (3pl drops i):
  decir dij- → dijeron  (NOT dijieron)
  (also traer → trajeron, conducir → condujeron)

THE "WILD" FOUR (monosyllables — NO tildes):
  ser/ir : fui, fuiste, fue, fuimos, fuisteis, fueron
  dar    : di, diste, dio, dimos, disteis, dieron
  ver    : vi, viste, vio, vimos, visteis, vieron

MEANING SHIFTS:
  supe = "found out"   pude = "managed"   no pude = "couldn't"
  quise = "tried to"   no quise = "refused"   tuve que = "had to"
  hubo = "there was/were"
```
