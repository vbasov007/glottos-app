# Lesson 2: Spanish nouns — gender and number
**Vocabulary: Everyday objects, home, food, animals**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — understand the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Say it out loud** — every noun **with its article**: not *<say>casa</say>*, but *<say>la casa</say>*. The article and the word are learned together as one chunk.
3. **Speed up** — drill singular → plural pairs until they pour out on their own.

> Gender is not something you "figure out" — it's something you **glue to the word** along with the article. *<say>la mano</say>*, not *<say>mano</say> + pause to think about which gender*.
> The good news: 80% of nouns obey one simple ending rule. The other 20% are a short list that you can absorb in one sitting.

---

## Part 1: Why gender at all?

English has no grammatical gender. *The table*, *the book*, *the problem*, *the hand* — same article, no extra information attached.

Spanish is different. **Every noun has a gender** — masculine or feminine. There is no neuter. And it isn't about biology: *<say>la mesa</say>* (the table) is feminine, *<say>el libro</say>* (the book) is masculine. Tables aren't female and books aren't male — gender is just a grammatical label, two boxes Spanish has been sorting its nouns into for the last thousand years.

Gender controls three things downstream:

- **The article:** *<say>el</say>* / *<say>la</say>*, *<say>un</say>* / *<say>una</say>*
- **Adjectives that agree:** *<say>libro rojo</say>* vs *<say>casa roja</say>*
- **Pronouns that replace the noun:** *<say>lo veo</say>* (I see it — masc.) / *<say>la veo</say>* (I see it — fem.)

> Get gender wrong and the whole sentence collapses around it. So we always learn a noun **with its article**: *<say>el libro</say>*, *<say>la mesa</say>*. Not the bare word — the block "article + word".

---

## Part 2: The main rule — gender by ending

### 80% of nouns follow a simple ending rule

| Ending | Gender | Examples |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **-o** | masculine | <say>el libro</say>, <say>el gato</say>, <say>el vino</say> |
| **-a** | feminine | <say>la casa</say>, <say>la mesa</say>, <say>la chica</say> |
| **-ción / -sión** | feminine | <say>la canción</say>, <say>la televisión</say> |
| **-dad / -tad** | feminine | <say>la ciudad</say>, <say>la libertad</say> |
| **-umbre** | feminine | <say>la costumbre</say>, <say>la lumbre</say> |
| **-or** (usually) | masculine | <say>el amor</say>, <say>el color</say>, <say>el dolor</say> |
| **-aje** | masculine | <say>el viaje</say>, <say>el equipaje</say> |
| **-ma / -pa / -ta** (from Greek) | masculine | <say>el problema</say>, <say>el mapa</say>, <say>el planeta</say> |

> **Memorize by group, not by individual word.** *<say>La canción</say>*, *<say>la lección</say>*, *<say>la nación</say>*, *<say>la información</say>* — all hang on the same -ción hook. Learn the hook once; dozens of nouns come along free.

If a noun ends in **-e** or a consonant outside the patterns above, gender isn't predictable — just learn it with the article: *<say>el coche</say>* (masc.), *<say>la leche</say>* (fem.), *<say>el pan</say>* (masc.), *<say>la sal</say>* (fem.).

---

## Part 3: The exceptions — the must-memorize list

Two short lists. The words on them are very common, so they need to be in your bones.

### Words ending in -a that are masculine (Greek origin)

These came into Spanish from Greek via Latin. The -ma ending in particular often signals masculine.

| Word | English |
|------|---------|
| <say>**el** problema</say> | problem |
| <say>**el** tema</say> | topic, theme |
| <say>**el** sistema</say> | system |
| <say>**el** programa</say> | program |
| <say>**el** clima</say> | climate |
| <say>**el** idioma</say> | language |
| <say>**el** día</say> | day |
| <say>**el** mapa</say> | map |
| <say>**el** planeta</say> | planet |
| <say>**el** sofá</say> | sofa |

### Words ending in -o that are feminine

| Word | English | Note |
|------|---------|------|
| <say>**la** mano</say> | hand | the classic exception |
| <say>**la** foto</say> | photo | short for *<say>fotografía</say>* (feminine) |
| <say>**la** moto</say> | motorbike | short for *<say>motocicleta</say>* (feminine) |
| <say>**la** radio</say> | radio | in Latin America often *<say>el radio</say>* |

> **Why these matter:** *<say>el problema</say>*, *<say>la mano</say>*, *<say>el día</say>*, *<say>la foto</say>* aren't obscure words — you'll use them in your first week of speaking Spanish. So the "exceptions" are unavoidable. Get them in your bones now and you'll never hesitate later.

### Bonus: words that change meaning with gender

| Masculine | Feminine |
|-----------|----------|
| <say>**el** capital</say> — capital (money) | <say>**la** capital</say> — capital city |
| <say>**el** cura</say> — priest | <say>**la** cura</say> — cure |
| <say>**el** orden</say> — order (arrangement) | <say>**la** orden</say> — order (command) |
| <say>**el** policía</say> — policeman | <say>**la** policía</say> — police force / policewoman |

## Part 4: People and animals — male/female pairs

When a noun refers to a person or an animal that has a sex, Spanish usually gives you two forms.

### Swap -o for -a

| Masculine | Feminine |
|-----------|----------|
| <say>el chico</say> | <say>la chica</say> |
| <say>el gato</say> | <say>la gata</say> |
| <say>el perro</say> | <say>la perra</say> |
| <say>el hijo</say> | <say>la hija</say> |
| <say>el amigo</say> | <say>la amiga</say> |

### Add -a to a consonant ending

| Masculine | Feminine |
|-----------|----------|
| <say>el profesor</say> | <say>la profesora</say> |
| <say>el doctor</say> | <say>la doctora</say> |
| <say>el español</say> | <say>la española</say> |

### One form, gender shown by the article

Many nouns ending in -e or -ista take the same form for both — the article alone tells you which.

| Word | Use |
|------|-----|
| <say>el estudiante</say> / <say>la estudiante</say> | male / female student |
| <say>el artista</say> / <say>la artista</say> | male / female artist |
| <say>el dentista</say> / <say>la dentista</say> | male / female dentist |
| <say>el joven</say> / <say>la joven</say> | young man / young woman |

## Part 5: Plurals — three rules

### Rule 1: word ends in a vowel → add **-s**

| Singular | Plural |
|----------|--------|
| <say>el libro</say> | <say>los libros</say> |
| <say>la casa</say> | <say>las casas</say> |
| <say>el día</say> | <say>los días</say> |
| <say>la mano</say> | <say>las manos</say> |

### Rule 2: word ends in a consonant → add **-es**

| Singular | Plural |
|----------|--------|
| <say>el profesor</say> | <say>los profesores</say> |
| <say>la ciudad</say> | <say>las ciudades</say> |
| <say>el reloj</say> | <say>los relojes</say> |
| <say>la canción</say> | <say>las canciones</say> (the tilde disappears!) |

> **Watch the tilde:** *<say>canción</say> → <say>canciones</say>*. In the singular the tilde is needed because the word is *aguda* and ends in -n. In the plural the word becomes *llana* — stress sits naturally on the second-to-last syllable, so the tilde drops. The pronunciation doesn't change; the spelling rule just kicks in.

### Rule 3: word ends in -z → -**ces**

The -z flips to -c before the -es ending. Pure spelling convention.

| Singular | Plural |
|----------|--------|
| <say>el lápiz</say> | <say>los lápices</say> |
| <say>la luz</say> | <say>las luces</say> |
| <say>el pez</say> | <say>los peces</say> |
| <say>la vez</say> | <say>las veces</say> |

### Special cases

| Singular | Plural | Note |
|----------|--------|------|
| <say>el lunes</say> | <say>los lunes</say> | unchanged (ends in -s, stress not final) |
| <say>la crisis</say> | <say>las crisis</say> | unchanged |
| <say>el café</say> | <say>los cafés</say> | stressed vowel + tilde → +s |
| <say>el rubí</say> | <say>los rubíes</say> | stressed -í → +es |

> **Trap:** gender **never** changes in the plural. *<say>La mano</say> → <say>las manos</say>* — still feminine. *<say>El día</say> → <say>los días</say>* — still masculine. The plural article tracks gender too: **<say>los</say>** (masc.) / **<say>las</say>** (fem.).

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
ALWAYS LEARN THE NOUN WITH ITS ARTICLE:
  Not "casa" — "la casa". The article glues to the word for life.

ENDING RULE (80% of nouns):
  -o            → masc.   el libro, el gato
  -a            → fem.    la casa, la mesa
  -ción/-sión   → fem.    la canción
  -dad/-tad     → fem.    la ciudad
  -or           → masc.   el color
  -aje          → masc.   el viaje

EXCEPTIONS — MEMORIZE COLD:
  -a but masculine (Greek):
    el problema, el tema, el sistema, el programa, el clima,
    el idioma, el día, el mapa, el planeta, el sofá
  -o but feminine:
    la mano, la foto, la moto, la radio

PLURAL FORMATION:
  vowel     → +s     libro → libros, mano → manos
  consonant → +es    profesor → profesores, ciudad → ciudades
  -z        → -ces   lápiz → lápices, luz → luces
  unstressed -s → no change   el lunes → los lunes

TILDE IN THE PLURAL:
  canción → canciones (tilde drops)
  sofá    → sofás     (tilde stays)

THREE TRAPS:
  1. el problema — NOT la problema.
  2. la mano     — NOT el mano.
  3. canción → canciones — the tilde vanishes.
```
