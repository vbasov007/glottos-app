# Lesson 10: Existence (יש / אין). Possession (yesh le-). Question words. Negation lo. Connectors
**Vocabulary: yesh / ein, the inflected forms yesh li / yesh lekha…, the set of question words, connectors ve- / aval / o / ki**

---

## How to work with this lesson

1. **Read** — get the rule (5 minutes, no more!)
2. **Drill the yesh le- paradigm** — ten forms (li, lekha, lakh, lo, la, lanu, lakhem, lakhen, lahem, lahen). This is the main ping-pong of the lesson.
3. **Question words — learn as a closed list.** Only seven, one card-page. They'll pay off by L11.
4. **Question → answer matrix** — answer every prompt both in the affirmative (yesh) and the negative (ein).

> The single most important thing to grasp: Hebrew handles both "there is / isn't" (existence) and "have / don't have" (possession) **with the same pair of words** — yesh / ein. Hebrew has no verb "to have" at all. "I have…" is literally "there is to me…" — *yesh li*.

---

## Part 1: yesh and ein — existential "there is" and "there isn't"

English has "there is" / "there are" for existence: *There's a table here.* *There's a book on the table.* Hebrew has exactly such a word — **<say>יֵשׁ</say>** (yesh).

And exactly the negative partner — **<say>אֵין</say>** (ein), "there isn't, there's no": *There's no table here.* *There's no book on the table.*

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>יֵשׁ</say> | yesh | there is, there are, exists |
| <say>אֵין</say> | ein | there isn't, there are no, doesn't exist |

> **Almost a 1-to-1 match with English "there is/there isn't".** A rare gift — Hebrew existential sentences are built nearly the way English builds them. Don't try to "improve" — write it the English way and you'll land it.

### Impersonal sentences with yesh / ein

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>יֵשׁ סֵפֶר עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן</say> | yesh sefer al ha-shulchan | There's a book on the table |
| <say>אֵין סֵפֶר עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן</say> | ein sefer al ha-shulchan | There's no book on the table |
| <say>יֵשׁ אֹכֶל בַּמְקָרֵר</say> | yesh okhel ba-mekarer | There's food in the fridge |
| <say>אֵין אֹכֶל בַּמְקָרֵר</say> | ein okhel ba-mekarer | There's no food in the fridge |
| <say>יֵשׁ זְמַן</say> | yesh zman | There's time |
| <say>אֵין זְמַן</say> | ein zman | There's no time |
| <say>יֵשׁ בְּעָיָה</say> | yesh be'aya | There's a problem |
| <say>אֵין בְּעָיָה</say> | ein be'aya | No problem (the classic "no problem!") |

> **Notice:** there is **no copula verb** — neither "there is" nor "is not" is a verb in Hebrew, just a particle. So it doesn't conjugate for person or for tense (in the present).

> **Trap:** don't confuse yesh ("there is" — existence) with **hu / hi / hem / hen** ("he is = he is identified as" — the pronominal copula from L5). *Ha-sefer hu adom* — "the book — it's red" (copula). *Yesh sefer adom* — "there's a red book" (existence).

## Part 2: Possession through yesh le- — literally "there is to (someone)"

Hebrew has **no verb "to have"**. None. At all.

To say "I have a book", Hebrew literally says: "there is to me a book", **yesh li sefer**. The construction is **yesh** + **le-** ("to, at") with a pronoun suffix.

> **Structurally — almost like English "there is X to me".** Except English usually picks "have"; Hebrew sticks with "there is + to whom + what". The principle — "there is + to someone + something" — is the same.

### The yesh li / yesh lekha paradigm — all ten forms

This is the most important table of the lesson. Ten forms. Run it through your mouth until it sounds like one word.

| Hebrew | Translit | English | To whom |
|--------|----------|---------|---------|
| <say>יֵשׁ לִי</say> | yesh li | I have | ani (I) |
| <say>יֵשׁ לְךָ</say> | yesh lekha | you have | ata (you, m.) |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָךְ</say> | yesh lakh | you have | at (you, f.) |
| <say>יֵשׁ לוֹ</say> | yesh lo | he has | hu |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָהּ</say> | yesh la | she has | hi |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָנוּ</say> | yesh lanu | we have | anachnu |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָכֶם</say> | yesh lakhem | you (pl.) have | atem (you, m.) |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָכֶן</say> | yesh lakhen | you (pl.) have | aten (you, f.) |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָהֶם</say> | yesh lahem | they have | hem |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָהֶן</say> | yesh lahen | they have | hen |

> **Watch out:** "to you" in Hebrew is **different** for m. and f. — lekha (m.) vs. lakh (f.). "To him" / "to her" too — lo (m., with holam) vs. la (f., with kamatz and a mappiq dot in ה). Gender slips in everywhere, as L4 warned.

> **Trap for the English ear:** **lo** (<say>לוֹ</say>) = "to him", but **lo** (<say>לֹא</say>) = "no, not". They're written with **different letters** (vav vs. alef), but sound identical. Distinguish **by context**: <say>יֵשׁ לוֹ סֵפֶר</say> yesh lo sefer ("he has a book") vs. <say>לֹא יוֹדֵעַ</say> lo yodea ("I don't know").

### Negating possession — ein le-

"I don't have…" — **ein li**. The same paradigm, just yesh is replaced by ein.

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>אֵין לִי</say> | ein li | I don't have |
| <say>אֵין לְךָ</say> | ein lekha | you don't have (m.) |
| <say>אֵין לָךְ</say> | ein lakh | you don't have (f.) |
| <say>אֵין לוֹ</say> | ein lo | he doesn't have |
| <say>אֵין לָהּ</say> | ein la | she doesn't have |
| <say>אֵין לָנוּ</say> | ein lanu | we don't have |
| <say>אֵין לָכֶם</say> | ein lakhem | you don't have (m.) |
| <say>אֵין לָכֶן</say> | ein lakhen | you don't have (f.) |
| <say>אֵין לָהֶם</say> | ein lahem | they don't have |
| <say>אֵין לָהֶן</say> | ein lahen | they don't have |

### Examples

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>יֵשׁ לִי סֵפֶר</say> | yesh li sefer | I have a book |
| <say>אֵין לִי זְמַן</say> | ein li zman | I don't have time |
| <say>יֵשׁ לְךָ שְׁאֵלָה?</say> | yesh lekha she'ela? | Do you have a question? (to m.) |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָנוּ שִׁעוּר</say> | yesh lanu shi'ur | We have a lesson |
| <say>אֵין לָהּ כֶּסֶף</say> | ein la kesef | She doesn't have money |
| <say>יֵשׁ לָהֶם יְלָדִים</say> | yesh lahem yeladim | They have children |
| <say>יֵשׁ לוֹ חָבֵר טוֹב</say> | yesh lo chaver tov | He has a good friend |

> **No cases here.** The possessed object (sefer, zman, kesef…) appears in the same form as in the dictionary. No "genitive" / "accusative" — Hebrew is not Latin or Russian, nouns don't decline.

> **The article on the possessed object** — by meaning. *<say>יֵשׁ לִי סֵפֶר</say> Yesh li sefer* — "I have a book" (any book). *<say>יֵשׁ לִי הַסֵּפֶר</say> Yesh li ha-sefer* — "I have the (specific) book". In L11 you'll learn that for a **definite** direct object you need the particle **<say>אֵת</say>** (et), but **yesh li / ein li are not a direct object**, they're an existential — so et is not used here (more on this in a footnote in L11).

## Part 3: Question words — a closed list of seven

Good news: Hebrew has few question words, and they're a **closed list**. Learn them in one lesson and they pay you back for 50.

| Hebrew | Translit | English | When |
|--------|----------|---------|------|
| <say>מִי</say> | mi | who | about people |
| <say>מָה</say> | ma | what | about things, events |
| <say>אֵיפֹה</say> | eifo | where | about location |
| <say>מָתַי</say> | matai | when | about time |
| <say>לָמָּה</say> | lama | why, what for | about cause |
| <say>אֵיךְ</say> | eikh | how | about manner, state |
| <say>כַּמָּה</say> | kama | how much / how many | about quantity |

### The extended set — for recognition

These four more you need to **recognize**; we'll actively learn them as they come up:

| Hebrew | Translit | English | Note |
|--------|----------|---------|------|
| <say>מֵאַיִן</say> / <say>מֵאֵיפֹה</say> | me-ayin / me-eifo | from where | literary vs. colloquial |
| <say>לְאָן</say> | le'an | to where, whither | direction |
| <say>אֵיזֶה</say> / <say>אֵיזוֹ</say> | eize (m.) / eizo (f.) | which | agrees in gender |
| <say>הַאִם</say> | ha-im | whether (yes-no question particle) | formal; usually dropped in speech |

### How a question is built

> **The main point:** the question word goes **at the start** of the sentence, just like in English.

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>מִי זֶה?</say> | mi ze? | Who is this? |
| <say>מָה זֶה?</say> | ma ze? | What is this? |
| <say>אֵיפֹה הַסֵּפֶר?</say> | eifo ha-sefer? | Where's the book? |
| <say>מָתַי הַשִּׁעוּר?</say> | matai ha-shi'ur? | When's the lesson? |
| <say>לָמָּה לֹא?</say> | lama lo? | Why not? |
| <say>אֵיךְ קוֹרְאִים לְךָ?</say> | eikh kor'im lekha? | What's your name? (m.) |
| <say>כַּמָּה זֶה עוֹלֶה?</say> | kama ze ole? | How much does it cost? |
| <say>כַּמָּה סְפָרִים יֵשׁ לְךָ?</say> | kama sfarim yesh lekha? | How many books do you have? |

### Yes/no questions — just intonation

If it's a yes/no question, Hebrew does **not** use an auxiliary verb (no do/does as in English). You simply **raise the intonation** at the end:

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>יֵשׁ לְךָ סֵפֶר?</say> | yesh lekha sefer? | Do you have a book? |
| <say>אַתָּה תַּלְמִיד?</say> | ata talmid? | Are you a student? |
| <say>הִיא מוֹרָה?</say> | hi mora? | Is she a teacher? |

> **Simpler than English:** no "do/does/are/is" gymnastics. The word order doesn't change, only the intonation rises.

> **Formal ha-im:** in the news or in official speech you may see **<say>הַאִם</say>** (ha-im) — "whether": *<say>הַאִם יֵשׁ לְךָ סֵפֶר?</say> Ha-im yesh lekha sefer?* Almost never used in conversation.

## Part 4: Negation — the universal לֹא (lo)

Hebrew negates **very simply**: place **<say>לֹא</say>** (lo) before whatever you're negating. It works almost everywhere.

| Hebrew | Translit | English | What's negated |
|--------|----------|---------|----------------|
| <say>לֹא</say> | lo | no (as an answer) | — |
| <say>אֲנִי לֹא תַּלְמִיד</say> | ani lo talmid | I'm not a student | predicate noun |
| <say>הִיא לֹא יוֹדַעַת</say> | hi lo yoda'at | She doesn't know | verb |
| <say>זֶה לֹא טוֹב</say> | ze lo tov | That's not good | adjective |
| <say>הוּא לֹא כָּאן</say> | hu lo kan | He's not here | adverb of place |
| <say>אֲנִי לֹא רוֹצֶה</say> | ani lo rotse | I don't want | verb |

> **Simple rule:** lo goes **before** the thing being negated (verb, adjective, predicate noun). Just like English "not".

> **Notice:** lo is both "no" as an answer and "not" as a negation particle. The same as English's two "no/not", except Hebrew uses one word — lo — for both.

### A special case: ein as negation of the present tense

In literary (and occasionally colloquial) Hebrew there's a second negation — **ein** before a present-tense participle:

| Hebrew | Translit | English | Register |
|--------|----------|---------|----------|
| <say>אֲנִי לֹא יוֹדֵעַ</say> | ani lo yodea | I don't know | colloquial, regular |
| <say>אֵינֶנִּי יוֹדֵעַ</say> | eineni yodea | I don't know | literary |

> **For now, stick with lo.** All 50 lessons run on the colloquial standard; eineni / einkha / eino is the **literary** form you'll meet in texts but rarely use in speech.

> **lo vs. ein li:** lo negates an action or a property. ein li negates possession. Don't mix them: "I don't have" = **<say>אֵין לִי</say> ein li**, not "lo yesh li" (that's not Hebrew).

## Part 5: Connectors — ve- (and), aval (but), o (or), ki (because)

To stitch sentences and words together, four connectors will get you through.

| Hebrew | Translit | English | Note |
|--------|----------|---------|------|
| <say>וְ-</say> | ve- (vu-, va-, u-) | and | **prefix**, written together with the next word |
| <say>אֲבָל</say> | aval | but, however | a separate word |
| <say>אוֹ</say> | o | or | a separate word |
| <say>כִּי</say> | ki | because, since | introduces a reason |

### ve- is a prefix, not a separate word

The biggest trick: **"and"** in Hebrew is **one letter ו** glued to the next word. The pronunciation shifts depending on what follows:

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>תַּלְמִיד וּמוֹרָה</say> | talmid u-mora | A student and a (female) teacher |
| <say>סֵפֶר וְדַף</say> | sefer ve-daf | A book and a sheet |
| <say>יֵשׁ לִי סֵפֶר וְעֵט</say> | yesh li sefer ve-et | I have a book and a pen |

> **Pronunciation nuance:** before labials (ב, מ, פ) and before a shewa — usually **"u-"** (or "v-"). Elsewhere — **"ve-"**. Details in L11–L15. **For now, write ve- and you won't be wrong in meaning.**

### aval — "but"

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>יֵשׁ לִי סֵפֶר, אֲבָל אֵין לִי זְמַן</say> | yesh li sefer, aval ein li zman | I have a book, but no time |
| <say>הוּא תַּלְמִיד, אֲבָל הִיא מוֹרָה</say> | hu talmid, aval hi mora | He's a student, but she's a teacher |

### o — "or"

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>תֵּה אוֹ קָפֶה?</say> | te o kafe? | Tea or coffee? |
| <say>הוּא אוֹ הִיא?</say> | hu o hi? | He or she? |

### ki — "because"

| Hebrew | Translit | English |
|--------|----------|---------|
| <say>אֲנִי לֹא בָּא, כִּי אֵין לִי זְמַן</say> | ani lo ba, ki ein li zman | I'm not coming because I don't have time |
| <say>הוּא שָׂמֵחַ, כִּי יֵשׁ לוֹ חָבֵר</say> | hu sameach, ki yesh lo chaver | He's happy because he has a friend |

> **ki vs. lama:** **lama** is the **question** "why?". **ki** is the **answer** "because". Don't mix them: <say>לָמָּה יֵשׁ לִי סֵפֶר?</say> lama yesh li sefer? — "Why do I have a book?"; <say>כִּי אַתָּה נָתַתָּ לִי</say> ki ata natata li — "Because you gave it to me". In L31 we'll meet ki as "that" (a complement-clause conjunction) — but that's later.

## Part 6: Putting it together — existential dialogues

Now you have everything for a normal everyday dialogue. Let's combine.

### Mini-dialogue: meeting in a café

> — <say>שָׁלוֹם! מָה שְׁלוֹמְךָ?</say>
> — <say>טוֹב, תּוֹדָה. וְאַתְּ?</say>
> — <say>גַּם כֵּן טוֹב. יֵשׁ לָךְ זְמַן לְקָפֶה?</say>
> — <say>כֵּן, יֵשׁ לִי זְמַן. אֲבָל אֵין לִי כֶּסֶף הַיּוֹם.</say>
> — <say>אֵין בְּעָיָה, אֲנִי מַזְמִין. תֵּה אוֹ קָפֶה?</say>
> — <say>קָפֶה, בְּבַקָּשָׁה. תּוֹדָה רַבָּה!</say>

Translit:
> — Shalom! Ma shlomkha?
> — Tov, toda. Ve-at?
> — Gam ken tov. Yesh lakh zman le-kafe?
> — Ken, yesh li zman. Aval ein li kesef ha-yom.
> — Ein be'aya, ani mazmin. Te o kafe?
> — Kafe, bevakasha. Toda raba!

Translation:
> — Hi! How are you (to m.)?
> — Good, thanks. And you (to f.)?
> — Also good. Do you have time for a coffee?
> — Yes, I have time. But I don't have money today.
> — No problem, I'm treating. Tea or coffee?
> — Coffee, please. Thank you very much!

> **Notice:** **shlomkha** (to m.) → **at** (to f., you-f.) → **lakh** (to you-f.) — the addressee's gender shifts through the dialogue. That's **natural** in Hebrew; you'll have to get used to switching too.

## Part 8: Exercises

### Exercise 1. Drill the yesh le- paradigm

Say aloud ten times, picking up speed:

> yesh li — yesh lekha — yesh lakh — yesh lo — yesh la — yesh lanu — yesh lakhem — yesh lakhen — yesh lahem — yesh lahen

And immediately — the **negative paradigm**:

> ein li — ein lekha — ein lakh — ein lo — ein la — ein lanu — ein lakhem — ein lakhen — ein lahem — ein lahen

Target: 10 forms in 8 seconds, no stumbles. This **must** become a reflex.

### Exercise 2. Translate from English to Hebrew (yesh / ein)

1. I have a book.
2. He doesn't have time.
3. She has a dog.
4. We have a problem.
5. You (m. group) don't have questions.
6. They (f.) have a cat.
7. There's food on the table.
8. There are no children in the house.

6. *יֵשׁ לָהֶן חָתוּל.* — **Yesh lahen chatul.**
7. *יֵשׁ אֹכֶל עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן.* — **Yesh okhel al ha-shulchan.** (impersonal: yesh **without** le-)
8. *אֵין יְלָדִים בַּבַּיִת.* — **Ein yeladim ba-bayit.** (impersonal)

> **Notice the difference** in 7–8: this is **existential** "there is/isn't" (presence in a place), not **possession** (someone **has**). The structure is different: **yesh / ein + what + where**, no le-.

</details>

### Exercise 3. Question words — pick the right one

Insert the appropriate question word (mi, ma, eifo, matai, lama, eikh, kama):

1. ___ זֶה? — That's a sefer.
2. ___ זֶה? — That's Yossi.
3. ___ הַסֵּפֶר? — On the table.
4. ___ הַשִּׁעוּר? — Tomorrow.
5. ___ אַתָּה לֹא בָּא? — Because I don't have time.
6. ___ קוֹרְאִים לָךְ? — My name is Dana.
7. ___ סְפָרִים יֵשׁ לְךָ? — Five.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *מָה זֶה?* — **Ma ze?** ("what is this?" — about a thing)
2. *מִי זֶה?* — **Mi ze?** ("who is this?" — about a person)
3. *אֵיפֹה הַסֵּפֶר?* — **Eifo ha-sefer?** ("where?")
4. *מָתַי הַשִּׁעוּר?* — **Matai ha-shi'ur?** ("when?")
5. *לָמָּה אַתָּה לֹא בָּא?* — **Lama ata lo ba?** ("why?")
6. *אֵיךְ קוֹרְאִים לָךְ?* — **Eikh kor'im lakh?** ("how do they call to you")
7. *כַּמָּה סְפָרִים יֵשׁ לְךָ?* — **Kama sfarim yesh lekha?** ("how many?")

> **Hack:** all seven question words **start with a vowel or with m/l/k** — mi, ma, eifo, matai, lama, eikh, kama. Handy — they jump out by their "voiced" beginning.

</details>

### Exercise 4. Negation with lo

Translate into Hebrew:

1. I'm not a teacher.
2. She doesn't know (yoda'at).
3. That's not good.
4. They're not here.
5. I don't want (rotse) coffee, but tea.
6. I don't have questions, but I have a problem.
7. Why aren't you coming (ba)? — Because I don't have time.

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *אֲנִי לֹא מוֹרֶה.* — **Ani lo more.** (m.; to f. it would be *ani lo mora*)
2. *הִיא לֹא יוֹדַעַת.* — **Hi lo yoda'at.**
3. *זֶה לֹא טוֹב.* — **Ze lo tov.**
4. *הֵם לֹא כָּאן.* — **Hem lo kan.** (or *po*)
5. *אֲנִי לֹא רוֹצֶה קָפֶה, אֲבָל תֵּה.* — **Ani lo rotse kafe, aval te.**
6. *אֵין לִי שְׁאֵלוֹת, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לִי בְּעָיָה.* — **Ein li she'elot, aval yesh li be'aya.**
7. *לָמָּה אַתָּה לֹא בָּא? — כִּי אֵין לִי זְמַן.* — **Lama ata lo ba? — Ki ein li zman.**

> **Notice:** **lo** negates an action/property (lo more, lo yoda'at, lo tov, lo kan, lo rotse, lo ba). **ein li** negates possession. These are **different** constructions — don't mix.

</details>

### Exercise 5. Connect sentences — ve-, aval, o, ki

Connect using the appropriate connector:

1. I'm not coming. There's no time. *(because)*
2. I have a book. I have a pen. *(and)*
3. Tea. Coffee. *(or)*
4. He has money. He doesn't have time. *(but)*
5. She's happy (sme'echa). She has a friend. *(because)*
6. He's a student. She's a teacher. *(but)*

<details>
<summary>Key</summary>

1. *אֲנִי לֹא בָּא, כִּי אֵין לִי זְמַן.* — **Ani lo ba, ki ein li zman.**
2. *יֵשׁ לִי סֵפֶר וְעֵט.* — **Yesh li sefer ve-et.** (two items fused — Hebrew loves to glue them with ve-)
3. *תֵּה אוֹ קָפֶה?* — **Te o kafe?**
4. *יֵשׁ לוֹ כֶּסֶף, אֲבָל אֵין לוֹ זְמַן.* — **Yesh lo kesef, aval ein lo zman.**
5. *הִיא שְׂמֵחָה, כִּי יֵשׁ לָהּ חָבֵר.* — **Hi sme'echa, ki yesh la chaver.**
6. *הוּא תַּלְמִיד, אֲבָל הִיא מוֹרָה.* — **Hu talmid, aval hi mora.**

</details>

### Exercise 6. The "question — answer" matrix

Run the dialogue aloud 3 times, then from memory. First from role A, then from role B.

> A: שָׁלוֹם! מָה שִׁמְךָ?
> B: שָׁלוֹם, קוֹרְאִים לִי דָּן. וְלָךְ?
> A: קוֹרְאִים לִי רוּת. נָעִים מְאוֹד.
> B: גַּם כֵּן. אֵיפֹה אַתְּ גָּרָה?
> A: אֲנִי גָּרָה בְּתֵל אָבִיב. וְאַתָּה?
> B: אֲנִי גָּר בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. יֵשׁ לָךְ זְמַן הַיּוֹם?
> A: לֹא, הַיּוֹם אֵין לִי זְמַן, כִּי יֵשׁ לִי שִׁעוּר. אֲבָל מָחָר יֵשׁ לִי זְמַן.
> B: מְצֻיָּן! עַד מָחָר.

Translit for checking:
> A: Shalom! Ma shimkha?
> B: Shalom, kor'im li Dan. U-lakh?
> A: Kor'im li Rut. Na'im me'od.
> B: Gam ken. Eifo at gara?
> A: Ani gara be-Tel Aviv. Ve-ata?
> B: Ani gar bi-Yerushalayim. Yesh lakh zman ha-yom?
> A: Lo, ha-yom ein li zman, ki yesh li shi'ur. Aval machar yesh li zman.
> B: Metsuyan! Ad machar.

Translation:
> A: Hi! What's your name (to m.)?
> B: Hi, my name is Dan. And yours (to f.)?
> A: My name is Rut. Very nice to meet you.
> B: Likewise. Where do you live (to f.)?
> A: I live in Tel Aviv. And you (to m.)?
> B: I live in Jerusalem. Do you have time today?
> A: No, today I don't have time because I have a class. But tomorrow I do.
> B: Excellent! See you tomorrow.

> **Track** all four connectors in the dialogue: **u-** (= ve-) in *u-lakh*, **aval** in *aval machar*, **ki** in *ki yesh li shi'ur*. Plus the whole set of yesh li / ein li / yesh lakh — in one living conversation.

## Lesson cheat sheet

```
EXISTENCE (existential "there is / there isn't"):
  יֵשׁ   yesh   there is, exists
  אֵין   ein    there isn't, doesn't exist
  → like English "there's a table" / "there's no table"

POSSESSION (Hebrew has NO verb "to have"!):
  yesh / ein + le- + (whom) + what

  yesh li      I have           ein li      I don't have
  yesh lekha   you (m.) have    ein lekha   you (m.) don't have
  yesh lakh    you (f.) have    ein lakh    you (f.) don't have
  yesh lo      he has           ein lo      he doesn't have
  yesh la      she has          ein la      she doesn't have
  yesh lanu    we have          ein lanu    we don't have
  yesh lakhem  you (m.pl.) have ein lakhem  you (m.pl.) don't have
  yesh lakhen  you (f.pl.) have ein lakhen  you (f.pl.) don't have
  yesh lahem   they (m.) have   ein lahem   they (m.) don't have
  yesh lahen   they (f.) have   ein lahen   they (f.) don't have

  Example: yesh li sefer = "there is to me a book" → "I have a book"

QUESTION WORDS (closed list — learn whole):
  מִי       mi      who
  מָה       ma      what
  אֵיפֹה    eifo    where
  מָתַי     matai   when
  לָמָּה    lama    why
  אֵיךְ     eikh    how
  כַּמָּה    kama    how much / how many

  + me-eifo / le'an / eize-eizo / ha-im — recognize

NEGATION:
  לֹא  lo  "not/no" — universal (goes BEFORE the negated word)
       ani lo talmid     I'm not a student
       hi lo yoda'at     she doesn't know
       ze lo tov         that's not good
       hu lo kan         he's not here

  ein li / ein lekha…  — negation of POSSESSION (not lo!)
  DON'T say "lo yesh li" — that's a calque; Hebrew uses ein li.

  Trap: lo (לוֹ) "to him" vs. lo (לֹא) "no/not".
  Different letters (vav vs. alef), same sound. Distinguish by context.

CONNECTORS:
  וְ-     ve-    "and"  (prefix, glued to the next word)
                  before labials and shewa — u-:  talmid u-mora
  אֲבָל   aval   "but"   yesh li sefer, aval ein li zman
  אוֹ    o      "or"    te o kafe?
  כִּי    ki     "because" (answers lama)
                  lama lo? — ki ein li zman.

YES/NO QUESTION — just intonation (no do/does, no ha-im in speech):
  yesh lekha sefer?   do you have a book?
  ata talmid?         are you a student?
  hi mora?            is she a teacher?

KEY INTUITION:
  Hebrew handles "there is / isn't" (existence) and "have / don't have" (possession)
  with the SAME pair of words — yesh / ein.
  There is no verb "to have" at all in Hebrew.
  "I have X" = "there is to me X" (yesh li X).
```
