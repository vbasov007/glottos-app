# Hebrew Course Curriculum (50 units)
**Glottos Matrix — structural language-learning course for English speakers**

> **Target level: B2, not C1.** A deliberate choice. Modern Hebrew gives a European learner no cognate support and two heavy lifts at the same time: "root + pattern" morphology (binyanim and mishkalim) and reading without vowel points. Fifty lessons is an honest path to a confident B2; everything beyond is named in lesson 50.

> Principle: 5% understanding the rule, 95% training your mouth. Every topic gets drilled through the matrix: ani → ata/at → hu/hi → anachnu → atem/aten → hem/hen.

## BLOCK 1: A1 FOUNDATIONS — SCRIPT, ROOT, BINYAN MAP (Lessons 1–10) → Test "Talmid / Talmida / תלמיד" (Apprentice)

*Goal: learn to read pointed Hebrew, grasp the root concept and the seven-binyan scaffold, and build simple sentences in the first binyan.*

| # | Grammar | Vocabulary |
|---|---------|------------|
| 1 | Read Hebrew letters right-to-left, count from 1 to 10 | Greetings and politeness: shalom, toda, ken, lo, slicha, lehitra'ot. Classroom words: sefer, daf |
| 2 | Read vowel points (nikkud) below the letters | Everyday nouns: house, food, animals. Numbers 10–20 |
| 3 | Read pointed Hebrew fluently; recognize handwriting | Places, everyday objects, expanded daily vocabulary |
| 4 | Tell masculine from feminine; form plurals -im / -ot | People, family, professions — deliberately mixed gender |
| 5 | Personal pronouns and verbless sentences: "I (am) a teacher" | Nationalities, countries, identity-description vocabulary |
| 6 | Spot the three-letter root in every word — the atom of Hebrew | Cognate families (e.g. k-t-v: write, letter, address, written) |
| 7 | Meet the seven-binyan map — Hebrew's verb-pattern system | One root drilled through every binyan; terminology |
| 8 | Present tense in Pa'al: "I write, she writes" — gender-marked | Pa'al verbs: daily routine, study, work, movement |
| 9 | Definite article ה- and prepositions ב-/ל-/מ-; adjective agreement | Adjectives: color, size, quality, character; spatial vocabulary |
| 10 | "I have / I don't have" (yesh / ein), questions and negation | Question words as a set; connectors ve-, aval, o, ki |

**→ TEST "TALMID / TALMIDA" (Apprentice): you can read pointed text, introduce yourself and ask for directions, level A1**

## How to work with each unit

1. **Theory** — watch the video / read the rule (5 minutes)
2. **Root and pattern** — break every new word into its root and mishkal/binyan; that's how a native speaker stores it
3. **Translation** — translate phrases from English to Hebrew, check against the key
4. **Language scales** — drill the new rule through every person: ani → ata → at → hu → hi → anachnu → atem → aten → hem → hen (Hebrew splits "you-plural" and "they" by gender — that's ten forms, not six)
5. **Matrix** — drill Q&A in three scenarios: you (m.) → you (f.) → they
6. **Mirror principle** — the answer is already embedded in the question; don't build from scratch

## Memory hacks specific to Hebrew

- **The root is the atom.** For any new word, first look for the three-consonant root. Hebrew stores its lexicon as families, not as a list: כ-ת-ב → write, letter, address, written, correspondent. Memorizing a family is easier than memorizing five random words.
- **The binyan is the engine.** By lesson 7 you'll have a seven-pattern verb map. From there, every new verb gets a shelf: "this is Pi'el (intensive)", "this is Hif'il (causative)". Without the map it's chaos; with it, every new verb has a place to land.
- **Nikkud is scaffolding.** Vowel points are training wheels — real Hebrew doesn't use them. By lesson 22 you'll drop them and pull the vowels out of unpointed text using the root and the pattern. That's what lessons 6 onward have been teaching you.
- **Gender is everywhere.** Not just on nouns and adjectives like in Spanish or French, but also on **verbs** (he writes vs. she writes — different forms) and **numbers** (masculine vs. feminine forms). Miss the gender once and the whole sentence comes apart. Learn each noun together with its gender from day one.
- **Preposition + pronoun = one word.** "To me" is li, "to you" is lekha. Not li + ani — a single inflected form. By lesson 15 this becomes reflex.
- **Smikhut > of.** Hebrew prefers "the-teacher's-house" (one phonetic unit) over "the house of the teacher". That's L20, but get used to it early: bet-sefer (literally "house-of-book") = school.
- **No cognates.** Unlike French or Spanish, Hebrew gives the English speaker almost no "anchor" words. Spaced repetition isn't optional here — it's the only thing that keeps the vocabulary from sliding back out of your head.
